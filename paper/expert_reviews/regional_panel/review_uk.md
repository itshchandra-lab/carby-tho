# Peer Review — UK Specialist
## "The Carbon-Thorium Standard: Energy-Backed Currencies and the Architecture of Monetary Failure in a Multipolar World"

**Reviewer specialisation:** UK monetary history, sterling's decline, City of London, North Sea energy policy, Brexit, UK-US special relationship, Britain's post-imperial monetary legacy
**Recommendation:** Major Revisions
**Score:** 6/10

---

## Summary Judgement

This is an ambitious and intellectually serious paper. The core intuition — that energy governance is politically constructed and that reserve currency trajectories track it with a generational lag — is genuinely productive. The falsification registry is a methodological virtue.

My concerns are substantial. The sterling narrative — which the paper deploys as its founding historical case — is constructed in a way that is partly selective and partly under-argued. The paper's treatment of the United Kingdom is simultaneously its most important historical evidence and its most persistent empirical embarrassment. The lag sensitivity findings for the UK (never significant at any specification) are treated as a minor complication; I believe they are a more serious problem than the paper admits. Several of the issues I raise below require the authors to either revise claims materially or substantially strengthen the defence of existing ones.

---

## 1. The Sterling Case as Founding Narrative: Historiographically Incomplete

The paper opens with the sterling-dollar transition as the paradigmatic instance of the energy-monetary mechanism: Gulf producers held sterling because energy revenues arrived in sterling; when oil invoicing shifted to dollars after 1974, sterling's reserve share collapsed. The Schenk (2010) citation on oil royalty payments in sterling is accurate. But this account omits the full causal architecture with which Schenk herself works.

**WWII debt accumulation.** Britain emerged from WWII as the world's largest debtor, with blocked sterling balances exceeding £3.5 billion owed to colonial and Commonwealth creditors. The sterling area arrangements of the 1940s–1950s were fundamentally debt management instruments, not energy instruments. The sterling balances that Gulf producers held were in many cases inherited from wartime procurement, not generated by oil royalty payments alone.

**Marshall Plan conditionality.** The Anglo-American Financial Agreement of 1946 required sterling convertibility experiments that repeatedly destabilised UK reserves. The 1947 convertibility crisis — when the UK lost $1 billion in reserves within a month — locked the sterling area into inconvertible form for another decade. This is a governance failure, not an energy failure.

**Suez 1956.** The paper does not mention Suez. This is a serious omission. The run on sterling during the Suez crisis — in which the Eisenhower administration withheld IMF support unless Britain accepted a ceasefire, directly inducing the reserve drain that forced Macmillan's hand — established that sterling's international role was contingent on US political permission. This is precisely the Norrlof security-monetary conditioning the paper invokes, but applied to the UK in a direction that *complicates* rather than supports the energy narrative. The dollar did not displace sterling through energy economics in 1956; it displaced it through security coercion.

**1967 devaluation and IMF conditionality.** The November 1967 devaluation was forced by chronic balance-of-payments deficits caused primarily by military overstretch east of Suez, welfare state expansion, and the exchange rate commitment successive chancellors refused to abandon. Energy invoicing is a secondary variable in this causal sequence.

**The honest version** of the sterling narrative: the energy-invoicing mechanism was *one contributing factor* in sterling's reserve decline — a real factor, documented by Schenk, operating through the specific channel the paper describes — but it operated against a background of structural sovereign overextension, security dependency, and repeated governance failures that the paper's framework does not capture. The authors should either acknowledge this complexity and explain why the energy mechanism is the *structural* driver underlying the others, or downgrade the sterling case from "founding narrative" to "partial supporting evidence" and carry the historical weight on the US and Japan cases.

**The causal arrow problem.** The paper notes that Gulf oil royalty arrangements sustained sterling demand into the postwar period — sterling's life was being *extended* beyond what the coal-based industrial story would predict. This actually suggests the energy mechanism was prolonging sterling's role, not explaining its eventual decline. The authors should clarify which direction the causal arrow runs for which period.

## 2. Sterling's Resilience Paradox

The TMPI table assigns the UK a score of zero. NEP is negative. The paper's own §3.1 uses the UK as the paradigmatic case of "significant current monetary power with negligible transition potential," and Figure 5 labels UK MPI as "declining."

If the framework is correct, GBP should by now be approaching the reserve share of a small commodity currency. Instead, GBP holds approximately 4.5% of global reserves as of 2024 — the world's fourth reserve currency. This is not a niche position. It is a substantial one, maintained with TMPI = 0 and declining NEP.

**The City of London is the obvious explanation and the paper never names it.** The paper theorises Switzerland as a boundary condition — "institutional substitution case: financial entrepôt status and monetary credibility built over two centuries." The same logic applies to the United Kingdom with far greater force. London accounts for approximately 38% of global FX daily turnover (BIS 2022 Triennial) — by far the world's largest foreign exchange trading centre. London is the dominant centre for international bond issuance, global insurance, derivatives, and the world's deepest eurodollar markets.

**If Switzerland at <1% of global FX turnover is an institutional substitution case, the City of London at 38% of global FX turnover requires at least a paragraph's worth of theoretical engagement.** The failure to theorise the UK as an institutional substitution case analogous to Switzerland but of much greater monetary significance is a genuine gap in the framework's scope conditions.

The relevant literature the paper misses: Kynaston's *The City of London* (four volumes, 1994–2001) documents the institutional architecture through which London maintained international financial primacy long after Britain ceased to be an economic hegemon. Strange's own *Casino Capitalism* (1986) and *Mad Money* (1998) were preoccupied with precisely this puzzle — how did London sustain its role in an era of British relative decline? Strange's answer was not energy: it was eurodollar markets, regulatory arbitrage, legal infrastructure, and accumulated correspondent banking networks. The paper cites Strange (1988) but not her UK-specific work.

## 3. North Sea and Lag Sensitivity: The Self-Undermining Case

Table §6.6 shows the United Kingdom displaying no statistically significant lag relationship between NEP and reserve share at any specification — λ = 5, 8, 10, 12, or 15 years. The paper treats this as consistent with "boundary condition logic." This framing is too comfortable.

The UK is not a boundary condition in the way Switzerland is — it is the paper's *founding historical narrative*. The paper opens in §1 with sterling as the paradigmatic case. §3.1 uses the UK as the example of "high current monetary power with negligible transition potential." The coal-oil transition is the paper's key historical analogy. If the time-series analysis of the UK case shows no significant relationship at any lag, the paper is in a structurally awkward position: its primary illustrative case is not confirmed by its own quantitative method.

**A critical methodological clarification the paper must make:** The quantitative test uses COFER data beginning in 1995. Sterling was already near its floor when COFER begins (3.5–5.0%, where it remains today). The narrative claims the 1950–1980 mechanism; the quantitative test covers 1995–2024. These are different periods testing different variation. The paper should be explicit: the historical claim (sterling decline 1950–1980) is supported by Schenk and Eichengreen/Flandreau, *not* by the NEP regression. The regression non-significance for the UK in the post-1995 period is not a falsification of the historical claim — but it is also not evidence for it. These two evidentiary bases must be distinguished.

## 4. Brexit as a Natural Experiment in Energy Governance Restructuring

The paper does not mention Brexit anywhere. This is a significant omission for a paper about energy governance, carbon markets, and institutional design as monetary determinants.

**UK ETS vs EU ETS comparison:** The United Kingdom left the EU ETS in January 2021 and launched the UK ETS in May 2021. The UK ETS and EU ETS have since diverged in price: UK prices traded at a premium through 2022–2023 before converging in 2024 under different supply-demand conditions. **This two-system comparison is precisely what the paper's backward register methodology calls for.** Two governance regimes governing the same underlying physical reality (European carbon emissions), differing by political architecture, whose price paths can be compared as a GS test. If the UK ETS exhibits lower political volatility, the framework would predict higher UK governance quality and sterling credibility relative to euro. If higher volatility, further sterling erosion. Either prediction is interesting and testable — and the paper ignores both.

Beyond carbon markets, Brexit removed the UK from the EU's internal energy market. Whether this constitutes a GS improvement (increased autonomy over domestic energy price) or deterioration (reduced integration buffers) depends on empirical price path comparison the paper does not provide.

## 5. Nuclear Ambitions and Channel 3

UK TMPI = 0 because thorium reserves are negligible. But Channel 3 (credibility and store-of-value) operates through energy import dependence, not just thorium endowment. Hinkley Point C (in construction, costs now exceeding £35 billion) and Sizewell B (proposed), along with Rolls-Royce SMR development, will improve UK NEP modestly if they come online as planned. The paper's framework, through Channel 3, would predict some monetary credibility improvement even absent thorium endowment.

More importantly, **Rolls-Royce SMRs represent a UK knowledge-structure investment** of the kind the paper identifies in §2.2 as crucial for the thorium era through Strange's framework. The UK is not building thorium capacity, but it is building reactor design and export infrastructure. If SMR technology is transferable to thorium fuel cycles (which liquid-metal-cooled SMR designs potentially are), UK institutional and knowledge position in the thorium era may be non-zero despite zero reserves.

**The AUKUS knowledge-structure dimension.** The UK is an AUKUS partner transferring submarine nuclear propulsion technology to Australia. The UK's Rolls-Royce PWR3 reactor design for the SSN-AUKUS submarines is a potential vector for nuclear technology globalisation. The paper should at minimum note that AUKUS creates a UK knowledge-structure vector into the TMPI rankings of both Australia and the United States, even if it does not alter UK's own TMPI score.

## 6. "Global Britain" and Monetary Architecture

The paper engages with multipolarity theory but does not consider the UK's specific position within the architecture of de-dollarisation.

Since Brexit, the UK has positioned "Global Britain" as a bridge: maintaining deep US financial and security ties while developing independent relations with Gulf states, India, and the Indo-Pacific. In monetary terms: (a) the London-based RMB clearing hub (HSBC and Standard Chartered facilitating yuan settlement through London); (b) the UK-India FTA negotiation (implications for GBP-INR settlement); (c) the UK-GCC FTA (signed 2024, including arrangements affecting energy pricing and Gulf SWF flows into London markets).

**London's role as an RMB clearing hub** creates a peculiar dynamic where UK institutional capacity facilitates a challenger currency's reserve expansion. UK GCC settlement arrangements potentially recreate (in modest form) the sterling-Gulf relationship Schenk documents — Gulf SWFs investing through London, denominated in GBP or USD but routed through London infrastructure. The paper's framework has no language for a state whose institutional capacity is a platform for *other* currencies' reserve expansion rather than its own. This is a genuine theoretical gap the UK case exposes.

## 7. Missing Literature

- **Kynaston, David.** *The City of London* (4 vols., 1994–2001). — Essential for understanding how London maintained international financial primacy independently of British economic primacy.
- **Strange, Susan.** *Casino Capitalism* (1986) and *Mad Money* (1998). — Strange's UK-specific writings directly address London's financial primacy in an era of US hegemony. Their absence, given how prominently Strange (1988) is invoked, is surprising.
- **Burnham, Peter.** *The Political Economy of Postwar Reconstruction* (1990). — The classic account of sterling management constraints as domestic political economy.
- **Gamble, Andrew.** *Britain in Decline* (1985; 5th ed. 2014). — The canonical UK relative decline framework. The paper invokes UK decline without engaging this literature.
- **Helleiner, Eric, and Jonathan Kirshner, eds.** *The Future of the Dollar* (2009). — Chapter-length treatments of sterling as a comparator case directly relevant to the paper's comparative reserve currency analysis.

## 8. Specific Technical Corrections

1. The citation "Eichengreen and Flandreau (2009)" for the 1950–1980 sterling figures should be checked for precision — that paper focuses on the 1920s–1940s period. The 1950–1980 figures derive primarily from Schenk (2010) and Eichengreen *Exorbitant Privilege* (2011).

2. The paper assigns UK GS a value in the MPI construction but does not explicitly state what that value is. UK ETS vs EU ETS comparison should produce a UK GS estimate for the post-2021 period.

3. Figure 5 labels UK MPI trajectory as "declining" but the paper never computes a specific MPI for the UK in the present or forward register. Either show the calculation or remove the label.

4. Sterling reserve share over the COFER window (1995–2024) has been *relatively stable* at 3.5–5.0%. The paper's narrative claims declining UK reserve share; the COFER data shows approximate stability. Distinguish the pre-1995 narrative from the post-1995 data more clearly.

---

## 9. Required Revisions

1. **Reframe §1's sterling historical account** to acknowledge multi-causal decline (WWII debt, Marshall Plan conditions, Suez, 1967 devaluation) while defending the energy-invoicing channel as a *contributing structural mechanism*, not the sole driver
2. **Add a City of London institutional substitution paragraph to §6.4**, explicitly analogising UK to Switzerland but at greater scale — and specifying what this implies for the framework's scope conditions
3. **Add a methodological clarification to §6.6** distinguishing the pre-1995 historical claim (supported by Schenk) from the post-1995 quantitative test (non-significant for UK and covering the wrong period)
4. **Add UK ETS / Brexit discussion** — run the UK vs EU ETS GS comparison as a natural experiment the backward register methodology calls for
5. **Supplement the bibliography** with Kynaston, Strange (1986/1998), Burnham, and Gamble at minimum
