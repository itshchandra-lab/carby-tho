# Peer Review — China Specialist
## "The Carbon-Thorium Standard: Energy-Backed Currencies and the Architecture of Monetary Failure in a Multipolar World"

**Reviewer specialisation:** China's foreign policy, renminbi internationalisation, Belt and Road Initiative, energy security, great power competition
**Journal:** Review of International Political Economy / China Quarterly
**Recommendation:** Major Revisions
**Score:** 5/10

---

## Summary Assessment

This paper makes a genuinely interesting theoretical contribution by formalising the link between energy governance and reserve currency status. The backward register is the paper's strongest empirical section. The Japan natural experiment is well-designed. The India forward prediction is bold, specific, and falsifiable.

However, the paper suffers a critical analytical failure at precisely the point where its framework should be most tested: China. China is simultaneously the world's largest energy importer, the most aggressive state-level de-dollarisation actor, the country with the most advanced operational thorium reactor programme, and the economy whose monetary challenge to dollar hegemony is the central preoccupation of contemporary IPE scholarship. The paper treats China as a marginal case, dispenses with it in four sentences (§6.4), assigns it TMPI = 4.2, and moves on. This is not a gap in the literature review. It is a structural hole in the argument.

---

## 1. Realism Lens: China's Active De-Dollarisation Strategy Is Absent

China's de-dollarisation strategy operates on at least five simultaneous tracks:
1. Yuan-denominated oil futures on the Shanghai International Energy Exchange (INE crude, launched 2018)
2. Cross-Border Interbank Payment System (CIPS) — over 1,400 participating institutions as of 2024, designed as yuan alternative to SWIFT
3. Digital yuan (e-CNY) — explicitly engineered for cross-border settlement outside dollar correspondent banking
4. PBOC bilateral swap lines covering ~40 countries, cumulative commitments exceeding RMB 4 trillion
5. Yuan-ruble energy settlement corridor — over 30% of Russia-China trade volume following 2022 sanctions

The paper's MPI produces China's monetary trajectory from its energy position. China's NEP ≈ 0 → MPI suppressed → path to reserve status runs through institutional quality and capital account opening. The paper presents this as a prediction consistent with observed reality. What it actually represents is the framework passing an easy test while ignoring the harder one.

**The harder test:** China is attempting to decouple Channel 1 (invoicing) from energy position through political construction — precisely the mechanism the backward register identifies. The 1974 petrodollar arrangement was a bilateral political transaction that converted an energy relationship into a monetary architecture. China is attempting the same construction through different bilateral relationships (Russia, Iran, Gulf states, BRICS partners) at a different historical moment. If China successfully establishes yuan-denominated oil invoicing through a network of bilateral energy arrangements that bypass NEP as the framework measures it, the framework's predictive architecture is not merely incomplete — it is systematically wrong about the most important case.

**Missing literature:** Cohen (2015) on currency internationalisation as a political project; Liao and McDowell (2015) on China's renminbi diplomacy; McNamara (2008) on the political foundations of monetary authority.

## 2. The China Nuclear Undercount: TMPI 4.2 vs India 15.8

**On nuclear capacity:** China had 57 operating nuclear reactors and 22 under construction as of 2024 — the largest active construction programme on Earth, targeting 100 GW by 2030 and 150 GW by 2035. The TMPI's nuclear share variable captures a stock at a point in time rather than a trajectory. A framework designed to capture forward-looking monetary potential should not score nuclear capacity on current share alone when one country is stagnant and another is adding 8–10 GW per year.

**On TMSR-LF1:** China's thorium molten salt reactor programme at the Shanghai Institute of Applied Physics is widely considered the most operationally advanced thorium MSR programme in existence. China's target is TMSR-LF1 grid connection by 2030. India's PFBR — the paper's key Stage 2 milestone — began core loading in 2024 after a 14-year delay. The paper scores India's binding constraint as "Nuclear capacity (programme early stage)" while scoring China's as "Reserves and partial capacity." This is difficult to defend when China is closer to operational thorium deployment than India on the paper's own logic.

**On thorium reserve underreporting:** Chinese geological survey data on thorium reserves is notoriously under-reported in Western databases because the resource is co-located with rare earth deposits that China regards as strategically sensitive. The 2.1% figure almost certainly understates China's actual thorium resource base.

**Required:** Either (a) defend the TMPI scoring methodology explicitly against this critique, showing why trajectory adjustments would not change India's relative advantage; or (b) introduce a trajectory-weighted version of the nuclear share variable.

## 3. WGI as Institutional Quality: The Wrong Proxy for China's Monetary Ambitions

The paper uses WGI composite as institutional quality proxy. China scores 0.56; India scores 0.52. For the purpose of predicting monetary ambition and capacity, WGI is the wrong instrument.

WGI Voice and Accountability penalises China heavily for its political system. This dimension measures democratic participation, not monetary credibility. Japan scores highly on Voice and Accountability and holds 5–6% of global reserves. China scores poorly and is actively building reserve currency infrastructure. These outcomes suggest WGI is capturing something orthogonal to the mechanism the paper measures.

For China's monetary project, the relevant institutional dimensions are: CIPS penetration depth; PBOC swap line coverage; offshore yuan (CNH) market depth; Bond Connect accessibility; digital yuan interoperability. None appear in WGI. A state can score poorly on WGI while simultaneously building the most advanced cross-border payment infrastructure in the world. China is that state.

The paper correctly states in §3.4 that Channel 1 is "structurally unavailable to states with closed capital accounts." But China is attempting to route around this constraint through CIPS + e-CNY + bilateral swap arrangements — creating a quasi-Channel 1 that operates through dedicated infrastructure rather than open financial markets. CIPS processed approximately 123 trillion yuan in 2023. The paper's claim that "China's path runs through capital account opening" may be correct in the long run; it is inadequate as a characterisation of what China is doing in the medium run.

**Missing literature:** Petry, Fichtner, and Heemskerk (2019) on index providers as financial infrastructure; Braun and Gabor (2020) on central bank digital currency infrastructural power; Chin and Helleiner (2008) on China's emerging role in global financial governance.

## 4. NEP Misspecification: The Coal/Oil/Nuclear Fungibility Problem

The paper defines NEP as production share of world primary energy minus net import share, treating all primary energy as fungible. China is the world's largest coal producer (~50–55% of global coal output) but also the world's largest coal consumer, producing a near-zero NEP. This is arithmetically consistent but analytically problematic.

**Monetary invoicing dimension:** Coal is traded almost entirely in dollars or bilateral arrangements. Oil is the monetary energy input — the commodity whose invoicing currency determines reserve demand. China's coal self-sufficiency is real energy security; it does not generate the same monetary leverage as oil self-sufficiency because coal is not globally invoiced in the same way. The paper's NEP formula treats these as equivalent when they are not.

**The Malacca Dilemma:** China's oil imports are approximately 75% seaborne and therefore subject to South China Sea/Malacca Strait vulnerability. A framework that assigns China zero energy position because it consumes its own coal misses that China is highly vulnerable on the specific energy dimension that carries monetary weight: imported hydrocarbons.

**Required:** Either (a) introduce an energy-type weighting distinguishing monetarily-relevant flows (oil, LNG) from domestically consumed non-traded energy (coal, grid-stabilised nuclear); or (b) explicitly defend the all-types-equal specification and explain why the distinction does not affect the framework's conclusions.

## 5. Yuan Internationalisation: Four Facts the Paper Suppresses

The paper states China's path runs through "institutional quality and capital account opening — consistent with the observed trajectory of gradual renminbi internationalisation." This suppresses four important facts:

1. **China's strategy deliberately avoids full capital account opening.** The e-CNY and CIPS architectures are designed precisely to internationalise yuan use without the vulnerability that full opening creates. If China succeeds, the paper's Channel 1 claim is partially refuted.

2. **Yuan reserve share has been growing monotonically** since full COFER reporting began in 2016. The paper acknowledges this series exists but analyses it in no detail.

3. **The Shanghai INE crude futures contract** (denominated in yuan, convertible to gold) was designed explicitly to create a non-dollar hydrocarbon invoicing mechanism. The paper's own Channel 1 logic applies exactly to this instrument. It is not mentioned.

4. **China-Russia energy corridor post-2022** demonstrates Channel 1 and Channel 2 dynamics activating for the yuan through energy supply relationships rather than capital account openness. Russia is settling energy sales in yuan. Russian central bank reserves include yuan-denominated assets. This is the paper's own mechanism operating in real time for China.

## 6. Security Dimension: BRI as Strange's Four Structures Simultaneously

The paper engages with the security-energy-monetary nexus through the Norrlof/Katzenstein axis. It describes the passive dimension of the security-monetary relationship but misses what China is actively constructing.

The BRI is not merely an infrastructure investment programme. It functions as a monetary penetration tool: creating yuan-denominated debt obligations, yuan settlement requirements in corridor countries, and CIPS adoption as a condition of financial participation. More importantly, BRI is simultaneously a knowledge structure project, a production structure project, and a finance structure project in Strange's own terms — potentially the most deliberate attempt to shift all four of Strange's structural power dimensions simultaneously since the United States did so after 1944. The paper's §2.2 invokes Strange but fails to apply her framework to the most obvious contemporary case.

**Missing literature:** Jones and Zeng (2019) on BRI and Chinese financial statecraft; Ye (2020) on state-mobilised globalisation in China; Chin and Helleiner (2008) on China as creditor.

---

## 7. Required Revisions

**Non-negotiable:**
1. A dedicated China section (minimum 2,000 words) in the present register, treating China's de-dollarisation strategy as a test of Channel 1 operating through political construction rather than energy position
2. Revision of TMPI scoring for China to incorporate nuclear trajectory rather than current share, with explicit comparison to India's PFBR timeline vs China's TMSR-LF1 timeline
3. A revised institutional quality measure for China capturing CIPS, e-CNY, and swap lines rather than WGI dimensions
4. NEP formula revision or explicit defence of the coal/oil/nuclear fungibility assumption
5. A China-specific falsifiable prediction in the falsification registry (§8.3)

**Recommended:**
6. Engage BRI as Strange's four structures through a dedicated paragraph
7. Add minimum 8–10 items from the literature gaps: Prasad (2017), Liao and McDowell (2015), Subacchi (2017), Chin and Helleiner (2008)
8. Figure 5 should include China's MPI trajectory

---

*The paper is not a reject. The theoretical framework is original and the India prediction is bold. But it cannot be published by any journal that takes China's monetary challenge to the dollar system seriously — which, in 2026, is every major journal in IPE and IR.*
