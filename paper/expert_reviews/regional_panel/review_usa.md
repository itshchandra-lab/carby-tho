# Peer Review — USA / IPE Specialist
## "The Carbon-Thorium Standard: Energy-Backed Currencies and the Architecture of Monetary Failure in a Multipolar World"

**Reviewer specialisation:** American hegemony, dollar dominance, US energy policy, financial sanctions, political economy of the international monetary system (Norrlof, Farrell-Newman, Kirshner, Eichengreen tradition)
**Journal:** Journal of International Political Economy / Review of International Political Economy
**Recommendation:** Major Revisions
**Score:** 6/10

---

## Summary Assessment

This is an ambitious, intellectually serious paper that identifies a genuine lacuna in reserve currency theory and proposes a framework to formalise it. The writing is often excellent. The Japan natural experiment is the strongest piece of evidence. The falsification registry is a genuinely admirable commitment, rare in this literature.

However, the paper has a structural problem that the authors almost diagnose but then contain too quickly. The quantitative core is not merely "US-driven" in the mild sense that US coefficient estimates are large. It is USA-driven in the sense that the entire inferential enterprise rests on one country, one era, one set of confounds that the paper has not adequately distinguished, and a coefficient (β=84.0) whose interpretation is far less stable than the paper acknowledges. Until those confounds are addressed, the paper offers what is, at bottom, a sophisticated single-country case study dressed in comparative frameworks it cannot currently sustain.

---

## 1. The Shale Revolution as Confirmation: Causal Identification Fails the Most Important Test

The paper's headline quantitative claim: US shale revolution (NEP rising 0.143→0.166, 2010–2019) accounts for USD reserve share stabilisation at 58.5–59% from 2021 onwards, via a ten-year lag. The logic is clean and the predicted direction is correct. But the causal identification is inadequate.

Consider what happened simultaneously in 2021–2024:
- The most aggressive Federal Reserve tightening cycle since Volcker (near-zero → 5.25–5.50%)
- A post-COVID flight-to-safety episode of unusually long duration
- Russia/Ukraine energy price spikes increasing the USD-denominated invoice value of globally traded commodities (strengthening demand for dollar working balances — the paper's own Channel 1 mechanism, operating through price not sovereignty)
- REER appreciation mechanically compressing non-dollar reserve shares when measured in dollar terms

The paper does not control for any of these. The appeal to "structured case comparison" in §6.1 is honest — but if the comparison is structured, rival explanations must be structured out, not merely noted. The 2021 stabilisation is equally consistent with: Obstfeld-Rogoff flight-to-safety dynamics, Gopinath dominant currency paradigm at elevated oil prices, and mean-reversion from COVID-era anomalies. The energy mechanism is consistent with the data. It is not distinguishable from these alternatives on present evidence.

**Required:** Either (a) a placebo test showing that periods of flat or declining US energy position did not produce reserve-share stabilisation; or (b) a mechanism trace showing that the specific channels the energy mechanism predicts (petrodollar recycling flows, trade invoice accumulation) specifically increased in 2021–2024.

## 2. One Observation, One Country: The Limits of a General Theory

The paper's frankness about the jackknife result is commendable. But the containment strategy — "claims rest on structured case comparison, not panel finding" — deserves scrutiny.

If the quantitative leg rests on the United States case, and the US case is subject to the confounds above, then the backward register (EU ETS governance construction) and the Japan natural experiment carry the theoretical weight. Both are consistent with the energy mechanism. Neither *requires* the specific transmission chain the paper proposes. The EU ETS finding demonstrates that carbon governance is politically constructed — not that political construction produces reserve currency consequences. The Japan case demonstrates nuclear capacity loss correlates with failure to gain reserve share — not that this is caused by the energy mechanism rather than by Japan's documented institutional preference against reserve currency status.

**The deeper methodological issue:** A general framework whose quantitative core is a single-country result is not a general framework. It is a single-country framework with comparative illustrations. Kindleberger's HST was essentially a single-country argument about the US in the 1930s, and it generated thirty years of productive scholarship. But the paper should own this forthrightly. The most honest formulation: "We propose an energy-monetary mechanism; we have strong evidence for it in the US case; the comparative cases are consistency checks; we are betting the forward prediction on the same mechanism applying to India." That framing is defensible. The current framing — oscillating between claiming generality and disclaiming it — is not.

**An additional problem with β=84.0 applied to India:** The coefficient is estimated from a reserve currency hegemon with deep capital markets and dollar-invoiced energy trade, then applied to a state with a closed capital account (KAOPEN = -1.254), 1.6% FX turnover, and a nuclear programme 14 years behind schedule. Different structural starting positions, different institutional configuration, different channels. The transfer is not defended anywhere in the paper.

## 3. Sanctions and Weaponised Interdependence: GS Cannot Capture Chokepoint Dynamics

The paper engages Farrell-Newman (2019, 2023) and McDowell (2023) competently at the level of argument. But the formalism does not follow the theory. GS is defined as energy governance volatility / commodity benchmark volatility — a regime-level, economy-wide variable. Financial sanctions are entity-specific, targeted, and operate through chokepoint infrastructure (SWIFT, correspondent banking, dollar clearing at the Federal Reserve). These are categorically different modes of power.

The "brittle chokepoint" dynamic in Farrell-Newman: the US achieves coercive reach through asymmetric network centrality; using that reach degrades the centrality itself by motivating construction of alternative nodes. This is a second-order dynamic argument about self-eroding chokepoint power. The GS term captures first-order political construction of energy governance regimes. It cannot capture the second-order dynamic because GS is not a function of how frequently or aggressively the chokepoint is used.

**The Russia section (§6.5) comes closest to engaging this.** The Mechanism A/B distinction is analytically useful. But the paper then says Mechanism B "does not independently support the thesis" — whereas Mechanism B is arguably the more important finding for the sanctions literature. SWIFT exclusion accelerating the formation of energy-denominated bilateral corridors that were latent in the energy geography is a direct demonstration of how sanctions interact with the energy-monetary mechanism. The GS term should reflect this: an excluded state's energy governance infrastructure becomes more, not less, politically durable with respect to the Western monetary system — which argues for a differentiated GS construct (GS of inclusion vs. GS of exclusion).

**Missing:** Braun (2020, 2022) on the Federal Reserve's institutional role in extending dollar hegemony through swap lines. Fed swap line allocation is determined on political criteria, not market ones — it went to US allies and key trading partners. This is governance construction analogous to the EU ETS allocation rules: a politically constructed allocation mechanism with GS > 1 in the paper's own terms. Its absence from the framework is a significant gap.

## 4. Petrodollar Durability: β=84.0 Assumes a Stable Structural Relationship

β=84.0 is a long-run DOLS coefficient estimated over a period that largely predates petrodollar stress. As of 2026, the petrodollar system faces genuine pressure: Saudi Arabia pricing some oil in yuan for Chinese customers, OPEC+ multiple fractures, GCC sustained internal debates about dollar-peg maintenance, frozen Russian sovereign reserves (~$300 billion) signalling to every surplus state about dollar risk.

If the structural relationship between US energy position and USD reserve share is mediated by petrodollar recycling (the paper's own Channel 2), and that mechanism is now weakening, β=84.0 is an overestimate of the forward relationship. The paper does not address this. It should either: (a) demonstrate that the ARDL bounds test holds in sub-samples that include the post-2022 period; or (b) acknowledge explicitly that β=84.0 may be a declining structural parameter, not a stable one — with first-order implications for the India forward prediction.

---

## 5. The TMPI Formula and the US Nuclear Problem

The paper normalises US TMPI to 100. But US nuclear energy share has *declined* from ~8.4% (2010) to 7.6% (2024). Fleet average age: ~41 years. Last new reactor before Vogtle was in 1996. Three Mile Island Unit 1 restarted in 2024 under a commercial PPA (not a sovereignty-enhancing nuclear programme). Vogtle Units 3 and 4 came online seven years late and ~$17 billion over budget. If US nuclear share continues declining — not a remote scenario given fleet aging and unfavourable new construction economics — the gap between US TMPI and India's rising TMPI closes faster than the paper's scenario analysis suggests.

**The scenario analysis should include a US nuclear decline scenario** where US share falls to 5–6% by 2035 (fleet retirements without replacement) while India's rises to 3–4% (PFBR online, AHWR development progressing). In that scenario, the US-India TMPI gap narrows substantially and India moves to second place. This is the scenario under which the India prediction becomes most important — and the paper should own it.

## 6. Hegemonic Stability Theory: Does the MPI Actually Outperform?

The paper argues HST "cannot identify challengers before they emerge." The MPI is presented as correcting this. But looking at the TMPI rankings: USA = 100, Canada = 20.8, India = 15.8, Brazil = 9.7, Russia = 9.3. The only state with a plausible path to challenging US monetary dominance on this framework holds 1.6% of global FX turnover, has a closed capital account, has a nuclear programme 14 years behind schedule, and is predicted to reach 3–5% FX turnover by 2031. This is not a description of a challenger. It is a description of a niche player whose rise is conditional on three sequential institutional transformations with individually low probability over a ten-year horizon.

HST would also predict that the United States retains dominance and no credible challenger has yet emerged. The MPI produces the same prediction with a more elaborate mechanism. The question: has the framework genuinely improved on HST's explanatory and predictive power, or has it added analytical complexity while confirming the incumbent's dominance with more decimal places?

The honest answer: "Yes, for the present era the MPI and HST agree. The value of the MPI is its forward-looking specificity — it tells you not just that the US dominates but how dominance will be contested and by whom as the energy transition progresses." That is a defensible answer, but it needs to be made more explicitly.

## 7. Missing Literature

- **Braun, Benjamin. 2020.** "American Asset Manager Capitalism." — On the Federal Reserve's institutional role in dollar hegemony extension through swap lines; directly analogous to EU ETS allocation as GS > 1 instrument.
- **Pettis, Michael. 2023.** *Trade Wars Are Class Wars.* — The "exorbitant burden" argument: reserve currency status forces persistent US current account deficits, de-industrialises the manufacturing base. The paper treats USD reserve share preservation as unambiguously good for US structural power. Pettis challenges this directly.
- **Stiglitz, Joseph.** Work on global reserve system externalities — particularly the domestic political economy of reserve currency status (current account deficit requirements) that may be politically unsustainable.
- **Braun and Gabor (2020)** on central bank digital currency infrastructural power — relevant to the sanctions/dollar chokepoint dynamic.

---

## 8. Required Revisions

1. Address the simultaneous 2021–2024 confounds (Fed rate cycle, flight-to-safety, REER appreciation, petrodollar stress) — either through mechanism trace data or explicit acknowledgement that the US case is "consistent with" rather than "confirmed by" the energy mechanism
2. Make an explicit choice: own the single-country origin of β=84.0 and position comparative cases as consistency checks, not confirmations
3. Address the coefficient transfer problem: β=84.0 from a reserve currency hegemon applied to a challenger requires justification
4. Acknowledge β=84.0 as a potentially declining structural parameter given petrodollar unwinding
5. Add a US nuclear decline scenario to the sensitivity analysis
6. Either extend the GS framework to capture chokepoint/exclusion dynamics or explicitly acknowledge the level-of-analysis difference between GS and Farrell-Newman
7. Add Braun, Pettis, and Stiglitz to the bibliography
