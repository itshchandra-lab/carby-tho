## 1. The India Prediction: More Hope Than Political Analysis

The paper's claim that India's INR will reach 3–5% of global FX turnover by 2031 depends on a sequencing chain: nuclear capacity growth → current account improvement → capital account liberalisation → INR invoicing demand. This chain misses the political economy reality of India's capital account politics.

India's KAOPEN score has been frozen at −1.254 since 1970 not because of a missing credibility mechanism but because capital control is a **deliberate strategic choice**. The RBI views capital account openness as a vulnerability vector, not an inevitable downstream consequence of energy credibility. Even as current account conditions improved in 2003–2007 (during India's growth peak), liberalisation remained cautious, reactive to crisis episodes, and reversible. Modi-era nuclear policy is advancing, but the assumption that rising energy self-sufficiency automatically triggers capital account opening contradicts forty-five years of Indian monetary conservatism.

More problematically: the paper conflates the possibility of invoicing with the political will to establish invoicing norms. Trade invoicing in INR requires that India's partners — GCC states, ASEAN trading partners, African commodity suppliers — accept INR settlement when they can settle in dollars. Currency unions work because members are institutionally committed to the arrangement. Bilateral corridors work when both parties benefit from sanction evasion (Russia-India crude barter) or face coercion that leaves them no option. There is no mechanism in the paper explaining why refiners in Dubai or Singapore would denominate crude sales in INR absent institutional embedding or coercive pressure.

The petrodollar arrangement succeeded because it combined security dependence (Saudi Arabia needed US protection) with recycling architecture (OPEC surpluses needed safe assets). India has neither — it is not a USD-dependent security client, and it offers no comparable recycling pull.

The paper acknowledges India's non-alignment as an advantage but reads it backwards. Non-alignment historically meant India avoided the dollar bloc; it did not mean India could independently command invoicing demand. India's strategic autonomy makes it *less* likely that energy partners will denominate in INR out of institutional coercion or dependence.

## 2. The Petrodollar Architecture: Incomplete Framing

The paper relies on Spiro (1999) for the 1974 petrodollar story but does not grapple with the full complexity of why that arrangement persisted and why challengers failed. The US-Saudi deal succeeded because:

- Saudi Arabia was the swing producer and OPEC's most conservative member (aligned with US Cold War interests)
- The UK recycled OPEC surpluses through the Eurodollar market, creating institutional infrastructure for dollar intermediation
- Gulf states had no alternative security provider; US protection was non-negotiable
- The dollar's depth in global financial markets provided no practical substitute

Post-2022 ruble-yuan energy corridors are real but constitute *alternative settlement at the margin*, not systemic alternatives. The analogy to a future "thorium standard" is therefore weak. Thorium requires the establishment of invoicing norms not from necessity (as with Saudi oil dependence on US protection) but from affinity and convenience. That is a different institutional problem entirely.

---

## 3. AUKUS and Australia: The Katzenstein Filter Is Underspecified

The paper predicts that Australia's nuclear development under AUKUS will not produce independent AUD reserve gains because Australia is embedded in the US security system. This is politically informed and likely correct. But the mechanism is asserted, not demonstrated.

What would constitute falsification? If AUD reserve share grows even modestly with AUKUS nuclear deployment, the paper claims the filter is refuted. But Australia's existing "niche" position in reserves reflects its commodity-exporter status and financial openness, not AUKUS per se. AUKUS creates a new institutional binding — security coordination that may actually *reduce* incentives for AUD invoicing if AUKUS includes financial integration as a security measure. The distinction between "niche reinforcement through alliance" and "niche reinforcement through commodity dependence" is left underspecified.

---

## 4. Russia: The Mechanism Holds Descriptively, Not Explanatorily

The paper correctly notes that ruble-yuan and ruble-rupee corridors followed pre-existing energy supply routes. This is true and important. But it is insufficient as validation of the mechanism. The corridors formed because:

- Russia faced immediate payment settlement crises under sanctions (Mechanism B is causally sufficient)
- China and India faced strategic incentives to diversify from dollar settlement (independently motivated)
- Energy geography provided the pathway, but coercion provided the impetus

Demonstrating that energy geography *facilitates* response to coercion is not the same as demonstrating that energy position predicts monetary power *absent* coercion. Russia's evidence is contaminated by crisis conditions.

Moreover, the paper shows no evidence that ruble invoicing in energy trades has actually deepened currency demand beyond settlement mechanics. Central bank reserve accumulation in rubles has been minimal. The corridor formation is real; the monetary power translation is absent. This is exactly the gap the paper should address but does not.

---

## 5. Policy Implications: Audience Mismatch

The paper makes no policy recommendations, and that is a significant weakness for any policy audience. The forward register asks a question with clear policy stakes — which rising powers should US policymakers monitor as potential challengers? — but refuses to answer it.

A policy audience reading this would extract two conclusions: (a) energy transitions matter to monetary power in ways standard accounts miss, and (b) India is the rising power with the deepest structural position to build alternative monetary infrastructure by 2035. Both are policy-relevant. But the paper does not articulate what these facts should *mean* for US strategy, Indian monetary ambitions, or the architecture of the next multilateral monetary system.

---

## 6. Top Three Foreign Policy Problems

**First:** The India prediction rests on capital account liberalisation happening as a downstream consequence of energy credibility improvements. This contradicts 45 years of Indian monetary conservatism and misunderstands RBI decision-making. The sequencing argument (channels 3→2→1) is logically possible but politically implausible.

**Second:** The petrodollar analogy is invoked but not carefully constructed. The 1974 arrangement was unique in combining security dependence, recycling necessity, and market depth. The paper does not establish that a "thorium standard" could replicate this combination. Thorium reserves do not create security leverage; thorium energy self-sufficiency does not generate recycling surpluses.

**Third:** The paper makes claims about Russia, China, and India's future monetary power that exceed what the data supports. The quantitative finding is USA-driven (acknowledged in §6.8), and the forward prediction (India by 2031) depends on political transformations the paper neither demonstrates nor adequately interrogates.

---

**Verdict:** This is serious analytical work that captures something real. The backward register and the Japan natural experiment are the strongest contributions. But the forward prediction — the paper's policy payload — depends on political transformations in India that the paper neither demonstrates nor adequately interrogates. The paper should either substantially deepen the political economy analysis of Indian capital account reform, or reframe the India case as conditional on specific observable shifts in RBI policy that the paper identifies as necessary but acknowledges are not currently on track.
