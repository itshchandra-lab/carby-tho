# Peer Review — BRICS and South-South Cooperation Expert
## "The Carbon-Thorium Standard: Energy-Backed Currencies and the Architecture of Monetary Failure in a Multipolar World"

**Reviewer specialisation:** Political economy of BRICS, Global South monetary architecture, South-South development finance
**Journal:** *Review of International Political Economy* / *Development and Change*
**Recommendation:** Major Revisions
**Score:** 6/10

---

## Summary Assessment

This manuscript makes a genuinely original contribution to the literature on energy geography and monetary power. The MPI framework is elegant, the falsification conditions are unusually precise, and the treatment of Russia as a Mechanism-A-versus-B problem is the most analytically honest treatment of post-2022 Russian monetary isolation in this literature. However, the paper has a structural lacuna that requires major revision: it analyses BRICS member states as if they were independent monetary actors operating in a bilateral world, when the most consequential monetary-political development of the 2020s is precisely the attempt to build a *collective* BRICS monetary architecture. This is not a peripheral omission. The paper's core empirical predictions — INR internationalisation, BRL stagnation, CNY marginalisation in the thorium era, RUB suppression — cannot be evaluated without situating them within the political economy of BRICS collective action.

---

## 1. BRICS as Collective Monetary Project: The Absent Architecture

The paper's most consequential omission is institutional. The 2024 Kazan Summit produced a specific mandate to develop a "BRICS Bridge" multilateral payment settlement platform, building on Russia's SPFS and India's UPI-adjacent architecture, with explicit discussion of a commodity-backed unit of account for intra-BRICS trade settlement. This is not a currency union proposal. It is precisely the kind of "layered" alternative that the paper, following Pforr, Pape, and Petry (2025), identifies as the operative mode of monetary challenge to dollar hegemony. The paper cites PPP 2025 but never asks whether BRICS collective architecture *already constitutes* a layer.

This matters for the MPI framework specifically. The paper models GS (governance score) as a function of domestic institutional quality (WGI) and bilateral energy corridor depth. But a BRICS payment platform creates a *collective* governance structure that no individual state's WGI captures. If India settles its Russian oil purchases — approximately 1.3 million barrels per day by late 2023 — in a BRICS settlement unit that is partially INR-denominated, then INR internationalisation is not driven by India's domestic governance quality alone. It is partially driven by a collective architecture that the paper's GS measure systematically excludes.

More sharply: the paper predicts INR FX turnover rises from 1.6% to 3-5% by BIS 2031. This is a testable prediction. But the mechanism through which it occurs matters enormously for whether the paper's theory is confirmed or falsified. If INR reaches 3% via BRICS Bridge settlement (collective architecture) rather than via direct bilateral trade invoicing (bilateral corridor), the paper's mechanism is wrong even if the point prediction is correct. The paper needs a section that distinguishes these transmission pathways and specifies which mechanism its framework predicts will dominate.

---

## 2. BRICS+ Energy Consolidation: A Framework Stress Test

The 2024 BRICS+ expansion is not adequately theorised. Saudi Arabia, UAE, and Iran together account for a larger share of proved crude oil reserves than Russia, and their inclusion creates an energy-monetary nexus that the paper's individual-state MPI framework cannot handle.

Under the paper's framework, Saudi Arabia would have extremely high present-era NEP but low TMPI (thorium reserves negligible; nuclear programme nascent). The UAE would have high NEP and substantially higher WGI than Saudi Arabia. Iran would have high NEP but near-zero GS due to SWIFT exclusion and sanctions — structurally similar to Russia in the paper's framework. None of these cases are discussed.

The critical question: does the BRICS+ energy consolidation *raise the collective MPI* of the bloc? If we think of BRICS+ as an energy-monetary entity — which is precisely how Russia has marketed it — then the bloc's aggregate energy position is extraordinary. Saudi + UAE + Iran + Russia in hydrocarbons, plus India + Brazil + Australia-adjacent thorium reserves in nuclear transition metals, represents something qualitatively different from individual state trajectories.

There is also a political economy interaction the paper misses between India's thorium-based TMPI and Saudi Arabia's hydrocarbon position within BRICS+. The paper predicts that India's monetary rise is driven by nuclear transition and the implicit depreciation of oil as a reserve backing asset. But India's energy position depends heavily on cheap Russian and Iranian oil in the transition period — the very hydrocarbons that the paper's p_t transition function is eventually replacing. The political economy of India's energy imports is incoherent with the paper's prediction about India's energy-monetary independence unless the timing of p_t is specified much more carefully.

## 3. New Development Bank and the Governance Score

The paper treats governance quality as exogenous, measured by WGI. This is a serious theoretical weakness from a development finance perspective, and the NDB problem makes it acute.

The New Development Bank, established in 2015, has by 2025 approved over $35 billion in development finance, with a portfolio that explicitly prioritises climate-aligned infrastructure. In 2021 the NDB formally adopted a Paris Alignment policy. The NDB is in the business of building energy governance structures in its member states — Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa — that are not captured by WGI scores. When the NDB finances a solar installation in Rajasthan or a transmission grid in São Paulo state, it is engaging in what the paper calls "governance construction." The NDB is building the institutional infrastructure through which energy sovereignty translates into monetary credibility, but the paper's GS measure cannot see this.

This is not merely a measurement problem. It is a theoretical problem. The paper argues that governance construction is the mechanism through which TMPI translates into future reserve status. The NDB is precisely a multilateral vehicle for accelerating governance construction in BRICS members. If Brazil's WGI = 0.46 is a binding constraint on BRL internationalisation, and the NDB is actively financing governance-building infrastructure in Brazil, then the paper's falsification condition for BRL ("BRL does NOT gain reserve share without capital account opening") may be too narrowly specified. Capital account opening is one pathway. NDB-facilitated governance construction is another.

Grabel (2018), whose concept of "productive incoherence" in global financial governance is directly relevant here, is absent from the paper. Grabel argues that the fragmentation of financial governance creates more space for developmental finance than a coherent hierarchical system would. The NDB, CRA, AIIB, CIPS, BRICS Bridge — these do not constitute an alternative Bretton Woods. But they may constitute a Graebel-ian productive incoherence that enables the layering strategy the paper endorses, without requiring the domestic governance construction that the paper treats as a prerequisite.

## 4. India-China Rivalry Within BRICS: The Coherence Problem

The paper predicts India TMPI = 15.8 and China TMPI = 4.2. This differential is the most politically consequential prediction in the paper, and the paper does not engage with its implications for BRICS coherence.

India and China have fought a border war (1962), have ongoing territorial disputes in Ladakh, and compete for influence across South Asia, Southeast Asia, and Africa. Within BRICS, India has historically acted as a brake on China-driven initiatives — India was notably reluctant about the Kazan BRICS Bridge discussions precisely because deep integration with Chinese monetary architecture creates strategic vulnerabilities. India's UPI-adjacent payment architecture is explicitly designed as an alternative to both SWIFT *and* CIPS — it is a third pole, not a Chinese-adjacent instrument.

The paper's prediction that India rises within the monetary order while China is marginalised in the thorium transition era is, from a political economy standpoint, a prediction about *Indian strategic autonomy* from Chinese monetary infrastructure as much as it is about energy geography. The Katzenstein filter that the paper applies to India should logically also be applied to the CNY system. India has powerful domestic political incentives to route its monetary internationalisation *independently* of both USD and CNY infrastructure. This strengthens the paper's India argument but the paper does not make it.

More damagingly, if India-China rivalry is sufficiently intense, India's presence in BRICS constrains the bloc's collective monetary capacity. India will not fully integrate into a BRICS settlement system that China dominates or even heavily shapes. This creates a collective action problem: the two states with the highest monetary ambitions within BRICS — China (present era) and India (thorium transition era) — have incompatible strategic logics. BRICS collective monetary architecture requires either Chinese dominance (which India will resist), Indian-Chinese condominium (requiring trust that does not exist), or a deliberately weak architecture that neither state can instrumentalise (which limits the architecture's monetary significance).

## 5. Brazil: The Paper's Most Important Negative Prediction — and Its Weakest

The falsification condition for Brazil — "BRL does NOT gain reserve share without Chinn-Ito KAOPEN improvement by BIS 2037" — is the paper's most empirically vulnerable prediction, for three reasons.

**First**, the paper systematically underanalyses Brazil's present-era energy export position. Brazil is not only the world's second-largest holder of thorium reserves. It is also a significant oil exporter: pre-salt field production reached approximately 3.5 million barrels per day by 2024. Brazil also has the world's most sophisticated biofuels complex (sugarcane ethanol), and hydropower meets approximately 60-65% of its domestic electricity generation. Brazil's NEP in the present era is structurally strong, and the paper's treatment of Brazil as primarily a TMPI story (thorium future) rather than an NEP story (energy export present) is a significant analytical error. Under the paper's own framework, a state with high NEP and moderate GS should be generating some meaningful MPI in the present register. The paper needs to show its MPI calculation for Brazil explicitly in the present era before asserting the capital account binding constraint dominates.

**Second**, the paper does not engage with the Lula administration's heterodox trajectory. The third Lula administration (2023-present) has pursued managed currency bilateralisation: BRL-CNY direct swap arrangements, BRL-ARS trade agreements, and active participation in BRICS payment system discussions. This is not capital account *opening* in the Chinn-Ito sense. It is selective bilateral convertibility — closer to Grabel's "productive incoherence" than to anything the Chinn-Ito index captures. The paper's falsification condition, by tying BRL internationalisation to Chinn-Ito improvement, may be measuring the wrong policy variable for the Lula-era trajectory.

**Third**, the paper's binding constraint characterisation — "double: capacity and institutions both weak" — is empirically contestable. Brazil's Central Bank achieved formal operational independence in 2021 (Law 13,820/21). Brazil's financial system is sophisticated, with deep domestic capital markets and a functioning inflation-targeting regime. Monetary institutions in Brazil are substantially more credible than fiscal institutions, and it is monetary institution quality that most directly affects currency internationalisation.

## 6. De-Dollarisation as Collective Action Problem

The paper's MPI framework is structurally bilateral: energy geography generates bilateral corridor effects that accumulate into reserve currency status. This is the paper's most significant theoretical limitation from the perspective of the de-dollarisation literature.

De-dollarisation in practice is a collective action problem with strong network externalities. Dollar dominance persists partly because everyone expects everyone else to continue using dollars — coordination on an alternative requires either a hegemon to anchor the alternative or a credible commitment mechanism that enables simultaneous defection from the dollar. BRICS collective action discussions are precisely an attempt to build such a commitment mechanism. The 2024 Kazan discussions, the BRICS Bridge proposals, the CRA — these are attempts to lower the coordination costs of collective de-dollarisation.

The paper's framework cannot model this. Its MPI captures individual state energy-monetary trajectories, but it has no term for network externality effects — the way that each additional state joining a non-dollar settlement network lowers the cost for the next state to join. If BRICS Bridge reaches critical mass (India and China both settle intra-BRICS trade through it), the incentive for Brazil, South Africa, and BRICS+ members to join increases nonlinearly.

This also means the paper's framework cannot identify the threshold at which collective de-dollarisation becomes self-sustaining. Eichengreen, Mehl, and Chiţu (2017) provide tools for this analysis that the paper does not engage.

## 7. South-South Energy Finance and NEP Calculations

Chinese energy loans through BRI — coal plants in Pakistan, hydropower in Ethiopia, oil infrastructure in Angola and the DRC — create energy dependencies in recipient states that the paper's NEP measure does not account for.

A state whose energy infrastructure is Chinese-financed and potentially Chinese-operated has a weaker energy sovereignty position than its raw energy production statistics suggest. Pakistan's coal plants are not energy independence — they are energy dependence on a Chinese capital-and-technology provider. The paper's NEP should arguably incorporate an energy *debt* or *dependency* adjustment for states with large Chinese energy infrastructure exposure.

Chinese BRI energy finance is itself a form of governance construction operating at the bilateral and multilateral level. When China finances a hydropower dam in Ethiopia or a port-adjacent oil refinery in Tanzania, it is building institutional and physical infrastructure through which CNY-denominated energy invoicing could be extended — monetary statecraft operating through energy finance, exactly the mechanism the paper theorises for individual states. The paper needs to engage with whether BRI energy finance is a form of Chinese governance score construction in recipient states.

## 8. The Pforr-Pape-Petry "Layering" Argument: Is It Coherent?

The paper uses PPP (2025) to argue that INR does not need to displace USD — it just needs to "occupy a layer." This is the paper's most theoretically sophisticated move, and it is broadly correct as political economy. But the paper does not think through what "layering" means in the context of BRICS institutional architecture.

The existing BRICS monetary layer already exists in embryonic form: CIPS processes CNY-denominated transactions; the CRA provides $100 billion in emergency liquidity support; the NDB provides development finance in local currencies; BRICS Bridge (if implemented) will provide multilateral settlement. If this architecture constitutes a "layer," it is currently a *Chinese-anchored* layer — CIPS is a Chinese system, the NDB's largest shareholder is China, and the CRA's IMF-link requirement (above 30% of quota) effectively makes it dollar-adjacent rather than a genuinely alternative instrument.

The paper's prediction that India occupies the emerging thorium-era monetary layer implies INR would either operate within the existing BRICS layer (politically problematic for India) or construct a parallel layer (high coordination costs the paper does not account for). The "layering" metaphor obscures rather than clarifies the political architecture question: whose layer is it?

Grabel's (2018) argument that *productive incoherence* of multiple overlapping, competing, inconsistent monetary governance structures is actually more beneficial for developmental finance is directly relevant here. Applied to the paper's argument: INR may rise within a productive incoherence that includes CIPS, BRICS Bridge, bilateral swap lines, and UPI-adjacent settlement — none of which constitute a single coherent "layer" but all of which collectively reduce dollar dependence.

## 9. Missing Literature

**Gallagher, Kevin P., and Richard Kozul-Wright. 2022.** *The Case for a New Bretton Woods.* Cambridge: Polity. The paper's argument about energy sovereignty as a missing mechanism in reserve currency theory needs to engage with Gallagher and Kozul-Wright's argument that what is missing is *development finance architecture*, not energy geography.

**Ghosh, Jayati. 2023.** "A Viable International Monetary System." *Development and Change.* Ghosh's framework for heterodox alternatives to dollar hegemony directly addresses the mechanism through which rising powers can achieve monetary autonomy without currency displacement.

**Grabel, Ilene. 2018.** *When Things Don't Fall Apart.* MIT Press. As noted above, Grabel's framework is the strongest existing theoretical basis for the paper's layering claim, and its absence is surprising.

**Chin, Gregory. 2014.** "China's Bold Economic Statecraft." *Current History.* Chin's treatment of Chinese monetary statecraft as deliberately infrastructure-led — BRI, CIPS, NDB — is directly relevant to the paper's treatment of CNY marginalisation in the thorium era.

**Eichengreen, Barry, Arnaud Mehl, and Livia Chiţu. 2017.** *How Global Currencies Work.* Princeton University Press. The paper's MPI framework predicts bilateral corridor effects accumulate into reserve status. Eichengreen et al.'s network externality analysis directly challenges whether bilateral accumulation can achieve the critical mass necessary for reserve status without coordination.

**NDB Annual Reports (2015-2025).** The paper's treatment of governance construction as a theoretical mechanism is not grounded in any empirical analysis of how multilateral development banks actually build governance.

---

## 10. Overall Verdict

**Score: 6/10. Recommendation: Major Revisions.**

This is a paper with a genuinely original framework, unusual precision in its falsification conditions, and important insights about the energy-monetary nexus. The Russia Mechanism-A-versus-B distinction is analytically exemplary. The Katzenstein filter applied to India is both original and empirically defensible.

However, the paper has a structural limitation not addressable through minor revisions. It analyses BRICS member states as if they were independent bilateral actors in a world where reserve currency competition happens state-by-state. The actual political economy of the 2020s de-dollarisation process is collective, contested within BRICS, and embedded in multilateral institutions — the NDB, CRA, CIPS, Kazan settlement discussions — that the paper does not engage.

The revisions required are substantial but achievable. The paper should add a section (approximately 3,000-4,000 words) on BRICS collective monetary architecture, engaging specifically with: the Kazan Summit outcomes, the NDB-GS relationship, the India-China rivalry constraint, and Grabel's productive incoherence framework. The Brazil section should incorporate present-era NEP analysis alongside the TMPI story. Falsification conditions for China and BRICS+ members should be added in the same format as Brazil and Russia. And the missing literature identified above should be incorporated throughout.

The paper in its current form makes a contribution that BRICS specialists will find frustrating precisely because the framework is good enough to address these questions — it simply has not been asked to do so. That is a problem of scope and ambition, not of fundamental error, and it is correctable.
