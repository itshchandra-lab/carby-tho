## 1. Strange and the Finance-Energy Linkage: Is the Contribution Genuine?

The paper's core claim (§2.2) is that Strange identified the finance-energy linkage but "left underdeveloped" its formalisation as a mechanism across transitions. This requires interrogation.

Strange (1988: Ch. 4) explicitly argued that the dollar's structural power rested on simultaneous command of security, production, finance, and knowledge structures, with energy as the lynchpin that converted security guarantees (Saudi protection) into financial flows (recycling). She treated oil pricing in dollars not as contingent institutional choice but as the *logical consequence* of US security dominance. Strange's actual claim was stronger than this paper credits: she argued the finance-energy link was *non-transferable* absent security reordering.

The paper's claim — that Strange identified the link but not its formal reproducibility across eras — mischaracterises her. Strange explicitly traced coal→oil→?next, noting that whoever controls the energy input controls the invoicing currency. Her actual gap was not formalisation but specification of *activation conditions*. She did not specify why thorium differs structurally from uranium or lithium in ways that would regenerate Strange's mechanism. The paper supplies that specification (§7.1: governance capacity, geographic concentration, deployment pathway, civilisational scale). This is genuine addition, but it is *narrower* than claimed. The paper operationalises Strange, not extends her.

**Assessment:** Modest but real contribution. The three-channel decomposition (§3.4) advances Strange by showing invoicing ≠ recycling ≠ credibility. But the claim of theoretical novelty oversells the work relative to Strange 1988.

## 2. Farrell-Newman and Weaponised Interdependence: Is the Framework Stretched?

The paper cites Farrell-Newman (2023) on chokepoint weapons at §2.3, then virtually drops it. This is appropriate.

Farrell and Newman's argument (2019, 2023) concerns financial and information network nodes — SWIFT, payments systems, semiconductor design — where unilateral US exclusion imposes costs on others. The mechanism is network topology: removing a single state creates cascading failures for users dependent on that node.

The paper does not claim the energy-monetary mechanism is a Farrell-Newman chokepoint. It correctly treats F-N as explaining institutional *degradation* (why dollar persistence erodes), not the underlying energy mechanism itself. The Russia case (§6.5) gestures toward F-N's relevance: the SWIFT exclusion and EU ETS expulsion reveal that Western infrastructure is politically excludable (Mechanism B). But the paper explicitly distinguishes this from the structural energy geography mechanism (Mechanism A). This is analytically sound.

**Assessment:** The framework is not stretched. F-N is correctly positioned as explaining institutional inertia's decay rate, not the base mechanism.

## 3. Russia: Can the Two-Mechanism Distinction Be Tested?

The claim (§6.5) is falsifiable in principle but testing faces a fundamental problem: **simultaneity**.

Mechanism A (structural energy geography) predicts that pre-existing energy supply chains would permit rapid bilateral corridor formation. Mechanism B (coercion revelation) predicts that SWIFT exclusion would accelerate alternative infrastructure.

Both mechanisms operated identically in timing. You cannot isolate A from B without a counterfactual. The paper's suggestion — that if corridors persist post-sanctions-lift, Mechanism A wins — is insufficient. Corridors could persist for reputational reasons or financial sunk costs independent of underlying geography.

The falsification logic is also asymmetric. If corridors dissolve post-sanctions-lift, it supports Mechanism B and falsifies A. But if corridors persist, it does not uniquely support A; it could reflect network switching costs post-formation. The paper needs an additional test: Do alternative corridors form with non-energy-major states post-sanctions? If INR-RUB energy settlement outperforms INR-RUB general bilateral settlement by a statistically significant margin, that supports A. Current evidence does not permit this test.

**Assessment:** The distinction is conceptually sound but empirically under-identified. The falsification logic leans heavily on dissolving as the negative test, providing asymmetric evidentiary weight.

## 4. Katzenstein on Small States: A Category Error?

This is the paper's most serious theoretical misapplication.

Katzenstein (1985) examined small European industrial states (Austria, Belgium, Denmark, Switzerland, the Netherlands) embedded in a hegemonic order (NATO/Cold War). His argument: small states cannot contest structural power independently; they bandwagon with the hegemon and gain security rents through subordination. The institutional mechanism is **alliance-dependent monetary subordination**.

India is a large (1.4B population), rising military power, nuclear-armed, with strategic autonomy and non-alignment tradition. Katzenstein's theoretical logic does not apply to India's structural position. The paper does not argue India is small; it argues India's non-alignment structurally *enables* independent leverage, unlike Australia/Canada under AUKUS.

This is correct analytically. But it is **not Katzenstein's argument**. The paper repurposes Katzenstein's concept of alliance-dependent monetary subordination. This is not applying Katzenstein; it is inverting him. The paper should cite the actual theoretical source for this claim — Norrlof (2010, 2020) on security premiums, which the paper already cites — and restructure the filter framing accordingly.

**Assessment:** The analytical point is sound (allied currencies are niche-bound). The theoretical attribution is incorrect. This is more than terminology — IO reviewers will flag it.

## 5. What the Geoeconomics Field Will Say

The hostile review will come from four vectors:

**Vector 1 — The USA-driven quantitative finding (§6.8).** All forward predictions rest on a single-country structural relationship. The US β=84.0 is from ARDL with only 30 observations. Dropping the US reverses the coefficient to −3. The lag parameter λ is estimated from the US shale case alone, then applied globally.

**Vector 2 — The India prediction is overdetermined.** The transmission chain (§7.5) requires simultaneous unlocking of three binding constraints. A reviewer will ask: Why should we believe this particular configuration of changes will occur together?

**Vector 3 — Thorium specificity is not theoretically derived.** The four properties (§7.1) read post-hoc as a model of thorium from current technological realities. Uranium concentrates in Kazakhstan, Niger, Australia — geographic concentration ✓. Nuclear fuel cycle requires sovereign capacity — governance requirement ✓. But so does lithium refining. The argument that thorium specifically must regenerate the finance-energy mechanism is contingent on Indian AHWR success, not mechanism-driven.

**Vector 4 — The backward register proves political construction, not monetary consequence.** The EU ETS's GS of 2.79 shows carbon governance is politically volatile. It does not show that carbon price volatility affected monetary outcomes. §5.4 explicitly disclaims this. But then why lead with the ETS as evidence for the mechanism?

**Likely reception:** Respectful but skeptical. Reviewers will praise the falsifiable predictions and Japan natural experiment logic. They will flag the USA-driven panel finding, the Katzenstein category error, and the gap between "political construction proven" and "energy-monetary mechanism asserted."

## 6. Top Three Geoeconomics-Specific Problems

**1. The security-energy nexus is undertheorized.** The mechanism by which energy sovereignty enables monetary autonomy *independent of* security commitments is asserted, not derived. India's nuclear capacity is currently security-dependent (Russian uranium, French technology). The paper needs explicit theorisation of the security-energy-monetary nexus.

**2. Governance Sensitivity is measured at regime collapse, not regime operation.** GS=2.79 comes from EU ETS Phase I's near-total price collapse when over-allocation was revealed. This is a single discontinuous event (May 2006). The paper does not measure GS under normal operation — how politically constructed carbon prices are during ordinary allocation cycles. The backward register measures a moment of crisis, not baseline political construction.

**3. The three-channel taxonomy is not derived.** The sequencing (Channel 3→Channel 2→Channel 1) is specific to India and described as applying to "states with closed capital accounts." For the US, all three operated simultaneously. For the eurozone, none operates to current advantage. The paper is fitting the channel sequence to each case post-hoc rather than deriving it from theoretical principles.

---

**Recommendation for resubmission:**
1. Drop the Katzenstein attribution or expand the security-energy theoretical foundation substantially.
2. Reconstruct the backward register to measure GS under normal regime operation, not crisis collapse.
3. Add a section on the security-energy interaction — how alliance structures condition whether energy position produces independent monetary leverage.
4. Either add a second robust case study to validate β, or reframe as a US natural experiment generating forward hypotheses.

*The India prediction is the paper's best bet for field impact. It is specific, dated, testable, and generated from a theoretically novel mechanism. Lead with that. Subordinate the panel quantification.*
