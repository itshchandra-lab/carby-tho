## 1. Theory of Change: Scope Conditions Under-Specified

The paper's core mechanism reads: energy position → governance regime choice → monetary power, with λ≈10 years of institutional lag. This is clear. What is *not* clear is the scope condition. The mechanism apparently holds for the USA, the eurozone, and Japan. But it fails for Switzerland (zero energy position; high reserve share; "institutional substitution" cited) and China (near-zero energy position at world scale; gradual renminbi internationalisation via capital account liberalisation).

The boundary conditions in §6.4 do not actually specify the scope condition precisely. What the paper describes as "institutional substitution" for Switzerland is really a claim that the mechanism is silenced by something else. But the paper does not state: "The mechanism operates only when X is true."

For an IO audience: falsification requires a precise scope condition stated in advance. The paper should state: "The mechanism activates where (a) energy endowment is material to current account balance, (b) governance regimes are politically contestable, and (c) institutional substitutes like offshore financial centres do not operate." The reverse — the scope condition violation that falsifies the mechanism itself, not just the predictions — must also be stated.

---

## 2. MPI as Index vs. Theory: Conceptual Incoherence

This is the paper's most serious analytical problem.

The MPI is presented as a "framework that formalises the relationship between energy sovereignty and monetary power." But a *theory* generates predictions from a causal mechanism. An *index* measures something.

The MPI equation is an index: it combines thorium reserves, nuclear capacity, institutional quality, and a transition probability into a score. This is measurement. It produces rankings. Rankings are not predictions.

But the paper uses the MPI to *generate predictions*: "India's position rises discontinuously as the energy transition progresses, a prediction no present-era framework produces." This is theory work, not measurement work.

The paper conflates these. §3 presents MPI as a framework; §7 uses it as if it were a causal theory. The India prediction does not follow from the MPI equation alone. It follows from:
1. The three transmission channels (§3.4)
2. The sequencing logic (§7.5)
3. The Katzenstein/Norrlof filter (§7.4)
4. The BIS 2028 binding constraints (§7.5)

These are theoretical claims separate from the MPI index. The paper should explicitly state: **"The MPI is a measurement tool. The theory consists of three causal mechanisms operating under specific institutional conditions with specific sequencing requirements. The MPI guides which states to examine; the transmission chain generates the prediction."**

Without this clarification, IO reviewers will ask: why multiply all three terms together? Why not estimate the coefficients empirically? Why assume multiplicative collapse at zero? The paper's answer — that it captures option-value — is intuitive but undermines the claim to structural theory. Option-value models in finance use option-pricing formulas precisely *because* multiplying terms together is incorrect. The paper borrows financial metaphor but not financial rigour.

## 3. Hegemonic Stability Theory: Strategic Omissions

The paper engages Kindleberger, Gilpin (1981), and Strange (1988). It does not engage:

**Gilpin (1987)** *Political Economy of International Relations* — Gilpin's theory of hegemonic cycles where rising powers systematically challenge incumbent orders. The paper's energy mechanism is fundamentally a hegemonic transition story; Gilpin 1987 provides the structural theory for why rising energy endowments trigger order contestation. Citing only Gilpin (1981) on war and change misses the explicitly monetary-order argument in 1987. This is a significant omission.

**Modelski's long-cycle theory** — The paper's three energy eras (coal → oil → thorium) map onto Modelski's long cycles. Modelski's framework explains *why* energy transitions correlate with hegemonic transitions: they shift the technological basis of military power, which redistributes geopolitical capability, which enables monetary challengers. The paper treats this as novel but it is continuous with Modelski.

**Cox (1981, 1983)** — The Gramscian IPE tradition. Cox's historical-structure approach — the way production relations condition interstate relations which condition world orders — is precisely the framework the paper is using: carbon-era production relations conditioned a dollar world order; thorium-era relations will condition something else. Cox provides the theoretical justification for why energy transitions matter as structural shifts in the conditions of hegemony, not merely as exogenous shocks.

For an IO submission: the paper must engage these traditions as constitutive of its theoretical claim, not as background. The energy mechanism is not a new discovery — it is a specification of hegemonic transition mechanisms that strategic omission obscures.

## 4. Falsification Logic: Precision Breakdown

§8.3's falsification registry is admirable in spirit but breaks down under scrutiny.

The India prediction reads: "INR FX turnover share rises above 2.5%, conditional on nuclear capacity growth AND measurable capital account easing." The falsification condition reads: "INR share flat or declining despite nuclear additions and capital account easing."

**Problem 1:** What if INR share rises to 2.3%? The registry does not specify whether 2.3% constitutes mechanism success or failure.

**Problem 2:** The paper says if capital account remains closed, "the transmission chain stalls at channel 3 and the 2031 prediction is *delayed rather than falsified*." This is a retreat. Either:
- The sequential transmission chain is a *necessary theoretical claim* — in which case capital account closure *falsifies* the 2031 prediction, full stop.
- Or the institutional conditions are *external constraints* — in which case the India prediction becomes conditional on India's institutional choices, not generated by the mechanism itself.

The paper cannot have it both ways. Either the mechanism is falsified when its necessary conditions are absent, or the prediction is not a test of the mechanism at all.

For an IO audience: falsification requires accepting being wrong about the *core claim*, not just wrong about the timeline.

## 5. Multipolarity and the Farhi-Maggiori Problem

§2.5 acknowledges Farhi-Maggiori's result that multiple reserve currencies may produce worse equilibria than a single dominant currency due to speculative flows. The paper proposes a "layered" multipolar system citing Pforr et al. (2025). But it does not engage the instability implications.

If Farhi-Maggiori is correct, the paper's forward prediction — India's INR gaining reserve share — describes a move toward an inherently unstable equilibrium. The question the paper does not answer: under what conditions does a multipolar order stabilise rather than producing the Farhi-Maggiori outcome of competitive devaluation and capital flight?

The paper invokes "governance" (§8.1) but does not explain what governance structure prevents the Farhi-Maggiori instability trap. This is a critical gap for a paper predicting the thorium era as a multi-reserve-currency world.

---

## 6. Top Three Problems for Revision

**1. State the scope condition precisely** — under what conditions does energy governance determine monetary power, and what violation falsifies the mechanism (not just a prediction)? The Switzerland and China boundary conditions are currently post-hoc explanations, not falsifiable predictions about the mechanism's limits.

**2. Separate the index from the theory** — the MPI should be presented as a tool for identifying structural candidates; the transmission channels and sequencing logic constitute the theory; make this division explicit in §3. This is not cosmetic — it changes how every subsequent claim is evaluated by an IO referee.

**3. Clarify the falsification logic** — if the mechanism requires sequential channel activation and institutional conditions, the failure of institutional conditions must either (a) falsify the prediction or (b) confirm the mechanism predicted correctly that without those conditions, the prediction wouldn't hold. The paper currently uses (b) as a defence against (a), which is not logically permissible within a falsificationist framework.

---

**Additional recommendation:** Engage Gilpin (1987), Cox (1981), and Modelski seriously in §2. The paper is making a hegemonic transition argument; it must locate itself within that tradition before claiming to extend it.

---

*Final assessment: The paper makes a genuine contribution to understanding hegemonic transitions through energy structures. The falsification registry is a genuine methodological contribution to IR. The Japan natural experiment and EU ETS backward register are well-executed. With the revisions above, this could reach IO. In its current form, the analytical ambiguities — scope condition, index/theory conflation, falsification logic — prevent acceptance.*
