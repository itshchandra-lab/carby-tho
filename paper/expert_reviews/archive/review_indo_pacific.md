# Peer Review — Indo-Pacific Strategic Studies Expert
## "The Carbon-Thorium Standard: Energy-Backed Currencies and the Architecture of Monetary Failure in a Multipolar World"

**Reviewer specialisation:** Indo-Pacific security and political economy, Quad dynamics, ASEAN monetary architecture, China maritime strategy, Japan-US alliance, AUKUS
**Journals of comparable standard:** *Asian Security* / *International Security* (Pacific Affairs section)
**Recommendation:** Major Revisions
**Score:** 5/10

---

## Summary Assessment

This paper makes an ambitious and, in places, genuinely stimulating argument: that energy sovereignty is the missing causal mechanism in reserve currency theory, and that the MPI framework can predict India's monetary rise as its thorium transition matures. The Fukushima natural experiment is the paper's strongest empirical contribution. However, the paper's engagement with Indo-Pacific strategic architecture is inadequate for a paper making large-scale predictions about the monetary futures of Japan, India, China, and Australia.

The paper treats these states as if they exist in a strategic vacuum — as if energy positions are determined by geology and fuel mix alone, and as if alliance architecture, maritime chokepoints, contested territories, and the security competition for energy transit routes are exogenous noise rather than constitutive of the energy-monetary relationship the paper is trying to theorise. I recommend **Major Revisions**. The core theoretical contribution is worth preserving, but the Indo-Pacific geopolitical architecture must be substantially integrated before the paper's predictions can be considered credible.

---

## 1. The Quad and India's Non-Alignment: Does the Katzenstein Filter Survive Quad-Era India-US Relations?

The paper's India prediction rests on a Katzenstein filter argument that India's strategic autonomy / non-alignment allows its energy leverage to "escape" the dollar routing that constrains Japan and the UK. This is the load-bearing architectural claim for the paper's most striking prediction, and it is insufficiently defended.

The problem: the paper treats Indian strategic autonomy as a stable structural property when it is, in fact, contested and evolving. India's Quad membership — alongside the United States, Japan, and Australia — represents precisely the kind of alignment deepening that the non-alignment doctrine was designed to avoid. The Quad is not merely a talking shop. The 2021 Quad Leaders' Summit, the 2023 Modi state visit to Washington, the iCET (initiative on Critical and Emerging Technology), the GE F414 engine co-production agreement, the P-8I Poseidon maritime patrol aircraft procurement, and the ongoing Malabar naval exercise series together constitute a defence technology integration trajectory that places India structurally closer to the US security architecture than at any point since 1947.

The paper must confront a direct question: if Katzenstein's (1985) argument is that states embedded in dense alliance networks channel their economic strategies through the dominant power's financial architecture, does Quad membership place India in the same structural category as Japan? The paper says no — implicitly — but never argues why. The distinction the paper appears to rely upon (India has domestic energy resources; Japan does not) is real but does not resolve the strategic autonomy question. Japan also had advanced civilian nuclear capability and yet the paper correctly argues that post-Fukushima energy dependence locked Japan into dollar subordination. The causal mechanism is not geology alone; it is the interaction between energy dependence and alliance structure.

The paper needs to engage with this tension. Ganguly and Pardesi (2009) provide the canonical analysis of India's strategic autonomy doctrine evolution. My recommendation: either (a) argue explicitly that Quad membership represents deep alignment without monetary alignment — making a genuine theoretical contribution about the decoupling of security and monetary subordination — or (b) revise the India prediction downward to reflect the security-monetary integration trajectory that Quad membership implies.

The current treatment, which acknowledges neither the Quad nor the iCET nor the 2023 state visit outcomes, is not defensible for a paper published in 2026.

## 2. The Malacca Dilemma and China's NEP: Strategic Vulnerability Invisible to the Formula

The paper's treatment of China's energy position is, from an Indo-Pacific security perspective, the single most consequential gap. The paper assigns China a Net Energy Position approximately equal to zero, reflecting coal self-sufficiency offsetting oil import dependence. The formulaic result — that China's energy position is roughly neutral — produces a TMPI of 4.2 in the thorium scenario. But this calculation treats China's energy imports as if they were equivalent in strategic terms to domestic production. They are not.

China imports approximately 10–11 million barrels of oil per day, of which roughly 75–80% transits the **Strait of Malacca** — a waterway approximately 800 kilometres long and, at its narrowest point (the Phillips Channel), just 2.7 kilometres wide. The Strait is bordered by Singapore (US-aligned), Malaysia, and Indonesia. The United States Seventh Fleet maintains the capability to interdict Malacca transit in a conflict scenario.

This is not a theoretical vulnerability. The PLAN has built its entire blue-water expansion programme around the operational imperative of either protecting Malacca transit or developing credible alternatives. The "Malacca Dilemma" (attributed to Hu Jintao in 2003) drives the BRI's maritime routing: CPEC and Gwadar port, the China-Myanmar Economic Corridor and Kyaukpyu port, and the "String of Pearls" port investments all represent attempts to either bypass Malacca or develop alternative energy import routes. Yoshihara and Holmes (2010) — regrettably absent from the paper's bibliography — argued that China's PLAN expansion is best understood as the strategic consequence of energy insecurity at sea.

The paper's NEP formula — production share minus import share — cannot capture this. A state that depends on a single chokepoint for all seaborne imports is not in the same strategic position as a state that produces the same share domestically across diversified routes. The formula treats geographic and institutional vulnerability as irrelevant to energy position. This is a fundamental modelling error for a paper making Indo-Pacific security predictions.

**Two implications the paper must address:**

First, if China's energy position is less secure than the NEP formula suggests — because of Malacca vulnerability — then China's MPI score overstates its monetary resilience. A China that is one US-aligned Malacca interdiction away from an economic crisis is not a plausible challenger to dollar dominance in the way the paper implicitly frames it.

Second, the paper predicts Chinese monetary influence will be curtailed in the thorium era. The mechanism it offers is the yuan's institutional immaturity. The Malacca Dilemma offers an alternative, and arguably prior, mechanism: China cannot achieve genuine monetary independence while its physical energy supply is strategically vulnerable to the power whose monetary dominance it seeks to challenge.

The BRI's monetary implications are also absent. China's approach to yuan internationalisation has been systematically geographic: BRI projects are denominated in yuan, settled through CIPS, and anchored in bilateral currency swap agreements. In Southeast Asia, this has produced real yuan circulation in Myanmar, Cambodia, and Laos. The paper's framework for predicting reserve currency trajectories should have something to say about whether BRI-driven yuan circulation in energy-producing regions constitutes an alternative pathway to monetary influence that bypasses the energy-sovereignty mechanism the paper theorises.

## 3. ASEAN and Regional Monetary Architecture: The Missing Middle of the Indo-Pacific

The paper moves from global aggregate (reserve currency shares) to bilateral case studies without any engagement with the regional monetary architecture that sits between these levels. ASEAN is a $3.6 trillion collective economic bloc, the world's fourth-largest trading entity, and the geographic locus of the energy-transit chokepoints central to the strategic logic the paper partially describes.

The Chiang Mai Initiative Multilateralisation (CMIM) — a $240 billion regional financial safety net established in 2010 — and its surveillance institution, AMRO (ASEAN+3 Macroeconomic Research Office), represent precisely the kind of "layering" financial architecture that the paper predicts for the INR without explaining how it would interact with an already-existing regional alternative.

The relevant question: the paper predicts INR "layering" in the Indo-Pacific monetary order. But layering against what baseline? If the relevant alternative to dollar dependence in Southeast Asia is already the CMIM/AMRO architecture — denominated primarily in yen and yuan swaps — then India's entry into Indo-Pacific monetary competition is not against the dollar alone but against an existing regional architecture that Japan and China have already partially built. The paper's bilateral framework cannot model this three-way competition.

The ASEAN dimension also matters for India's Act East Policy, which explicitly targets ASEAN as the primary arena for Indian economic and strategic expansion. The Rupee Payment Mechanism (RPM), which India has extended to several ASEAN states following the 2022 Russia sanctions episode, represents the primary empirical evidence for INR internationalisation that the paper might cite but does not engage with adequately. Whether the RPM constitutes genuine monetary layering or merely bilateral payment workarounds for sanctions evasion is a live empirical question the paper should address.

## 4. South China Sea as Energy-Monetary Flashpoint: Contested Territory and the NEP Formula

The paper's energy position calculations assume that a state's energy position is determined by its current production and import profile. But energy territories are contested, and the South China Sea contest is in part a contest over energy resources. The US EIA estimates 11 billion barrels of proven and probable oil reserves and approximately 190 trillion cubic feet of natural gas in the South China Sea.

The theoretical question: what happens to China's NEP if it achieves effective resource sovereignty over South China Sea hydrocarbons? The paper's framework should be able to model this scenario. Does this change the TMPI trajectory? Does it change the monetary competition prediction? The paper should either address this scenario or explain why it is excluded from the analytical framework.

There is a deeper issue about contested versus sovereign energy resources. For China, Japan (East China Sea gas disputes), and India (Andaman Sea, IOR energy transit), energy positions are in part the subject of ongoing strategic competition. A framework that treats energy positions as exogenous inputs to monetary prediction, rather than as outcomes of strategic competition that is itself partially monetary, is missing a feedback loop central to Indo-Pacific political economy.

If China completes effective military control over the South China Sea, the energy-security calculus for Vietnam, the Philippines, Malaysia, Brunei, and Taiwan changes fundamentally. These changes would affect the monetary preferences of states in the region in ways that the paper's framework would predict but does not model.

## 5. India-China Competition in the Indian Ocean: Security Constraints on India's Nuclear Sovereignty

The paper's India prediction requires a sovereign nuclear fuel cycle — the three-stage programme reaching commercial scale, operating outside the safeguards regime that governs the nuclear fuel cycles of US allies. This is a real strategic possibility. It is also a strategically contested one.

The Indian Ocean is the primary theatre of India-China strategic competition, directly relevant to India's nuclear energy development. China's "String of Pearls" — port investments at Gwadar (Pakistan), Hambantota (Sri Lanka), Kyaukpyu (Myanmar), and Djibouti — is treated by Indian strategic planners as encirclement. Whether this constitutes a genuine encirclement threat or a series of commercial investments that India has overread is debated, but the Indian strategic community treats the maritime dimension of Indian Ocean competition as the primary security constraint on India's strategic autonomy.

This matters for the paper's prediction because India's nuclear energy programme — particularly the fast breeder reactor programme at Kalpakkam — depends on sustained domestic political commitment and continued exclusion from international safeguards. The strategic pressure on India's nuclear programme is not purely technical; it is also diplomatic and security-driven. The more India integrates into the Quad security architecture, the more it faces pressure from the US non-proliferation establishment to bring its civilian programme into the IAEA framework. A safeguarded thorium programme is, by definition, not a sovereign fuel cycle of the kind the paper's prediction requires. The paper does not model this constraint.

Furthermore, the paper predicts India rises and China is marginalised in the thorium era. But the paper does not model what China does in response. China's leverage over India in the thorium transition is considerable: China controls approximately 80% of global rare earth production, including elements relevant to thorium processing. China's infrastructure investments in India's peripheral states give it diplomatic leverage. The paper treats India's rise as a process that unfolds in a permissive strategic environment. The Indo-Pacific security architecture suggests the environment will be actively contested.

## 6. Japan and the Kishida Doctrine: Does the Security Posture Shift Matter for Monetary Trajectory?

The paper's Japan analysis is empirically its strongest section. The Fukushima natural experiment is well-designed. The forward prediction (yen reserve share recovery tracking nuclear restart) follows logically.

However, the paper does not engage with the Kishida doctrine — the 2022 National Security Strategy that doubled Japan's defence budget, acquired counterstrike capabilities, and repositioned Japan as an active contributor to regional security rather than a passive beneficiary of US deterrence. This is a major omission.

The Kishida doctrine has several energy-monetary implications:

**First**, Japan's enhanced security posture gives it greater diplomatic leverage in energy negotiations. The energy partnerships Japan has developed with Australia (LNG, hydrogen, ammonia), with Southeast Asia (AZEC, Asia Zero Emission Community), and with India (nuclear energy cooperation discussions) are partly downstream of this enhanced security standing.

**Second**, Japan's expanded role in Indo-Pacific security architecture gives it instruments to shape the energy-monetary environment. Japan's strategic presence in the South China Sea (freedom of navigation operations, coast guard cooperation with Southeast Asian states) is partly about securing the maritime energy routes that the paper correctly identifies as the source of Japan's energy vulnerability.

**Third**, the Kishida doctrine's emphasis on defence technology and industrial capacity — including potential nuclear technology exports, subject to constitutional constraints — gives Japan a new dimension of energy-monetary influence.

The paper's prediction that yen reserve share will recover as nuclear restarts proceed may well be correct. But the mechanism is likely more complex than the paper's framework suggests: it is not merely that domestic nuclear production reduces energy dependence, but that enhanced security standing creates diplomatic leverage that translates into energy partnerships that reduce import vulnerability across multiple fuel types.

## 7. AUKUS and the Indo-Pacific Balance: Nuclear Sharing, Precedent, and Dollar Architecture

On the question of whether AUKUS nuclear sharing creates precedents affecting the Katzenstein filter for other Indo-Pacific states: AUKUS represents the first transfer of nuclear-powered submarine technology to a non-nuclear weapons state. The political logic required to make this legally sustainable under the NPT — Australia accepting extensive safeguards and transparency commitments in exchange for the technology — creates a precedent that cuts in two directions. An AUKUS-style nuclear deal for Japan would reinforce rather than reduce its monetary subordination, because it deepens the institutional entanglement with the US nonproliferation architecture that constrains Japan's sovereign nuclear options.

On whether AUKUS changes the energy-security calculus for India: AUKUS was designed in part as a response to Chinese naval expansion, and India's response to AUKUS has been cautiously positive despite formal non-alignment. India benefits from AUKUS in strategic terms — greater US and Australian naval presence in the Indian Ocean reduces the pressure India faces from Chinese maritime expansion — without bearing the sovereignty costs of formal membership. This is, in fact, a neat illustration of why India's non-alignment doctrine has strategic value: India extracts security benefits from the AUKUS architecture while preserving the institutional distance that the paper's Katzenstein filter requires. **The paper should make this argument explicitly rather than leaving it implicit or unaddressed.**

On whether AUKUS reinforces dollar dominance: the paper's implied prediction (yes) is probably correct. AUKUS deepens the financial and technological dependence of Australia and (indirectly) Japan on US military industrial capacity. Financing a nuclear-powered submarine programme creates long-duration, dollar-denominated financial obligations at sovereign scale. The dollar-denominated nature of US defence industrial contracts is a specific and underexplored mechanism of dollar dominance that the paper's framework could illuminate but does not.

## 8. Indo-Pacific Energy Supply Chains and the Multilateral Dimension

The Quad has explicitly developed a clean energy supply chain agenda. The Quad Climate and Clean Energy Agenda, the Quad Critical Minerals Dialogue, and the Indo-Pacific Economic Framework for Prosperity (IPEF, launched 2022 with fourteen member states) represent attempts to build non-Chinese clean energy supply chains across the Indo-Pacific. Japan has pursued hydrogen and ammonia imports from Australia under the bilateral Clean Hydrogen Supply Chain framework.

The monetary implications are directly relevant. If the Quad succeeds in building a non-Chinese clean energy supply chain, it potentially creates a multilateral energy trade architecture denominated in Quad currencies — dollars, yen, and potentially rupees. This would be a significant driver of INR internationalisation that is distinct from, and potentially more powerful than, the bilateral energy sovereignty mechanism the paper theorises.

The **critical minerals dimension** is particularly important and entirely absent from the paper. Thorium fuel cycle development requires not just thorium (which India has in abundance) but also rare earth processing, zirconium, hafnium, and advanced materials currently dominated by Chinese supply chains. A paper predicting India's energy sovereignty through the thorium cycle must address whether India can indigenise these supply chains or whether it will remain dependent on Chinese-controlled critical minerals processing — a dependence that would represent a structural constraint on exactly the sovereign fuel cycle the prediction requires.

## 9. Missing Literature

**Medcalf, Rory (2020). *Contest for the Indo-Pacific.* La Trobe University Press.** — The defining scholarly treatment of Indo-Pacific strategic geography. Foundational for any paper making large-scale predictions about Indo-Pacific monetary competition.

**Yoshihara, Toshi, and James R. Holmes (2010). *Red Star over the Pacific.* Naval Institute Press.** — The canonical analysis of PLAN expansion and its energy security motivation. Essential for the paper's treatment of China's NEP and the Malacca Dilemma.

**Ganguly, Sumit, and Manjeet S. Pardesi (2009). "Explaining Sixty Years of India's Foreign Policy." *India Review* 8(1): 4–19.** — The most rigorous treatment of India's strategic autonomy doctrine evolution. Essential for the paper's Katzenstein filter argument.

**Kausikan, Bilahari (2017). *Dealing with an Ambiguous World.* World Scientific.** — ASEAN's strategic hedging between US and Chinese power, directly relevant to the "layering" prediction and regional monetary architecture.

## 10. Overall Verdict

**Score: 5/10. Recommendation: Major Revisions.**

The paper makes a theoretically serious argument and the Fukushima natural experiment is a genuine empirical contribution. The MPI framework is creative and the thorium transition logic is intellectually stimulating.

However, the paper's Indo-Pacific geopolitical architecture is insufficiently developed to support the specific predictions it makes. The India non-alignment argument does not survive contact with Quad-era India-US relations as currently written. China's NEP calculation misses the Malacca Dilemma, which is the foundational strategic fact of Chinese grand strategy. The paper is entirely silent on ASEAN and regional monetary architecture. The South China Sea energy dimension is unaddressed. Japan's Kishida doctrine is not engaged with. The multilateral clean energy supply chain architecture reshaping Indo-Pacific energy relationships is absent. The critical minerals dimension is absent.

**Revisions should focus on:**
1. A genuinely theorised treatment of Quad membership and India's non-alignment claim
2. A revised NEP formula or supplementary index that captures strategic energy vulnerability (chokepoint exposure, route diversification, interoperability with adversary interdiction capability) alongside fuel mix
3. A section on ASEAN and regional monetary architecture as the existing "layering" architecture against which the INR prediction must be measured
4. Engagement with the Kishida doctrine for the Japan analysis
5. Incorporation of the critical minerals and clean energy supply chain dimension into the thorium fuel cycle sovereignty argument

With these revisions, the paper could make a substantial contribution. Without them, the Indo-Pacific security predictions are not credible.
