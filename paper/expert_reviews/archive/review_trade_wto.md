## 1. Trade Invoicing and the Gopinath Misapplication

The paper cites Gopinath et al. (2020) to claim that controlling "the dominant energy input" permits invoicing in domestic currency (§3.4). This is inaccurate on the empirical fact.

Gopinath's Dominant Currency Paradigm documents that USD invoicing in non-energy trade persists even when the US is *not* the importer — a puzzle the theory explains through network effects and pricing power. The 2020 study covers manufactured goods, agricultural trade, and financial instruments, explicitly finding that "the use of different currencies is not symmetric, and the dollar overwhelmingly dominates." For energy specifically, the evidence is inverted: OPEC oil was priced in dollars because *Saudi Arabia made a political choice in 1974*, not because controlling the commodity automatically grants invoicing power. The arrangement required US security guarantees and petrodollar recycling corridors.

Today's energy invoicing shows this dependency clearly: LNG is increasingly priced in other currencies (euro contracts dominate, Chinese importers negotiate yuan agreements), yet there is no corresponding shift in reserve currency composition. The paper treats invoicing as a *mechanism* for reserve currency acquisition; the actual evidence suggests invoicing norms are *outcomes* of prior monetary dominance, not origins. **The causal arrow runs from reserve status to invoicing capacity, not the reverse.**

For India, this reverses the binding constraint. The paper predicts INR invoicing will emerge after partial capital account opening (§7.5), but empirically, opening capital accounts does not create invoicing demand absent prior monetary credibility.

## 2. WTO Article XVII and the Thorium Trade Architecture

The paper's India prediction rests on "partial capital account liberalisation" becoming "politically viable" as nuclear capacity grows (§7.5). This is silent on the legal regime constraining such liberalisation.

India is bound by IMF Article VIII §2(a) restrictions on current-account convertibility and retains extensive Article XIV transitional authorisations (unchanged since 1993). Full capital account liberalisation would require either (a) formal IMF Article VIII consultation and removal of exchange restrictions, or (b) negotiation of new bilateral swap lines that explicitly permit off-market transactions. Neither is automatic.

More critically: **WTO GATT Article XVII** governs state trading enterprises and requires non-discriminatory treatment of foreign suppliers in state-monopoly markets. If India's energy sector (Nuclear Power Corporation, State Power Utilities) enters thorium-trade invoicing arrangements, it may be required to offer equivalent terms to third-country suppliers. A bilateral India-Germany or India-Japan thorium arrangement invoiced in rupees would trigger Article XVII scrutiny under WTO dispute settlement.

The Energy Charter Treaty, which India has not signed, would not shield such arrangements. India cannot unilaterally establish "thorium-era invoicing norms" through bilateral arrangement without either WTO litigation or accession to treaty regimes that subordinate energy trade to non-discrimination rules.

**The paper ignores this entirely.** This is not a minor gap — it is the primary legal architecture governing the exact institutional change the India prediction requires.

## 3. Reserve Currency and COFER-vs-Turnover Conflation

The paper conflates IMF COFER (official central bank reserves) with BIS FX turnover (private market transactions). These are mechanically distinct.

**FX turnover** is driven by trade settlement velocity and financial depth (speculative trading). **Reserve currency allocation** is driven by central bank risk management and geopolitical alignment. A state can have large FX turnover (China's renminbi at ~8–10% of BIS volume) but minimal COFER holdings (~2–3%). The paper's evidence for the present register comes entirely from COFER (IMF data), but the forward prediction is keyed to BIS turnover — different institutional actors, different constraints, different dynamics.

For India specifically: even if capital account opening occurs and nuclear capacity rises, achieving 3–5% BIS turnover share would require either (a) major commodity importers settling thorium purchases in rupees (low probability given established euro/USD conventions in energy markets), or (b) speculative financial trading in rupees requiring deep derivatives markets. India's offshore rupee (NDF) market is thin relative to its trade size. Scaling it to 3–5% global share would take far longer than the 2031 horizon absent enormous policy coordination the paper does not model.

## 4. EU ETS, CBAM, and WTO Consistency

The backward register's GS claim — that EU ETS governance is "politically constructed" — is accurate as description but raises a WTO dimension the paper ignores.

The EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM), implemented January 2024, is actively under challenge for GATT Article I (most-favoured-nation) and Article III (national treatment) violations. Panel decisions are pending. If CBAM is struck down or substantially reformed, the governance regime (GS) collapses — not because energy governance becomes less credible, but because trade law constrains carbon infrastructure.

The paper treats GS as endogenous to state choice; WTO law makes it partially exogenous. A state can *want* to construct politically durable carbon governance but be prevented by WTO obligations from doing so. This constraint is entirely absent from the paper's analysis of what makes governance "politically constructed."

## 5. India's Capital Account: IMF Article VIII Reality

The paper acknowledges India's KAOPEN = −1.254 and makes partial liberalisation a prediction condition for 2028. But it does not ask: under what mechanism?

Major liberalisation — permitting non-resident rupee accounts, offshore rupee trading, FDI repatriation — requires:

1. IMF consultation (creating political economy pressure against rapid change)
2. Amendment of India's 2022 Foreign Exchange Management Act (FEMA), which embeds capital controls legislatively
3. Coordination with RBI monetary transmission framework (opening channels creates inflation risk in a dual-deficit economy)

Indian policymakers have *resisted* capital account opening even during current account improvement (2003–2008, pre-GFC). The political feasibility is not automatic — it requires explicit reform legislation, not just favourable economic conditions.

---

## 6. Three Binding Trade-Law Problems

**(a) Energy invoicing is downstream, not upstream, of reserve status.** Controls on dominant commodities do not automatically confer invoicing power — they must be embedded in prior monetary dominance or security guarantees. India lacks both for thorium.

**(b) Bilateral thorium trade is constrained by WTO non-discrimination law.** India moving to domestic-currency invoicing for thorium triggers GATT Article XVII scrutiny for state trading enterprises and Article I liability if equivalent terms are not offered universally. The paper does not engage with this.

**(c) Reserve currency shifts depend on central bank behaviour, not private FX turnover.** Scaling INR FX turnover to 3–5% is mechanically possible; scaling COFER reserves is a separate political and legal problem requiring geopolitical realignment by central banks. The energy mechanism predicts the *environment* for transition (credibility, reduced vulnerability); it does not predict central bank *decisions*.

---

**Verdict:** The energy-governance link is plausible and well-evidenced in the backward and present registers. The forward register's India prediction, however, rests on underestimating: (a) the invoicing literature's causal direction, (b) WTO constraints on bilateral energy trade, (c) IMF Article VIII commitments limiting unilateral capital account opening, and (d) the distinction between private FX markets and official reserve holdings.

The paper would benefit from explicit engagement with Article VIII, GATT XVII, and Energy Charter Treaty constraints — not to falsify the thesis, but to specify which legal architecture must change for the prediction to hold. These should appear in the falsification registry as necessary preconditions.
