## Critical Findings

### 1. Article VIII vs Capital Account Openness — Legal Error

**Finding:** The paper conflates IMF Article VIII (current account convertibility) with capital account openness. These are legally and operationally distinct.

**India's status:** India accepted IMF Article VIII status in **August 1994**, meaning India's currency is fully convertible for current account transactions (trade, services, income). This is not in dispute and has been in force for three decades.

**What the paper means:** The binding constraint the paper is describing — KAOPEN = −1.254, capital controls under FEMA — refers to **capital account** restrictions, not current account convertibility. India's capital account remains under domestic policy control through the Foreign Exchange Management Act (2002) and RBI administrative controls.

**Required correction:** Remove any language that implies India lacks Article VIII status or that "Article VIII liberalisation" is a pending milestone. Replace with: "capital account liberalisation (Chinn-Ito KAOPEN)" throughout. The distinction matters legally: Article VIII is about current account; the paper's argument is entirely about capital account.

**Note:** The paper's current text (as of v3) does not appear to use "Article VIII" explicitly — but any future revision should maintain this distinction clearly.

---

### 2. COFER 2016 Structural Break — Data Integrity Issue

**Finding:** China began reporting to IMF COFER in **2016 Q4**. Prior to this, China's renminbi was either absent from or significantly underrepresented in COFER data.

**Implication:** The apparent rise in CNY reserve allocation in COFER data after 2016 reflects two distinct phenomena:
1. **Reporting inclusion effect**: New demand from previously unreported holders becoming visible
2. **Genuine new reserve demand**: Central banks that had not previously held CNY beginning to do so

These cannot be separated within COFER alone. The paper's any claim about CNY reserve trajectory using COFER data before and after 2016 is comparing incommensurable series.

**Required correction:** Add explicit caveat that CNY COFER data before and after 2016 are not comparable. Analysis of China's reserve currency trajectory should be confined to post-2016 data or use supplementary PBOC data.

---

### 3. COFER vs BIS Turnover Conflation — Methodological Issue

**Finding:** The present register uses IMF COFER data (official reserve holdings) to estimate β=84.0. The forward prediction uses BIS FX turnover share (private foreign exchange markets). These track fundamentally different populations.

**COFER measures:** What official central bank reserve managers hold — determined by reserve adequacy objectives, political alliances, and institutional habits.

**BIS FX turnover measures:** What private market participants trade — determined by commercial invoice patterns, hedging demand, speculative flows, and offshore market liquidity.

**The relationship is imperfect:** GBP has ~4.5% COFER share but ~13% BIS FX share (City of London entrepôt effect). JPY has ~5.7% COFER share but ~8% BIS FX share. USD has ~58.5% COFER share but ~44% BIS FX share.

**Implication for India:** Applying β estimated from COFER to predict BIS FX turnover systematically misstates the relationship. The prediction "INR FX turnover share → 3–5% by BIS 2031" is a BIS outcome, but the mechanism (β) is estimated from a COFER regression.

**Required correction:** Either (a) restate the prediction in COFER terms, using a separate empirical estimate of the COFER → BIS mapping, or (b) explicitly acknowledge the methodological gap and note that β is applied to the BIS outcome as a first approximation pending a direct BIS turnover regression.

---

## Minor Findings

- **SDR basket composition**: The paper does not mention the SDR. India's INR is not in the SDR basket; inclusion would be a significant signal of reserve currency status that could be added to the falsification registry.
- **COFER allocated vs unallocated reserves**: The paper correctly uses allocated COFER reserves but should note that China's reserves were in the "unallocated" category before 2016 — not absent from reserves globally, just from the visible reporting.
- **Reserve composition vs reserve level**: COFER measures shares of allocated reserves, not total reserve demand. A country's COFER share can rise as other currencies' shares fall, even without new INR demand.

## Overall Assessment

The paper's core argument is sound. The technical corrections are specific and fixable. The COFER vs BIS conflation is the most significant methodological concern and should be addressed explicitly in §6.8 or a new methodological note.
