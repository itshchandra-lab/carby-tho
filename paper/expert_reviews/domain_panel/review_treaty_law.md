# Peer Review — Treaty and International Law Expert
## "The Carbon-Thorium Standard: Energy-Backed Currencies and the Architecture of Monetary Failure in a Multipolar World"

**Reviewer specialisation:** International law, treaty studies, NPT, NSG, IMF law, SWIFT legal architecture, CUSMA energy provisions
**Venue standard:** *European Journal of International Law* / *Journal of International Economic Law*
**Recommendation:** Major Revisions
**Score:** 5/10

---

## Prefatory Note

This manuscript addresses a genuine theoretical gap: the relationship between energy governance architecture and reserve currency durability in a multipolar world. The core intuition — that energy sovereignty functions as a structural determinant of monetary credibility — is theoretically productive and the paper assembles an impressive empirical apparatus in support of it.

However, the manuscript proceeds as if international law either does not exist or is merely epiphenomenal to the political arrangements it describes. The paper's central predictions — India's thorium fuel cycle achieving monetary leverage, AUKUS activating Australian nuclear capacity, Russia's monetary corridor surviving financial isolation — rest on assumptions about state freedom of action that are directly constrained by binding treaty obligations. A working paper circulating in political science and economics venues may survive without this engagement; a paper making forward predictions about state behaviour in legally dense domains cannot.

## 1. NPT and India's Thorium Programme

### 1.1 India's Non-NPT Status and IAEA Safeguards

The paper treats India's three-stage nuclear programme as a sovereign pathway to thorium-backed monetary leverage without adequate qualification. India is not a party to the NPT. Its IAEA safeguards agreement is not the standard INFCIRC/153 model. It is INFCIRC/754, negotiated as a consequence of the 2008 US-India Civil Nuclear Agreement (123 Agreement). Under INFCIRC/754, India submitted a list of "civilian" facilities to IAEA safeguards in a voluntary separation plan — but its strategic/military facilities remain entirely outside safeguards jurisdiction.

India's thorium programme (particularly Stage 3, involving the U-233/thorium breeder cycle) is likely classified as strategic under India's internal separation plan. IAEA safeguards may therefore simply not apply to the facilities that will produce the monetary leverage the paper predicts. This is not a criticism of India's programme; it is an observation that the legal architecture creates a zone of sovereign opacity that the paper's Katzenstein filter is unable to interrogate.

### 1.2 NSG Dual-Use Controls and Stage 2 to Stage 3 Progression

The paper's India prediction assumes relatively smooth progression through Bhabha's three-stage cycle. The paper does not mention that the transition from Stage 1 to Stage 2 requires reprocessing technology — specifically, plutonium separation via PUREX or similar processes — and that such technology appears on the NSG Annex 2 dual-use list (INFCIRC/254/Part 2).

India is not a member of the NSG. The 2008 India-specific NSG waiver allowed civilian nuclear cooperation with India but did not resolve India's access to enrichment and reprocessing (ENR) technology. The ENR issue — whether India can obtain foreign ENR technology subject to NSG guidelines — remains contested. The US insisted on ENR restrictions in the 123 Agreement itself (though the final text was ambiguous, as Krepon and Perkovich documented at the time).

For Stage 2 and Stage 3 to develop on the timeline the paper implies, India would need either domestic development of reprocessing technology at scale (currently limited to KARP and PREFRE facilities) or foreign technology transfers that face significant legal and political obstacles.

### 1.3 The 123 Agreement and Non-Alignment

The 123 Agreement contains provisions that constrain India's sovereignty. Under Section 123(a)(2) of the US Atomic Energy Act and the bilateral text, India accepted US consent rights over reprocessing of US-origin nuclear material. The Hyde Act (2006) additionally constrains US cooperation if India is not working toward "full civil nuclear liability" and other non-proliferation conditions.

These are not academic points. They mean India's civil nuclear programme is legally entangled with US consent rights in ways that directly qualify the "non-aligned" energy sovereignty the paper's Katzenstein filter assumes. The paper cannot simultaneously cite India's civil nuclear programme as a source of monetary independence and ignore the legal architecture through which the US retains levers over that programme.

## 2. AUKUS Legal Architecture

### 2.1 The Article III(2) Problem

AUKUS Pillar One involves the transfer of naval nuclear propulsion technology — nuclear-powered submarines using HEU fuel — from the US and UK to Australia, a non-nuclear weapon state (NNWS) under the NPT.

The problem: naval nuclear propulsion reactors using HEU fall into a gap in the IAEA safeguards system. The NPT Article III(1) standard comprehensive safeguards agreement (INFCIRC/153) contains Article 14, allowing nuclear material used in non-proscribed military activities (including naval propulsion) to be withdrawn from safeguards for the duration of such use. This is the "military exemption." AUKUS is the first time a NNWS will operate HEU-fuelled naval reactors under a negotiated Article III(2) arrangement with IAEA oversight.

The IAEA Board of Governors has been negotiating since 2021 what an "appropriate arrangement" under Article III(2) for AUKUS would look like. As of the paper's writing horizon (2026), this negotiation is unresolved. The legal architecture the paper assumes — Australian nuclear capacity activated by AUKUS — is therefore legally incomplete. Critics (including China and Russia) have argued that AUKUS creates a dangerous precedent that could be exploited by other NNWS seeking to acquire weapons-usable nuclear material under a naval propulsion justification.

### 2.2 The Embedding of Australia in the US Nuclear Legal Orbit

AUKUS Pillar One makes Australia's security embedding a matter of binding international legal obligation, not merely political alignment. The modalities agreement (finalised in the San Diego declaration, March 2023) provides that the US and UK will sell submarines to Australia, with US nuclear propulsion technology transferred under the US-Australia Agreement for Cooperation on the Peaceful Uses of Nuclear Energy (Section 123 AEA agreement, updated in 2010). The consent rights structure under this 123 agreement — combined with the HEU tracking obligations that will arise under whatever IAEA Article III(2) arrangement is negotiated — means Australia's naval nuclear programme will require ongoing US consent rights for decades. This is a formal legal architecture of technological dependency.

The paper should acknowledge that its prediction about Australia's nuclear capacity is legally contingent on an IAEA negotiation that was incomplete at the paper's writing horizon.

## 3. SWIFT Exclusion and Sovereign Immunity

### 3.1 The Legal Basis for SWIFT Exclusion

The paper describes Russia's exclusion from SWIFT as demonstrating that "Western monetary and carbon infrastructure is political weaponry, not neutral mechanism." This framing is analytically useful but legally imprecise.

SWIFT is a Belgian cooperative society incorporated under Belgian company law. It operates under oversight of the National Bank of Belgium. The legal mechanism for Russia's exclusion in March 2022 was EU Council Regulation (EU) 2022/345, adopted under the EU's CFSP framework (Articles 29 TEU and 215 TFEU). It prohibited SWIFT from providing services to listed Russian entities. This is EU legislation with extraterritorial effect, not a decision of any international monetary institution.

Russia was not expelled from the IMF; it remains a member. The legal "weaponry" is wielded through EU domestic legislation, and its reach depends on SWIFT's network centrality. This has direct implications for the paper's argument about SWIFT-alternative infrastructure (the ruble-yuan corridor etc.) — the legal basis for exclusion is entirely jurisdiction-specific.

### 3.2 Frozen Russian Central Bank Reserves and Sovereign Immunity

The paper does not engage with the freezing of approximately $300 billion in Russian Central Bank reserves held in Euroclear (Belgium) and other EU/G7 custodians. This omission is significant.

The orthodox position is that central bank assets held abroad are protected by sovereign immunity under customary international law (codified in the UN Convention on Jurisdictional Immunities of States and Their Property (2004) and the US Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act). The freezing of Russian Central Bank reserves — as distinct from the assets of sanctioned individuals — is legally controversial because central bank reserves have traditionally been treated as categorically immune from enforcement jurisdiction.

The G7's subsequent debate about whether to confiscate (not merely freeze) these reserves has exposed a fault line between those who argue they can be confiscated under the law of countermeasures (ILC Articles on State Responsibility, Articles 49-54) and those who argue confiscation would violate sovereign immunity and set a precedent destructive to the reserve currency system itself.

This controversy is directly relevant to the paper's core argument. If central bank reserves held in reserve currency jurisdictions are subject to political confiscation, this is the most powerful possible empirical demonstration of the paper's thesis. Yet the paper does not engage with the legal mechanism or its implications for the durability of the dollar-euro reserve system.

## 4. EU ETS and International Climate Law

### 4.1 The ETS Directive and UNFCCC/Paris Framework

The EU ETS operates under Directive 2003/87/EC as amended multiple times. The Phase I price collapse was a designed feature of a trial period with national allocation plans deliberately set too generous — this is documented in European Commission ex ante assessments and was legally anticipated as a first-period experiment. The legal mandate of the ETS was never to maintain a particular price; it was to create a legal framework for declining caps.

A legally precise account would distinguish between: (a) political interference with price levels (which occurred, through over-allocation and national lobbying), and (b) the legal mechanism itself functioning as designed (declining caps eventually tightening in Phase III and Phase IV). One can concede (a) while contesting the paper's more sweeping version of the argument.

The EU's obligations under the Paris Agreement (NDCs, Articles 3-4) and UNFCCC are also relevant. The EU ETS is the primary legal mechanism through which the EU meets its Paris NDC obligations. The Market Stability Reserve reform was precisely a legal correction to the over-allocation problem the paper treats as constitutive of the EU ETS identity.

### 4.2 CBAM and WTO Compliance

The Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM, Regulation (EU) 2023/956) has contested WTO compliance. The core WTO question is whether CBAM qualifies for Article XX(b) (health) or Article XX(g) (conservation) exceptions to GATT Articles I and III. The WTO Appellate Body's jurisprudence on "necessity" (EC-Asbestos, Brazil-Tyres) and "relating to" (US-Gasoline, US-Shrimp) suggests CBAM can be defensible under XX(g) if not applied arbitrarily — but this requires the embedded carbon calculation methodology to be transparent and scientifically grounded, which is precisely the point contested by India, China, and Turkey, who have filed WTO challenges. A paper treating EU ETS and CBAM as instruments of energy governance hegemony should engage with this legal complexity directly.

## 5. CUSMA Proportionality Clause and Canadian Energy Sovereignty

This section contains what I regard as the paper's most significant legal omission: a claim with an actual legal mechanism, which the paper asserts on purely political science grounds.

The paper argues that Canada's energy sovereignty is constrained by "deep US security and financial integration" through the Katzenstein filter. The actual legal mechanism constraining Canadian energy sovereignty is CUSMA (Canada-United States-Mexico Agreement), specifically Chapter 32 and the annex on energy, which continues the NAFTA Chapter 6 proportionality obligation in modified form. Under this obligation, Canada is constrained in reducing energy exports to the US below historical proportional ratios. CUSMA Annex 32-C contains the binding treaty language.

The proportionality clause is the precise international legal mechanism through which Canadian energy sovereignty is constrained. It is not merely a political outcome of "deep integration"; it is a binding treaty obligation. A paper arguing that Canada cannot achieve the energy sovereignty necessary for monetary independence should cite CUSMA Chapter 32/Annex 32-C as the legal anchor for this claim. The failure to do so means the paper's Katzenstein filter for Canada is analytically asserted rather than legally grounded.

## 6. Petrodollar and International Monetary Law

### 6.1 The 1974 US-Saudi Arrangement and IMF Article VIII

The paper characterises the 1974 US-Saudi petrodollar arrangement as "a political transaction, not a market outcome." This is accurate as political economy and the Spiro (1999) citation is appropriate. However, the paper does not consider what international monetary law says.

Under IMF Article VIII, members are obligated to maintain convertibility for current account transactions (Section 2) and to refrain from discriminatory currency arrangements (Section 3). The petrodollar system — in which Gulf producers invoice oil exclusively in US dollars and recycle dollar surpluses into US Treasury instruments — does not obviously violate Article VIII because it involves voluntary currency choices in current account transactions. The arrangement's interaction with IMF Article IV — which obligates members to avoid manipulating exchange rates to gain unfair competitive advantage — raises questions that have never been formally adjudicated. The IMF's Article IV consultations with Saudi Arabia have never formally addressed whether the dollar invoicing arrangement is a form of exchange rate management. The paper's claim that the petrodollar system is "political" would be strengthened by noting that it has persisted without formal legal challenge within the IMF framework — which is itself informative about the IMF's capacity to discipline great power monetary arrangements.

## 7. IMF SDR and Reserve Currency Status

### 7.1 The Legal Pathway to Reserve Currency Recognition

The paper predicts that India's INR rises to 3–5% of global FX turnover by BIS 2031 and eventually achieves reserve currency status. The paper does not engage with the formal legal mechanism for reserve currency recognition.

The IMF SDR basket review is the closest thing international monetary law has to a formal reserve currency recognition mechanism. Under the IMF Articles of Agreement, the SDR basket is reviewed every five years. The criteria for inclusion: (a) the issuing member is among the top five exporters; and (b) the currency is "freely usable" under Article XXX(f), meaning it is "widely used to make payments for international transactions" and "widely traded in the principal exchange markets." The RMB's inclusion in the SDR basket in 2016 established that a currency can qualify despite capital account restrictions, provided it meets the "widely used" and "widely traded" tests in observable data.

For the INR, the critical obstacle is India's capital account. India's Foreign Exchange Management Act (FEMA, 1999) maintains significant capital account restrictions — the INR is not fully convertible on the capital account. The IMF's 2015 assessment of the RMB noted that China had made "considerable progress" on capital account liberalisation as a precondition for the SDR determination. India's path to SDR inclusion would similarly require visible progress on capital account liberalisation. The paper's forward prediction should acknowledge this legal-institutional pathway and its prerequisites.

## 8. Ruble-Yuan and Ruble-Rupee Corridors

### 8.1 IMF Article VIII Compliance

The bilateral monetary corridors raise Article VIII(3) concerns about discriminatory currency arrangements. A bilateral corridor in which energy trade is invoiced in bilateral currencies at rates negotiated bilaterally could constitute a multiple currency practice if the bilateral exchange rate deviates from market rates — which the ruble-rupee arrangement demonstrably did in 2022–2023 when Indian rupee-denominated oil payments accumulated in Indian banks and could not be converted due to ruble inconvertibility.

The ruble-rupee impasse is directly relevant to the paper's argument: the bilateral corridor encountered a convertibility problem that illustrates the limits of energy-backed bilateral monetary arrangements absent currency convertibility. The paper should engage with this not merely as an empirical setback but as a legal illustration of why IMF Article VIII convertibility obligations exist.

## 9. Nuclear Non-Proliferation and the Paper's Forward Predictions

### 9.1 U-233 as Weapons-Usable Material

The paper predicts that states with thorium reserves and fuel cycle capacity will gain monetary leverage in the thorium era. U-233 (uranium-233), bred from thorium-232 in a neutron flux, is a weapons-usable fissile material. It can be used in a nuclear weapon. U-233 is classified as a direct-use material by the IAEA, subject to the most stringent safeguards under INFCIRC/153.

A paper that argues thorium fuel cycle development is a legitimate pathway to structural monetary power, and that the fuel cycle "requires sovereign governance capacity," should engage directly with the implication: that the capacity being described is also a latent weapons-relevant capacity.

### 9.2 The Proliferation Incentive Problem

The paper's argument, if taken at face value by policymakers, creates an implicit incentive structure: states that develop thorium fuel cycle capacity gain monetary leverage. Since U-233 is the fissile product of thorium irradiation, this is equivalent to saying states that develop the capacity to produce weapons-usable material gain monetary leverage. The paper should address this directly, either by: (a) arguing that the monetary incentive is separable from the proliferation incentive; (b) arguing that the non-proliferation framework is adequate to contain the proliferation implications; or (c) acknowledging the tension and treating it as a limitation on the paper's normative prescriptiveness.

## 10. Overall Verdict and Required Revisions

**Score: 5/10. Recommendation: Major Revisions — Revise and Resubmit.**

The manuscript addresses a genuine theoretical gap and its empirical apparatus is impressive. The MPI framework is a valuable contribution. However, the paper's engagement with international law is so thin that its central predictions are legally underdetermined.

**Critical revisions required:**

1. The India analysis must engage with NSG dual-use controls on reprocessing technology, the INFCIRC/754 safeguards architecture, and the 123 Agreement's consent rights provisions. The current framing of India as a sovereign actor freely developing its thorium programme ignores a legal architecture that directly constrains this freedom.

2. The AUKUS analysis must engage with the incomplete IAEA Article III(2) negotiation and its implications for the paper's prediction of activated Australian nuclear capacity.

3. The Russian reserve freezing and SWIFT exclusion should be treated as direct empirical evidence for the paper's thesis about the political construction of monetary infrastructure — but this requires engaging with the legal basis for these measures (EU CFSP regulations, sovereign immunity doctrine, ILC countermeasures framework).

4. The paper must address the U-233 dual-use problem directly. A paper predicting that thorium fuel cycle capacity becomes a source of monetary power has an obligation to engage with the non-proliferation implications.

5. The CUSMA Chapter 32/Annex 32-C proportionality mechanism should replace or anchor the paper's Katzenstein filter for Canada.

6. The IMF SDR "freely usable" criterion and India's capital account should be addressed in the INR reserve currency prediction.

The paper's core contribution is robust enough to survive these revisions. Indeed, engagement with the legal architecture in each domain would strengthen rather than undermine the central argument: the legal frameworks governing nuclear technology, financial messaging, monetary convertibility, and energy trade are themselves artifacts of the hegemonic order the paper is analysing.
