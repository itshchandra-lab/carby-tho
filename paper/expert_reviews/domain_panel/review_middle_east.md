# Peer Review — Middle East Political Economy Expert
## "The Carbon-Thorium Standard: Energy-Backed Currencies and the Architecture of Monetary Failure in a Multipolar World"

**Reviewer specialisation:** Gulf political economy, petrodollar architecture, Saudi and UAE economic transformation, Iran sanctions, OPEC+ dynamics, GCC monetary integration
**Journal:** *Middle East Journal* / *International Journal of Middle East Studies* / *Energy Research & Social Science*
**Recommendation:** Major Revisions
**Score:** 5/10

---

## Summary Assessment

This manuscript makes a bold argument about the relationship between energy sovereignty and monetary power, and it draws repeatedly on Middle Eastern cases as empirical anchors — the 1974 petrodollar arrangement, the oil price benchmark, OPEC+ coordination, and the Gulf states' dollar peg. However, the paper's treatment of the Middle East is frozen in 1974. The Gulf political economy has undergone structural transformation since 2016, the paper's petrodollar narrative ignores both the yuan oil transactions of 2023 and the diversification of Gulf sovereign wealth portfolios, and the paper is entirely silent on the most consequential institutional actors in Gulf monetary statecraft: the DIFC, the ADGM, the Gulf SWFs, and Iran's position as a structural test case for the paper's framework. A framework claiming to model energy-monetary relationships in a multipolar world cannot treat the Middle East as a static backdrop. **Major Revisions** are required.

---

## 1. The Petrodollar Narrative: Stuck in 1974

The paper characterises the petrodollar system as a political arrangement originating in the 1974 US-Saudi deal, citing Spiro (1999) as the analytical anchor. This origin story is correct. The paper does not acknowledge what has happened since.

**The 2023 yuan oil transactions.** In March 2023, the first yuan-denominated LNG cargo was settled between Saudi Aramco and TotalEnergies through the Shanghai Petroleum and Natural Gas Exchange (SHPGX). In the same period, Saudi Arabia publicly acknowledged discussions about accepting non-dollar currencies for oil sales. These are not isolated events. Saudi Aramco's 2022–2023 shift toward partial yuan settlement for Chinese oil purchases represents the first visible crack in the dollar-invoicing norm since 1974.

The paper's framework should have something to say about this. Is the yuan oil settlement a precursor to dollar displacement in energy markets? Is it bilateral corridor formation of the kind the paper's MPI mechanism predicts? Does it represent a Katzenstein-filter exception because Saudi Arabia is not embedded in the dollar alliance system in the way Japan or the UK is? The paper's silence on the 2023 events is not defensible for a manuscript with a 2026 publication horizon.

**Vision 2030 and the transformation of the Saudi energy-monetary nexus.** The paper treats Saudi Arabia as an oil exporter whose monetary role is entirely parasitic on dollar hegemony. Vision 2030 represents an explicit state-led attempt to decouple Saudi fiscal and monetary sustainability from oil revenue. Neom, the PIF's diversification mandate, the Riyadh financial centre ambitions, and the Green Initiative's energy transition commitments are all relevant to how Saudi Arabia's energy-monetary nexus will evolve. A paper predicting monetary trajectories to 2031 and 2037 cannot treat Saudi Arabia as a static hydrocarbon rentier.

## 2. The UAE as Alternative Financial Centre: A Structural Omission

The paper mentions the UAE only in passing, if at all. This is a significant analytical gap.

The UAE — specifically Dubai and Abu Dhabi — has become the primary alternative financial centre for capital that wants neither full US jurisdiction (FATF compliance, OFAC exposure) nor Chinese jurisdiction (capital controls, RMB inconvertibility). The Dubai International Financial Centre (DIFC) and the Abu Dhabi Global Market (ADGM) are common-law jurisdictions with independent courts, offering institutional quality comparable to London or Singapore for financial contracting while being outside the US dollar enforcement architecture in meaningful ways.

**The UAE's role in the Russia-India oil corridor.** Following Russia's February 2022 invasion and subsequent SWIFT exclusion, the UAE emerged as the primary settlement intermediary in the Russia-India-UAE oil triangle. Russian crude discounted below Brent was purchased by Indian refiners, with settlement routed through UAE-based intermediaries using AED, INR, or CNH. The UAE's institutional architecture — AML-compliant enough to avoid FATF grey-listing, but not subject to OFAC secondary sanctions in the way European banks are — made it the natural settlement hub. This is precisely the kind of institutional-corridor formation that the paper's MPI framework should model, yet the paper does not engage with it.

Young's (2017) *Political Economy of Energy, Finance and Security in the UAE* provides exactly the analytical framework the paper needs for this dimension. Its absence is notable.

## 3. Iran as Structural Test Case

Iran is the paper's missing falsification case. The paper's MPI framework predicts that energy sovereignty translates into monetary influence. Iran is the world's second-largest holder of natural gas reserves and has the fourth-largest proven oil reserves. Under the paper's framework, Iran should have substantial monetary influence. It demonstrably does not.

Iran has been excluded from SWIFT since 2012. Its central bank reserves are frozen or inaccessible. Its currency has lost approximately 95% of its value since 2018. Iran trades oil bilaterally with China at substantial discounts (reportedly $5-15/barrel below market, though figures are contested), invoiced in CNY or barter arrangements, with minimal monetary leverage accruing to the Iranian rial.

This is precisely the scenario the paper describes for Russia Mechanism A — energy-rich but institutionally frozen. But Iran's exclusion predates the Ukraine war by a decade. Iran has had longer to develop workarounds. The ruble-yuan corridor model that the paper discusses for Russia has a precursor in Iran's bilateral arrangement with China and, more recently, the Iran-Russia oil-for-goods agreements.

The paper should either: (a) include Iran as an explicit negative case — an energy-rich state where the paper's mechanism fails completely under sustained financial exclusion — and explain what structural conditions make the mechanism fail; or (b) explain why Iran is excluded from the analytical scope, which requires an explicit argument about what makes Iran different from Russia in ways that matter for the MPI framework.

The Iran case also creates a non-trivial problem for the paper's p_t transition argument. Iran has thorium deposits (though not in the top tier of global reserves). If thorium transition creates monetary leverage, would a post-sanctions Iran with a developed thorium fuel cycle gain monetary influence? The paper should model this case even if it concludes the answer is no.

## 4. Qatar and the Small-State Anomaly

Qatar is the world's largest LNG exporter per capita and the third-largest holder of proven natural gas reserves. Under the paper's MPI framework, Qatar's energy position should be exceptional. Qatar's governance quality (WGI) is relatively high by regional standards. The rial maintains a hard dollar peg.

Qatar's monetary position does not match what the paper's framework would predict. The Qatari rial is not internationalising. Qatar's financial influence is exercised through the Qatar Investment Authority (QIA) — a sovereign wealth fund with approximately $475 billion in assets — not through currency denomination. Qatar's energy trade is dollar-denominated not because Qatar lacks energy sovereignty but because the LNG market's global benchmark and contract structure is dollar-dominated in ways that don't respond to individual producer preferences.

The paper's Katzenstein filter would explain this as Qatar being embedded in the US security architecture — and there is something to this (Al Udeid Air Base hosts the largest US air deployment in the Middle East). But Katzenstein's original argument is about states embedded in *dense* multilateral alliance networks, not about individual bilateral basing arrangements. Qatar's relationship with the US is closer to a bilateral security guarantee than to the kind of deep institutional embeddment the paper describes for Japan or the UK.

The Qatar case tests the boundary conditions of both the MPI framework and the Katzenstein filter. Is Qatar's non-internalisation driven by the dollar peg (monetary policy choice), the Katzenstein filter (security dependency), or the LNG market structure (commodity market convention)? The paper needs to model Qatar explicitly or acknowledge that small petrostates represent a class of cases its framework cannot handle without modification.

## 5. Saudi Nuclear Ambitions and the TMPI Calculation

The paper does not include Saudi Arabia in its TMPI calculations. This omission requires justification.

Saudi Arabia has been pursuing civilian nuclear capacity since at least 2009. The Saudi nuclear programme's trajectory:
- **2017**: Saudi Arabia signed an MOU with the IAEA for a civilian nuclear programme
- **2018**: Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman stated publicly that Saudi Arabia would develop nuclear weapons if Iran did
- **2020**: Saudi Arabia selected Korean KEPCO to build two 1.4 GW reactors at Qassim
- **2023-2026**: Saudi Arabia has been in negotiations with the US over a 123 Agreement, with the specific controversy being Saudi insistence on retaining the right to enrich uranium domestically ("golden sceptre" clause) — the same sovereignty provision India has through non-NPT status that the treaty law reviewer identifies as critical

Saudi Arabia's TMPI under the paper's formula would be low (minimal thorium reserves confirmed, no operational nuclear capacity). But Saudi Arabia's nuclear ambitions are directly relevant to the paper's Katzenstein filter argument: Saudi Arabia is attempting to develop nuclear capacity while negotiating a 123 Agreement with the US — which would, if completed, embed Saudi Arabia in the US non-proliferation architecture in ways that constrain its energy sovereignty. The Saudi case is therefore a contemporaneous test of whether nuclear ambition without strategic autonomy can translate into energy-monetary leverage of the kind the paper attributes to India.

## 6. Gulf Sovereign Wealth Funds and Dollar Reserve Architecture

The paper treats Gulf dollar recycling as a static property of the petrodollar system. This is analytically inadequate given the portfolio transformation underway in Gulf SWFs.

The Abu Dhabi Investment Authority (ADIA), the Public Investment Fund (PIF), the Kuwait Investment Authority (KIA), and the Qatar Investment Authority (QIA) together manage approximately $3.5 trillion in assets. Their portfolio allocation decisions are directly relevant to the paper's reserve currency predictions.

**The de-dollarisation of Gulf SWF portfolios.** Available data (SWF Institute, annual reports) suggests Gulf SWFs have been increasing allocations to: yuan-denominated assets (through Bond Connect, equity quotas, and bilateral agreements with PBOC); alternative assets (infrastructure, private equity, real estate) that reduce dollar-denominated fixed-income exposure; and domestic investment (Vision 2030, diversification mandates) that reduces offshore dollar recycling.

This is not a wholesale departure from dollar dominance. But it is a directional shift that the paper's binary framing — dollar hegemony versus dollar displacement — cannot capture. Gulf SWF diversification is precisely the kind of "layered" transition the paper, following PPP 2025, describes as the operative mode of monetary challenge. The paper should model whether Gulf SWF portfolio diversification constitutes a first-order, second-order, or third-order effect on dollar reserve share.

**The China-Gulf investment relationship.** Chinese investment in Gulf infrastructure (Aramco's acquisition stakes, SABIC JVs, PIF-CITIC partnerships), combined with yuan-denominated oil settlement, creates a bilateral energy-monetary corridor that is more developed than anything the paper models explicitly. This corridor — hydrocarbons flowing east, yuan and investment flowing west — is the most significant example of the paper's proposed mechanism operating in real time.

## 7. OPEC+ as Governance-Constructed Oil Benchmark

The paper uses the oil price CV (0.292 for the long-run window) as the denominator in its GS calculation. This figure is treated as a market-derived natural benchmark. It is not.

OPEC+ is the world's most consequential commodity cartel. It coordinates production decisions for approximately 40% of global oil supply among 23 member states. The 2020 "OPEC+ War" between Saudi Arabia and Russia — in which both sides deliberately increased production to crash prices — and the subsequent 2020 extraordinary agreement, the 2022 post-Ukraine production politics, and the 2023-2024 voluntary cut episodes all demonstrate that oil's CV is itself partially the product of political coordination by a governance body.

This creates a circularity problem in the GS formula. GS is meant to measure the political construction of a carbon market instrument relative to the "natural" volatility of the commodity benchmark. But the commodity benchmark is itself politically constructed by OPEC+. The appropriate counterfactual oil CV — oil in a world without OPEC+ coordination — is almost certainly higher than 0.292. OPEC+'s success at dampening oil price volatility means the paper may be underestimating GS (if uncoordinated oil would have higher CV) or measuring something conceptually different from what it claims.

El-Gamal and Jaffe (2010) in *Oil, Dollars, Debt, and Crises* provide the most rigorous treatment of the political economy of oil price formation that the paper should engage with. The paper's treatment of the oil CV as a neutral benchmark is a serious analytical weakness from a Middle East political economy perspective.

## 8. The Iran-Russia-UAE Triangle and Sanctions Architecture

The paper discusses Russia's financial isolation extensively. It does not discuss the most consequential institutional adaptation to Western financial sanctions: the Iran-Russia-UAE corridor.

Since 2022, the Russia-India oil trade, the Russia-China energy corridor, and the older Iran-China oil relationship have all been mediated, in part, through UAE-based financial institutions. The DIFC and free-zone entities have become routing points for sanctions-sensitive transactions in ways that exploit UAE's jurisdictional ambiguity — not a SWIFT participant for most categories of transaction, not subject to OFAC secondary sanctions to the degree EU banks are, but maintaining FATF compliance sufficient to avoid complete financial isolation.

This matters for the paper's argument in two ways:

**First**, it demonstrates that alternative settlement infrastructure is already operational — not at BRICS Bridge scale or CNY internationalisation scale, but at the level of bilateral commodity corridor, which is precisely the mechanism the paper's MPI framework models. The UAE case is empirical validation of the paper's transmission channel 1 (trade invoicing) operating in real conditions under adversarial financial pressure.

**Second**, the sustainability of the UAE corridor is uncertain. The US Treasury has sanctioned several UAE-based entities for facilitating Russian oil sales. The FATF placed the UAE on its grey list in 2022, triggering institutional reform. The current UAE corridor may be a transition phenomenon that contracts as US financial enforcement reaches into UAE jurisdictions. The paper's forward predictions about corridor formation should address whether the UAE model is scalable or jurisdictionally constrained.

## 9. Missing Literature

**Vitalis, Robert (2007). *America's Kingdom: Mythmaking on the Saudi Frontier.* Stanford University Press.** — The foundational critical account of the US-Saudi oil relationship and its political construction. Essential context for the paper's petrodollar narrative.

**Hertog, Steffen (2010). *Princes, Brokers, and Bureaucrats: Oil and the State in Saudi Arabia.* Cornell University Press.** — The standard reference on how Saudi state institutions translate oil wealth into political economy outcomes. Directly relevant to the GS mechanism applied to Gulf cases.

**Young, Karen E. (2017). *The Political Economy of Energy, Finance and Security in the UAE.* Palgrave Macmillan.** — The essential reference on UAE financial architecture and its relationship to energy statecraft. Foundational for any engagement with the UAE as monetary actor.

**El-Gamal, Mahmoud A., and Amy Myers Jaffe (2010). *Oil, Dollars, Debt, and Crises: The Global Curse of Black Gold.* Cambridge University Press.** — Rigorous treatment of the political economy of oil price formation, cartel dynamics, and the oil-dollar nexus. Addresses the circularity problem in using oil CV as a neutral benchmark.

**Fulton, Jonathan (2019). *China's Relations with the Gulf Monarchies.* Routledge.** — The systematic treatment of China-Gulf energy-monetary relationships. Essential for the paper's engagement with yuan oil settlement and CNY-Gulf corridor formation.

---

## 10. Overall Verdict

**Score: 5/10. Recommendation: Major Revisions.**

The paper's Middle East engagement is thematically present but analytically underdeveloped. The petrodollar narrative is accurate for 1974 but does not engage with 2023 realities. Iran is missing as the most important negative case in the paper's framework. The UAE's role as an alternative settlement hub is entirely absent despite being directly relevant to the paper's transmission channel argument. Qatar tests the Katzenstein filter's boundary conditions in ways the paper doesn't model. OPEC+ creates a circularity problem in the GS calculation that the paper does not acknowledge.

**Required revisions:**
1. Engage with the 2023 yuan oil settlement as a test case for the paper's corridor-formation mechanism
2. Include Iran as an explicit negative case explaining why energy wealth does not translate into monetary power under sustained financial exclusion
3. Address the UAE's role as alternative settlement infrastructure in the Russia-India-China energy corridors
4. Acknowledge the OPEC+ political construction problem for the oil CV benchmark
5. Address Saudi nuclear ambitions as a contemporaneous test of the Katzenstein filter
6. Engage with Gulf SWF portfolio diversification as a "layered" form of dollar reserve reduction

These are not peripheral additions. They are cases that directly test whether the paper's framework is correct. The Middle East is the empirical heart of the energy-monetary nexus the paper claims to theorise. Its treatment as static backdrop is the paper's most avoidable analytical weakness.
