# Peer Review — Green Theory / Political Ecology Expert
## "The Carbon-Thorium Standard: Energy-Backed Currencies and the Architecture of Monetary Failure in a Multipolar World"

**Reviewer specialisation:** Green political economy, political ecology, ecological economics, carbon markets (Paterson, Newell, Lohmann, Polanyi tradition)
**Journal:** *New Political Economy* / *Global Environmental Politics* (Green IPE Special Issue)
**Recommendation:** Major Revisions (with possibility of rejection if fundamental framing is not addressed)
**Score:** 4/10

---

## Summary Assessment

This manuscript offers a technically sophisticated and genuinely novel contribution to reserve currency theory. The MPI framework, the empirical analysis of EU ETS volatility (CV = 0.815 versus RGGI's 0.047), and the analogy between carbon governance and the gold standard all represent original intellectual moves.

However, from the perspective of green political economy, this paper has a fundamental problem that cannot be resolved through minor revision: it reproduces, in the register of energy sovereignty analysis, precisely the logic of fossil capitalism it claims to diagnose. It treats energy as a strategic input to monetary power without once asking whether the energy-monetary nexus it so carefully maps is itself part of the problem of climate breakdown. Climate change, ecological limits, and the justice dimensions of energy transition are entirely absent from this world. This is not merely a gap in the literature review. It is a constitutive absence that shapes the paper's categories, its policy implications, and its normative horizon.

---

## 1. The Ecological Blind Spot: Energy as Strategic Resource

The paper's foundational move is to treat energy as a strategic resource for monetary power. NEP is defined as production share minus import share, with all energy types treated equally. This is analytically coherent within a realist IPE framework. But from a green theory perspective, this equivalence assumption is where the paper's ecological blind spot begins.

Treating fossil fuels, renewables, and nuclear energy as interchangeable inputs to an NEP calculation erases precisely the distinctions that matter most for planetary governance. A country with high NEP driven by coal extraction (India, historically) is treated as structurally equivalent to one with high NEP driven by solar (Morocco, prospectively) or nuclear (France). The ecological content of energy production — its carbon intensity, its land use, its waste streams, its embodied justice claims — is entirely invisible to the framework. The paper's metric is ecologically blind by design.

Matthew Paterson's work on climate governance is directly relevant here. Paterson (2001, *Understanding Global Environmental Politics*; 2012, with Newell, *Climate Capitalism*) argues that the relationship between fossil capitalism and monetary order is not incidental but structural: the petrodollar system, Bretton Woods's collapse, and the subsequent era of financialised capitalism are all bound up with the politics of hydrocarbon extraction and accumulation. The paper acknowledges this connection in its description of the "fossil standard" but draws entirely the wrong conclusion. Rather than asking what the energy-monetary nexus reveals about the structural requirements of capitalist accumulation and its incompatibility with ecological limits, the paper asks: which states will win the next round of energy-monetary competition? This is not green IPE. It is realist IPE dressed in a green nomenclature.

The more fundamental problem: any monetary standard anchored to an energy resource will systematically bias the political economy toward extraction and throughput of that resource. The gold standard incentivised gold extraction. The petrodollar system incentivised fossil fuel extraction. A thorium standard — if it ever emerged — would incentivise thorium extraction and nuclear infrastructure construction regardless of ecological consequences. The paper treats this as a design feature (stable monetary anchor) rather than a political ecology problem.

## 2. The EU ETS Analysis: Designed to Fail, or Evidence of Governance Failure?

The paper's empirical treatment of the EU ETS Phase I collapse is one of its strongest sections. The CV of 0.815 is a striking statistic, and the argument that carbon governance is "politically constructed" is correct. But the paper draws the wrong theoretical conclusions from correct empirical observations.

The paper treats the ETS collapse as evidence of governance failure — a mechanism that should have worked as a price signal but was distorted by political interference. This framing implicitly assumes that there exists a better-designed version of carbon pricing that would function as a stable anchor for GS. This is the technocratic residual of a fundamentally political problem.

Green political economists — and here the paper's failure to engage with Larry Lohmann's work (2009) is critical — would argue that the ETS was not designed to fail in the sense of being badly engineered. It was designed to fail in the deeper sense that it was a deliberate institutional choice to insert a market mechanism into a political problem in order to avoid the distributional conflicts that direct regulation would have forced into the open. Lohmann's analysis of carbon commodification ("Financialization, Commodification and Carbon," *Socialist Register*, 2009) demonstrates that carbon markets serve a legitimation function within fossil capitalism: they allow continued accumulation by creating a new financial asset class while deferring the structural transformation that genuine decarbonisation requires. The ETS Phase I collapse is not a bug — it is the predictable outcome of a mechanism designed to appear to address climate change while actually protecting incumbent industrial interests.

This matters for the paper's framework because the GS metric is presented as a measure of governance quality. But from a green political economy perspective, the question is: whose governance, and governance toward what end? If the ETS was functioning as a legitimation mechanism for continued accumulation, then its volatility may reflect the contradictions of trying to price a commons (the atmosphere) within a capitalist framework, not simply poor institutional design. The paper has no analytical category for this distinction.

The comparison between RGGI (CV = 0.047) and EU ETS (CV = 0.815) is presented as straightforwardly demonstrating superior governance quality in the RGGI case. But RGGI covers a much smaller, more homogeneous set of states, with a narrower sectoral scope and a more politically manageable distributional conflict. The 17x differential may reflect the difference in political economy between a voluntary compact of northeastern US states and a supranational carbon market spanning 27 countries with radically different industrial structures. To read this as a simple governance quality differential depoliticises the comparison in ways that green IPE cannot accept.

## 3. The Carbon-Thorium Analogy: Theoretical Coherence and Polanyian Problems

The paper's central analogy — between the "carbon standard" and the gold standard — is intellectually ambitious and partially persuasive, but it creates significant theoretical problems when examined through a Polanyian lens.

For Polanyi, the gold standard was not merely a technical monetary mechanism. It was a specific institutional embodiment of the broader project of the "self-regulating market" — the attempt to organise human social life around market logic in ways that systematically disrupted existing social bonds, community ties, and relationships with nature. The double movement was the counter-mobilisation of social forces to protect society from the corrosive effects of market logic.

The paper cites Polanyi but does not follow through on what this means for carbon markets. If carbon markets are a Polanyian "fictitious commodity" — an attempt to treat the atmosphere (a commons, a social relation, a global life-support system) as if it were a tradeable commodity — then the Phase I ETS collapse is not simply governance failure. It is the double movement in action: the resistance of social and political forces to the commodification of a global commons. From this perspective, GS > 1 is not evidence of weak governance. It is evidence of Polanyian protective re-embedding.

This is, from a green theory perspective, an ambiguous finding. The Polanyian double movement can protect socially desirable institutions (welfare states) or socially undesirable ones (incumbent fossil fuel industries). The question is which social forces are doing the re-embedding, in whose interests, and toward what ends. The paper has no framework for this question. It reads the double movement solely as governance failure, missing the normative complexity that Polanyi's framework actually generates.

Arsel and Büscher (2012, "Nature™ Inc.") extend this analysis to contemporary green economy discourse, arguing that attempts to price nature reproduce the Polanyian problem at a higher level of abstraction: rather than protecting nature from market logic, they embed nature more deeply within it. The paper's MPI framework, which treats carbon governance stability as a component of monetary power, implicitly endorses this commodification logic.

## 4. Thorium and Extractivism: Green Veneer on Extractive Logic

The TMPI is defined as: thorium reserve share × nuclear share × institutional quality. This is structurally identical to the logic of the petrodollar: monetary power derives from control over scarce energy resources and the institutional capacity to monetise them. The paper substitutes thorium for petroleum and thorium-cycle nuclear reactors for oil wells, but the underlying political ecology is identical.

The green extractivism literature — Martinez-Alier (2002, *The Environmentalism of the Poor*); Gudynas (2010, "The New Extractivism of the 21st Century") — is entirely absent from the paper's framework. Martinez-Alier's analysis of ecological distribution conflicts is directly relevant to the paper's claims about India's prospective monetary rise. Thorium deposits in India are geographically concentrated in specific coastal and inland regions, disproportionately affecting Adivasi and other marginalised communities. A thorium monetary standard that incentivises rapid extraction of these reserves would reproduce, in a nuclear register, the same patterns of ecological distribution conflict and sacrifice zone politics that characterise the petrodollar system.

More fundamentally, the paper does not ask whether a thorium monetary standard is desirable from an ecological standpoint. It asks whether it is feasible and who will benefit from it. These are important questions, but they are the wrong questions if the paper's ambition is to contribute to the literature on monetary transitions in an era of climate governance.

## 5. Climate Justice and the MPI

Climate justice scholarship (Ciplet, Roberts, and Khan 2015, *Power in a Warming World*; Okereke 2008, *Global Justice and Neoliberal Environmental Governance*) argues that the distribution of climate impacts, mitigation burdens, and transition benefits reproduces and often intensifies existing patterns of global inequality. The paper's MPI framework, applied to a thorium transition, generates a new hierarchy of monetary power that is largely orthogonal to the distribution of climate vulnerability.

The states that win in a thorium monetary order (India, potentially Brazil given its uranium-thorium geology) are not necessarily the states that bear the highest climate risks or that have contributed least to historical emissions. Meanwhile, states that lose monetary power — fossil fuel exporters like Russia, Saudi Arabia, and Norway — are not necessarily those that have contributed most to climate breakdown. The paper has no analytical framework for this distributional question. Its normative horizon is monetary stability and geopolitical order, not climate justice or ecological equity.

The justice question also applies to the paper's treatment of nuclear energy as a clean transition technology. The paper does not engage with the climate justice literature on nuclear energy, which is divided and context-dependent. The paper's TMPI rewards nuclear share as a straightforwardly positive indicator, with no differentiation by institutional context, safety regime, or waste management capacity.

## 6. Degrowth, Post-Growth Monetary Theory, and the Energy-Monetary Nexus

The paper does not engage with the post-growth monetary literature.

Tim Jackson (*Prosperity Without Growth*, 2009, 2017) argues that capitalist monetary systems are structurally dependent on continuous economic growth because of the dynamics of interest-bearing debt creation. The energy-monetary nexus that this paper identifies is, in Jackson's framework, one of the mechanisms through which monetary systems externalise ecological costs: reserve currency states can run persistent current account deficits that are effectively subsidised by the rest of the world's willingness to hold their liabilities. This allows reserve currency states to sustain higher levels of energy consumption than their domestic production would otherwise support.

Kate Raworth (*Doughnut Economics*, 2017) extends this analysis through the lens of planetary boundaries, arguing that the monetary system's growth dependency systematically drives economic activity beyond the safe operating space for humanity. A thorium monetary standard that replicates this logic — granting reserve currency status to thorium-rich states and allowing them to run deficits subsidised by thorium scarcity rents — would reproduce the growth-energy-monetary nexus in a new register, with new beneficiaries, but without addressing the structural dynamic that makes that nexus ecologically destructive.

## 7. Governance Sensitivity and the Polanyian Double Movement: A Deeper Reading

The paper defines GS as carbon governance volatility divided by commodity benchmark volatility. A GS of 2.79 (EU ETS Phase I) is treated as evidence of politically constructed, unstable governance. The paper argues that high GS undermines the monetary signal value of carbon. This is analytically coherent.

But consider the Polanyian interpretation. If carbon markets are a fictitious commodity, then high GS may reflect precisely the social and political resistance that Polanyi's double movement predicts. The EU ETS Phase I collapse was driven by: (1) industrial lobbying for over-allocation; (2) member state governments protecting energy-intensive industries from cost shocks; (3) price discovery problems in a genuinely novel market. These are all forms of the double movement. The paper has no framework for making the distinction between GS representing industrial incumbents blocking effective governance (normatively negative) and GS representing community-level resistance to carbon costs on vulnerable populations (normatively positive from a justice standpoint).

Sarah Bracking's work on performativity in the green economy (2015) is relevant here: financial instruments claiming to price environmental goods do not simply measure pre-existing values but actively constitute new political subjects, interests, and power relations. The EU ETS's Phase I volatility was not simply noise around a signal; it was the process through which new political interests were being constituted in relation to a new market. High GS may be evidence of institutional becoming rather than institutional failure. The paper's framework cannot engage with this possibility.

## 8. What the Paper Gets Right

**The EU ETS as politically constructed mechanism.** The CV analysis provides quantitative support for arguments that green political economists have made qualitatively for two decades. The 17x volatility differential between RGGI and EU ETS is a striking empirical finding.

**The analogy between gold standard and carbon standard** captures something real about the political economy of carbon governance that much of the climate policy literature misses by treating the ETS as a technical instrument.

**Attention to the geopolitical dimensions of energy transition.** The paper's attention to which states will gain and lose monetary power as the energy mix shifts is an important counterweight to the tendency to treat energy transformation as purely technical.

**The honest acknowledgment that thorium faces the same risks of "capture" as the carbon standard.** This creates an opening for the kind of critical engagement this review calls for. The paper would benefit enormously from developing this acknowledgement into a full analysis of the political conditions under which a thorium monetary order might or might not advance ecological and social goals.

## 10. Required Revisions

1. Engage directly with the ecological limits literature and explain why treating all energy types as equivalent inputs to NEP is analytically justified despite their radically different ecological profiles.
2. Revise the EU ETS analysis to incorporate the political economy of carbon commodification (Lohmann, Newell and Paterson) rather than treating Phase I collapse as simple governance failure.
3. Develop the paper's own acknowledgement that thorium risks the same "capture" as carbon, and situate this in the extractivism literature (Martinez-Alier, Gudynas).
4. Engage with the Polanyian double movement at the level of normative complexity it requires — GS > 1 is not straightforwardly a governance failure from a green theory perspective.
5. Either engage with the post-growth monetary literature or explicitly scope the paper as a contribution to realist IPE rather than green IPE.
