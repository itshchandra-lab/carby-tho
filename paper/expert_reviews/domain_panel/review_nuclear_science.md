# Peer Review — Nuclear Science and Policy Expert
## "The Carbon-Thorium Standard: Energy-Backed Currencies and the Architecture of Monetary Failure in a Multipolar World"

**Reviewer specialisation:** Nuclear Science and Policy — fuel cycle, reactor engineering, non-proliferation, IAEA safeguards
**Journals of comparable standard:** *Energy Policy*, *Nuclear Engineering and Design*, *The Nonproliferation Review*, *Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists*
**Recommendation:** Major Revisions Required
**Score:** 4/10

---

## Summary Assessment

This paper makes an ambitious interdisciplinary argument: that thorium reserves, combined with fuel cycle infrastructure and institutional quality, constitute a new axis of structural monetary power in a multipolar world order. The theoretical architecture is intellectually serious and worth engaging. However, the Forward Register (§7) rests on nuclear science claims that range from oversimplified to technically incorrect. Several errors are not trivial misstatements but strike directly at the central empirical claims. The paper requires major revisions, particularly §§2 and 7.

---

## 1. Thorium Fuel Cycle Science

### 1.1 Fertile vs Fissile: Is It Correctly Represented?

The paper's treatment of Th-232 as a fertile-not-fissile material is implicit but not explicit. The text says "breeding U-233 from thorium" without clearly stating the underlying nuclear physics: Th-232 absorbs a thermal or fast neutron to become Th-233, which beta-decays through Pa-233 (half-life 27 days) to U-233.

The protactinium-233 bottleneck is a critical omission. In a thermal spectrum reactor, Pa-233 has a non-trivial neutron absorption cross-section. If Pa-233 absorbs a neutron before decaying to U-233, the bred fissile material is lost. This is why thorium-cycle reactor designs must either: (a) use a fast neutron spectrum, or (b) design for Pa-233 separation from the neutron flux (the approach of molten salt reactors — MSRE design, and SINAP's TMSR-LF1), or (c) accept lower breeding ratios and run as a converter rather than a true breeder. This physics is not a minor technicality. It determines whether India's PFBR can actually serve the Stage 2 function the paper assigns to it.

**Technology Readiness Levels (TRL):**
- Th-U233 fuel cycle globally: TRL 3-4 (experimental proof of concept, no industrial demonstration)
- India's AHWR: TRL 4-5 (detailed design complete, no construction licence)
- China's TMSR-LF1: TRL 5-6 (experimental criticality reportedly achieved 2023-2024; not independently confirmed at time of this review)
- Russia's thorium programme: no active experimental programme; primarily desk studies

The paper writes as if thorium fuel cycles are an emerging industrial reality. At present they are a promising research direction at early experimental stage in one country (China) and advanced design stage in another (India).

### 1.2 Fast vs Thermal Routes: Engineering Challenges

The paper conflates two fundamentally different technical routes without distinguishing them:

**Fast reactor route** (PFBR, BREST): Thorium in the blanket. Fast neutron flux transmutes Th-232 → U-233 over time. U-233 must be separated by reprocessing. This requires: (i) remote-handled fuel fabrication because U-233 is always accompanied by U-232, whose decay product Tl-208 is a hard gamma emitter (2.6 MeV); (ii) full reprocessing capability (THOREX process); and (iii) new fuel fabrication infrastructure not yet demonstrated at scale anywhere in the world.

**Thermal reactor route** (AHWR, MSR): Thorium used directly as the fertile component in the thermal core, with fissile driver driving the reaction.

The paper's argument that "the thorium fuel cycle cannot be commoditised" has some merit but is stated without the necessary qualification. The reason thorium is harder to commoditise than uranium is specific: U-233 requires hot-cell fabrication due to the U-232 gamma problem; THOREX reprocessing is considerably less developed than PUREX; and there is no commercial thorium fuel market, no supply chain, and no licensed thorium fuel assembly design in any operating reactor fleet.

However, the paper's argument that this constitutes an *inherent sovereignty advantage* rather than simply *early-stage technology immaturity* is not adequately defended. As the technology matures, thorium fuel cycle services could in principle be provided by a Rosatom-equivalent state enterprise, replicating exactly the uranium enrichment dependence structure the paper criticises.

## 2. India's Three-Stage Programme — Technical Assessment

### 2.1 PFBR's Primary Function vs Thorium Breeding

This is the paper's most technically significant substantive error. The statement "Stage 2 produces plutonium that breeds U-233 from thorium" conflates two distinct reactor functions.

The PFBR is a **sodium-cooled fast reactor with a Pu-U MOX core and depleted uranium blanket**. Its primary design function is to breed plutonium — to achieve a breeding ratio greater than 1.0 using a U-238 blanket, producing more Pu-239 than it consumes. This is the purpose of the Stage 2 reactor in Bhabha's scheme: to build up India's plutonium inventory so that Stage 3 can be fuelled.

The PFBR's published design uses a **depleted uranium blanket, not a thorium blanket**. For the PFBR to breed U-233, India would need to substitute thorium oxide (ThO2) pins in the blanket positions — technically feasible but not the basecase design in published IGCAR documentation. IGCAR has published research on thorium blanket options for *future* fast reactors, but the phrase "Stage 2 produces plutonium that breeds U-233 from thorium" as a statement of current PFBR design intent is not accurate.

**Required correction:** "Stage 2 FBRs will breed additional plutonium using U-238 blankets; future fast reactors in the Stage 2 fleet *may* use thorium blankets to initiate U-233 production, but this is not the PFBR's primary design mission."

### 2.2 PFBR Status: What Does "Core Loading in 2024" Mean?

The statement that "the PFBR at Kalpakkam began core loading in 2024" is broadly consistent with public reporting, but the precise dating requires care. Core loading is a pre-criticality step, not proof of operational status. As of early 2026, the PFBR has not achieved **first criticality**, and grid connection remains further out. The sequence is: core loading → approach to criticality → first criticality → low-power physics tests → power ascension testing → full power operation → grid connection.

The paper's language is potentially misleading. "Began core loading in 2024" implies progress toward operation, which is accurate, but readers may conflate this with the reactor being operational or generating electricity. It is not.

### 2.3 Is 20% Nuclear by 2035 Feasible?

This is the paper's most significantly overstated claim.

India's current installed nuclear capacity is approximately 7.5 GW (operational). Total electricity generation capacity in India is approximately 950 GW (as of 2025). To achieve 20% nuclear share of electricity generation by 2035 would require approximately 190 GW of nuclear capacity — roughly **25 times India's current nuclear capacity** — within roughly nine years.

India's actual target, as stated in NPCIL projections, is 22,480 MWe by 2031 — approximately 22 GW, representing a tripling of current capacity, not 20% of total generation. India has missed every nuclear capacity target it has set since the 1970s, typically by factors of 5-10 (per M.V. Ramana's systematic analysis of DAE target accuracy).

A 20% nuclear share by 2035 is not consistent with any credible analysis of India's nuclear construction pipeline, grid infrastructure constraints, financing mechanisms, or construction lead times. **This figure needs to be sourced and critically evaluated, not stated as official policy.** If the source is a DAE press statement, it should be cited as aspirational with appropriate scepticism.

### 2.4 AHWR Design Status

The Advanced Heavy Water Reactor is a 300 MWe design developed by BARC using Th-Pu MOX fuel. The AHWR's current status is detailed engineering design, not construction. An AERB construction licence has not been issued. There is no construction schedule in the public domain with realistic milestones. The paper should not imply imminent construction. A more accurate description: the AHWR is the world's most technically advanced thorium reactor design in the pre-construction phase, but industrial deployment is likely 15-25 years from Stage 2 fuel cycle readiness.

## 3. China's TMSR Programme vs India's PFBR

### 3.1 TMSR-LF1 Actual Status

China's TMSR-LF1 at Wuwei, Gansu, is a 2 MWth experimental reactor using a LiF-ThF4 fluoride salt, moderated by graphite. Reports from SINAP (Shanghai Institute of Applied Physics) indicate the reactor achieved first criticality in approximately late 2023 or early 2024. If accurate, this makes TMSR-LF1 the **world's first thorium-fuelled reactor to reach criticality since the MSRE at Oak Ridge (1965-1969)** — a historic milestone.

China's TMSR-LF1 achievement represents a genuine technology breakthrough, not merely a design exercise. China's thorium fuel cycle TRL (approximately 5-6 for MSR, with experimental criticality data) may already exceed India's (approximately 4-5 for AHWR, no criticality demonstration). The paper gives India TMPI = 15.8 and China TMPI = 4.2, with the difference driven primarily by reserve share. But if **knowledge structure** (Strange's concept, which the paper itself invokes) is the operative variable for monetary power, China may be ahead of India in the dimension that matters most.

### 3.2 The Scale Problem with TMPI

The paper's TMPI formula systematically underweights nuclear energy capacity in favour of thorium reserve share:
- China: 57 operating reactors (~57 GW), 22 under construction, world's only operating thorium experimental reactor
- India: 23 operating reactors (~7.5 GW), one fast reactor approaching criticality, no operational thorium fuel

Yet India TMPI (15.8) >> China TMPI (4.2) because India holds 16.1% of world thorium reserves vs China's 2.1%.

The formula embeds an assumption that reserve share is the binding constraint. But the paper's own Strange-derived theoretical framework suggests knowledge structure and industrial capacity are the binding constraints for activating monetary leverage. If China can manufacture and operate thorium fuel cycle equipment and export that technology (as Rosatom exports uranium fuel cycle services), then China's low thorium reserve share becomes less relevant than India's high reserve share. The paper does not engage with this tension.

## 4. The AUKUS Nuclear Error

This section contains the paper's most consequential technical error, because it directly affects a named falsification condition.

**AUKUS submarines** use naval nuclear propulsion reactors fuelled with **highly enriched uranium (HEU), typically enriched to approximately 93% U-235**. These are:
- Not thorium fuel cycle reactors in any component
- Not civilian power generation assets
- Sealed, lifetime-core designs that are never refuelled during the submarine's operational life
- Subject to IAEA safeguards exemption for naval propulsion under the NPT Article III.1 "naval propulsion loophole"

**What AUKUS involves:**
1. Transfer of Virginia-class submarines to the Royal Australian Navy (mid-2030s)
2. Construction of SSN-AUKUS vessels jointly in Australia and the UK
3. No civil nuclear power component
4. No Australian enrichment facility
5. No Australian fuel fabrication capability
6. No thorium component whatsoever

Australia's position on domestic nuclear power remains governed by the *Australian Radiation Protection and Nuclear Safety Act 1998* and the *Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999*, both of which effectively prohibit civil nuclear power plants. AUKUS does not change this legal architecture.

**For Australia to translate AUKUS into civilian nuclear energy capacity**, it would need: civil nuclear power legislation (currently absent), reactor construction and operation experience (absent), a domestic nuclear fuel cycle (absent), and — for thorium specifically — reprocessing and advanced fuel fabrication infrastructure (absent everywhere except India at research scale). The gap between "Australia is acquiring nuclear-powered submarines" and "Australia can activate its thorium reserves for energy-backed monetary purposes" is not acknowledged.

**The AUKUS falsification condition as stated is technically incoherent.** If Australia's AUD reserve share stagnates despite AUKUS, this is not surprising — AUKUS has no thorium relevance. The falsification condition should either be dropped or completely reconceptualised.

## 5. Thorium Reserve Data — Accuracy and Conflation

### 5.1 The Reserve/Resource Conflation

For uranium, "reserves" has operational meaning: uranium is actively mined, priced, and traded, and "economically extractable at current prices" is a meaningful filter. For thorium, there is no commercial market, no spot price, and therefore no economic threshold at which "reserves" are distinguished from "resources."

The USGS thorium figures are better understood as **identified resources** rather than "reserves" in the economic sense. All thorium producers report thorium content of monazite deposits without a market price against which to assess economic recoverability. The paper's TMPI uses "thorium reserve share" as if this were analogous to an oil or uranium reserve — a material conceptual error. Use "identified thorium resources" throughout and acknowledge that the concept of a "reserve" is not strictly applicable to a fuel with no commercial market.

### 5.2 China's Underreporting Problem

The paper assigns China 2.1% of world thorium reserves. This figure is likely a significant underestimate. China's thorium is predominantly co-located with the Bayan Obo rare earth deposit in Inner Mongolia, currently mined primarily for REE and iron ore. Thorium is a byproduct that China currently treats as radioactive waste requiring management under its 2018 NORM regulations.

Independent estimates of China's thorium resource endowment range from 500,000 to 2 million tonnes, depending on methodology. If China's actual thorium endowment is at the high end of independent estimates, China's reserve share could be 15-20% of world total — comparable to India's. The paper should acknowledge this uncertainty and its implications for the TMPI ranking. A sensitivity analysis is warranted.

This is not a minor data footnote. If China's thorium endowment is 10× the reported USGS figure, and China already has the world's most advanced thorium reactor programme, then the paper's core finding — India as preeminent thorium-backed monetary power — becomes considerably more ambiguous.

## 6. Uranium vs Thorium as Next-Era Energy Input

### 6.1 Is Uranium Actually Widely Distributed?

The paper's claim that uranium "fails the first condition" because "reserves are too widely distributed" is factually questionable.

Uranium *production* is highly concentrated: Kazakhstan approximately 43%, Canada approximately 15%, Namibia approximately 11%, Australia approximately 10%, Uzbekistan approximately 7%. The top five producers account for roughly 87% of world production — more geographically concentrated than oil production.

The paper's argument needs to be framed differently: uranium fails not because it is distributed, but because the *enabling technology* (enrichment) has diffused to a sufficient number of states that the geographic concentration of uranium ore does not automatically translate into structural power for reserve-holding states. A state holding uranium ore without enrichment capacity is in a position of dependency vis-à-vis enrichment service providers.

### 6.2 The Rosatom Paradox

If the argument is that the thorium fuel cycle confers structural monetary power because it "requires sovereign governance capacity," then Rosatom's control of 70% of global enrichment services represents *exactly the kind of structural power* the paper is theorising — applied to uranium, by Russia, right now. Russia's TMPI of 9.3 dramatically underweights Rosatom's actual structural power position in the nuclear knowledge structure. A theoretically consistent application of Strange's framework would give Russia very high nuclear knowledge structure power — arguably higher than the USA — based on its enrichment dominance and fuel fabrication market share. The paper's TMPI formula cannot capture this because it includes no term for nuclear knowledge structure capacity (enrichment, fuel fabrication, reactor design, operator training, waste management).

## 7. Non-Proliferation and Thorium

### 7.1 U-233 Weapons Potential

The paper's engagement with non-proliferation is inadequate for a paper published in 2026 arguing that state-level thorium fuel cycle development should be understood as a legitimate pathway to structural monetary power.

U-233 is a **weapons-usable fissile material**. The critical mass for an implosion device is approximately 5-6 kg. The IAEA classifies U-233 as a direct-use material under INFCIRC/153 safeguards — the same category as Pu-239. The US closed its U-233 inventory under a disposition programme partly on non-proliferation grounds.

A paper that argues thorium fuel cycle development is a legitimate pathway to structural monetary power, and that the fuel cycle "requires sovereign governance capacity," should engage directly with the implication: that the capacity being described is also a latent weapons-relevant capacity. The phrase "sovereign governance capacity" may be read as a diplomatic euphemism for "weapons-usable fissile material production."

### 7.2 IAEA Safeguards for Thorium Cycles

The IAEA's safeguards system was designed primarily with uranium and plutonium cycles in mind. For thorium fuel cycles:
- Measurement techniques for U-233 inventory verification are less mature than for U-235 or Pu-239
- The intermediate material Pa-233 has no standard safeguards accounting category
- THOREX reprocessing is not covered by standard safeguards facility attachments that have been operationally tested

The paper describes the thorium fuel cycle's governance requirements as an asset. A more complete treatment would acknowledge that IAEA safeguards coverage of thorium cycles is less developed, creating a potential verification gap.

### 7.3 Proliferation Incentive Effects

If the paper's core argument is correct — that thorium fuel cycle development confers monetary leverage — then the paper inadvertently argues that there is a monetary *incentive* for states to develop thorium fuel cycle capacity. Combined with inadequate IAEA safeguards coverage of U-233, this creates a potential proliferation incentive that the paper does not acknowledge.

## 8. Rosatom's Knowledge Structure

Rosatom's actual position:
- Approximately 70% of world uranium enrichment services (SWU capacity)
- Approximately 40% of world nuclear fuel fabrication capacity (TVEL subsidiary)
- Construction contracts in 36 countries
- The only commercial operating fast reactor in the world (BN-800, commissioned 2016; BN-1200 under construction)
- The only commercial operating floating nuclear power plant
- The world's only commercial operating lead-fast reactor programme (BREST-OD-300, under construction at Seversk)

By Strange's knowledge structure framework, Russia's position in the nuclear knowledge structure is arguably more powerful than any other state, including the United States. TMPI = 9.3, placing Russia below India, Canada, and the USA, is a result that should strike any nuclear policy expert as implausible.

**BREST-OD-300:** The BREST-OD-300 at Seversk is a 300 MWe lead-cooled fast reactor. As of 2024-2025, reactor building construction is reportedly complete and equipment installation is in progress. BREST uses UN (uranium nitride) fuel and is designed for a closed uranium fuel cycle with on-site reprocessing. Russia has no active thorium fuel cycle programme in the experimental reactor category, but BREST's closed fast reactor cycle is directly applicable to thorium utilisation if thorium blankets are added.

## 9. What the Paper Gets Right

**The Thorium Uniqueness Argument Has a Basis:** The paper is correct that thorium fuel cycles are meaningfully different from uranium fuel cycles in ways that could confer sovereignty advantages: U-232 contamination in bred U-233 genuinely makes fuel fabrication difficult to outsource; THOREX reprocessing is underdeveloped commercially; there is no commercial thorium fuel supply chain. These are real constraints, correctly identified.

**India's Three-Stage Programme Is Strategically Significant:** No other state has a comparably integrated policy and programme for achieving a closed thorium fuel cycle at civilian scale. India's non-NPT status has paradoxically fostered indigenous fuel cycle development that NPT states have not been compelled to develop.

**Thorium Reserve Geographic Concentration:** The observation that thorium reserves are geographically concentrated in a different set of states than uranium reserves, with monetary-political implications, is a genuine insight not yet systematically developed in the literature.

**PFBR Delays as Evidence:** The paper's acknowledgment of PFBR's 14-year delay is honest and appropriate. The self-critical inclusion of delay evidence strengthens rather than weakens the paper's credibility.

## 10. Overall Verdict and Required Revisions

**Score: 4/10. Recommendation: Major Revisions Required.**

**Errors requiring correction before resubmission:**

1. **PFBR thorium blanket claim:** "Stage 2 produces plutonium that breeds U-233 from thorium" misrepresents the PFBR's basecase design (depleted uranium blanket, not thorium). Correct or qualify.

2. **20% nuclear by 2035:** Not consistent with any credible analysis of India's construction pipeline. The required ~190 GW of new nuclear capacity in nine years is physically impossible. Source this claim accurately or remove it.

3. **AUKUS thorium linkage:** The falsification condition connecting AUKUS to Australian thorium activation is technically incoherent. AUKUS submarines use HEU naval propulsion; no civil nuclear component, no thorium component. Must be substantially reconceived or removed.

4. **Reserve/resource conflation:** "Thorium reserves" does not have the same operational meaning as oil or uranium reserves for a fuel with no commercial market. Revise throughout to "identified thorium resources."

5. **China's thorium endowment:** The 2.1% USGS figure likely understates China's actual endowment by a factor of 5-10 due to strategic non-reporting of co-located REE/thorium deposits. A sensitivity analysis is required.

6. **TMPI knowledge structure gap:** The formula contains no term for nuclear knowledge structure capacity. This causes Russia (TMPI 9.3) to be ranked below India, Canada, and the USA despite holding structurally dominant positions in enrichment, fuel fabrication, and reactor construction globally. The formula requires either revision or explicit defence of the decision to exclude knowledge structure.

7. **Non-proliferation engagement:** For a paper arguing that state thorium fuel cycle development confers monetary leverage, the non-proliferation implications of U-233 production must be addressed substantively.
