## Prefatory Assessment

This paper has genuine intellectual ambition. The MPI framework is internally coherent, the three-register structure is a sensible epistemological architecture, and the India forward prediction is specific enough to be falsifiable. However, from the perspective of an Australia specialist, the AUKUS analysis contains a technical error of sufficient seriousness that it compromises the credibility of the broader argument. The Australia case is the paper's primary negative prediction and anchors the Katzenstein filter as a general mechanism. A flawed technical foundation for the anchor case weakens the filter's credibility throughout.

---

## 1. AUKUS Nuclear Analysis: A Category Error at the Heart of the Framework

The paper states that AUKUS "embeds Australian nuclear capacity in the US security orbit rather than activating it for independent monetary leverage" and predicts a "step-change at approximately 2035 via AUKUS." The framing implies AUKUS is the mechanism by which Australia develops nuclear *energy* capacity activating the TMPI formula. **This is technically incorrect.**

The AUKUS Pillar One agreement concerns the acquisition of Virginia-class nuclear-powered attack submarines (SSN-AUKUS). These are naval propulsion systems. They are fuelled by highly enriched uranium (HEU) supplied by the United States and the United Kingdom under a bespoke arrangement negotiated with the IAEA. The fuel cycle is controlled entirely by the supplying states. Australia will not enrich, fabricate, or reprocess the fuel.

**Two direct consequences:**

First, AUKUS submarines do not engage with thorium at all. The relevant zero in the TMPI is civil nuclear energy share — the fraction of primary energy from grid-connected reactors. AUKUS SSNs produce shaft power for submarine propulsion, not electricity. AUKUS does not change Australia's civilian nuclear energy share, does not utilise Australia's thorium reserves, and does not move Australia's TMPI from zero by any mechanism specified in the paper's framework.

Second, even under a generous reading — that AUKUS creates political conditions for future civil nuclear capacity — the timeline is considerably more speculative than "approximately 2035." Australia has no operating civil reactors, no civilian nuclear workforce at scale, no reactor licensing pathway in current legislation, and no identified sites for civil power plants. The bipartisan prohibition on nuclear power remains unresolved in terms of enabling legislation as of 2026. The earliest credible projection for first civil nuclear power generation in Australia, even on an accelerated post-AUKUS scenario, is the early 2040s.

**The deeper strategic error:** The HEU arrangement under AUKUS was specifically designed to prevent Australia from developing an independent fuel cycle. Part of the IAEA negotiation was precisely the assurance that Australia would not acquire enrichment capability. The agreement structurally prevents the kind of sovereign fuel cycle independence that the paper's framework identifies as the prerequisite for monetary leverage. AUKUS makes Australian nuclear capacity more, not less, dependent on the United States — the opposite of the energy sovereignty the paper models.

**Required correction:** Either (a) correct the technical description to make clear AUKUS concerns propulsion, not civil energy, and remove the 2035 civil nuclear claim; or (b) construct an explicit argument for how AUKUS *indirectly* creates conditions for civil nuclear capacity — identifying the legislative pathway, workforce pipeline, and a defensible timeline.

---

### Revision 1 verdict: ✅ FULLY ADDRESSED in draft v4

Draft v4 §7.4 corrects the technical error completely. The text now states explicitly: "AUKUS involves Virginia-class and SSN-AUKUS submarines using highly enriched uranium (HEU) propulsion supplied by the United States... entirely distinct from any civil thorium pathway." ## 2. Commodity Currency Status: An Underspecified Mechanism

The paper approvingly cites Arslanalp, Eichengreen, and Simpson-Bell (2022) on AUD's rise as a non-traditional reserve currency, attributing it to "commodity endowment and institutional openness." This is correct as description. What the paper does not provide is a mechanism connecting Australia's current commodity-driven reserve niche to the thorium-era scenario.

Australia's existing AUD reserve status is driven by LNG, coal, and iron ore exports with institutional quality (WGI = 0.85) making Australian assets credible stores of value. This is a fossil fuel and metals story, not a nuclear story.

**The present-era MPI inconsistency:** If Australia's NEP is strongly positive (Australia exports ~80 EJ/year in fossil fuels, consumes ~25 EJ domestically — one of the highest NEPs in the dataset), then the present-era MPI term (1−p_t)·[NEP·GS] should predict continued gradual AUD reserve share growth. The paper's stagnation prediction therefore needs to be more precisely specified: is it a claim that AUD share will stop rising even in the present era, or only that it will not jump discontinuously? The falsification condition (±0.5 pp from 6.7%) is tight enough that a continued gradual trend could itself falsify the prediction within the 2035–2040 window.

**The fossil fuel NEP cliff:** As global coal and LNG demand peaks and declines (IEA Net Zero scenario: coal demand peaking ~2023, LNG demand peaking ~2030), Australia's strongly positive NEP in fossil fuel terms will deteriorate sharply. The MPI framework predicts this should produce a corresponding decline in AUD reserve share with the appropriate lag in the 2030–2040 window. The paper does not make this prediction explicitly. It should. It is a falsifiable implication of the model applied to Australia, and it connects to the zero TMPI — Australia faces both a fossil fuel NEP decline and zero nuclear share, creating potential vulnerability to a step-down in reserve niche precisely when the paper is most interested in the transition.

### Revision 3 verdict: ✅ FULLY ADDRESSED in draft v4

The resolution — fossil fuel exports are dollar-invoiced by convention, recycling surpluses into dollar assets rather than AUD assets — is stated clearly. A new "fossil fuel NEP cliff" subsection explicitly models the deterioration scenario with IEA demand-peak citations and makes the AUD niche *decline* prediction for the late 2030s–2040s. ## 3. Realism and the Strategic Logic of AUKUS

From Canberra's perspective, AUKUS is not primarily about embedding Australia in the US orbit; it is about hedging against a scenario where the US security guarantee becomes unreliable. Acquisition of Virginia-class SSNs gives Australia a capability that no ally can switch off by political decision, because ownership and crewing are Australian. ASPI consistently frames AUKUS as a sovereignty-enhancement tool.

More directly relevant: the paper never specifies the *institutional pathway* by which US security embedding prevents AUD invoicing expansion. If the argument is that LNG is priced in USD by convention, that is a current-era fact but not a structural law. As Asian LNG markets develop and AUD-denominated long-term supply contracts become available, the invoicing norm constraint could loosen independently of any AUKUS nuclear development.

The deeper strategic question: If AUKUS, in a scenario where the US security guarantee weakens over the 2030s, could paradoxically become a foundation for *more* independent Australian strategic positioning, the Katzenstein filter's application as stable through the prediction period requires defending.

---

### Revision 2 verdict: ✅ ADDRESSED in draft v4 — with pushback on the ASPI sovereignty framing

Draft v4 §7.4 specifies the mechanism: "Australian fossil fuel exports are priced and settled in US dollars by market convention. The export surplus is therefore recycled into dollar-denominated assets rather than AUD-denominated ones — the same mechanism as the petrodollar recycling case." The platform-ownership vs fuel-cycle-sovereignty distinction directly addresses the ASPI sovereignty framing: the paper acknowledges Australia has genuine operational sovereignty over the submarines while arguing that the relevant mechanism operates at the fuel cycle layer, not the platform layer.

**Pushback on the reviewer's ASPI sovereignty argument:** The paper holds its position that AUKUS is a sovereignty-enhancement tool *for naval operations* (conceded), not for energy-monetary architecture (contested). The reviewer's suggestion that AUKUS could become a foundation for more independent strategic positioning if the US guarantee weakens is acknowledged as a structural stress test of the filter — and the paper's falsification condition (§8.3: stagnation regardless of AUKUS progression) would be the test vehicle. The paper does not need to pre-commit on this scenario; the falsification registry handles it.

**Gap remaining:** The paper does not cite ASPI reports directly for the civil nuclear timeline claim (no enabling legislation, early 2040s at earliest). This citation support is missing and should be added (see Revision 5 below).

## 4. Liberalism: Institutional Quality as Undervalued Standalone Pathway

Australia has WGI = 0.85 — the highest in the dataset. The paper acknowledges this but the TMPI's zero-collapse property overrides it entirely. However, Helleiner (2014), Keohane (1984), and Arslanalp et al. (2022) all identify institutional quality as an independent driver of reserve currency niche expansion, separate from energy endowment. If institutional quality has independent explanatory power for AUD reserve share — as the Arslanalp et al. paper directly suggests — then the MPI's multiplicative structure (WGI enters only as a component of TMPI, multiplying a zero nuclear share to produce zero) may systematically undervalue Australia's position.

The paper should either incorporate institutional quality as a partial standalone predictor in the present-era term or explicitly argue why WGI contributes to AUD gains only through the commodity channel.

---

### Revision 7 verdict: ✅ ADDRESSED in draft v4

§7.4 now contains an explicit paragraph ("Australia's WGI = 0.85 and the institutional quality objection") between the NEP cliff and UK mootness sections. The argument: WGI = 0.85 contributes to AUD reserve demand through the commodity channel, not as a standalone predictor — the operative mechanism is (institutional quality × commodity endowment) → credible store of value → reserve demand. The Switzerland/NZ cross-section is deployed to support this (both have equal or superior WGI but lack commodity anchor). The UK contrast (City of London premium through financial depth, not commodity endowment) confirms the standalone channel has a distinct structural basis Australia lacks. A forward falsification test is registered: AUD share persisting above 6.5% post-2045 despite fossil fuel export decline would constitute evidence that the standalone WGI channel is operative — consistent with the symmetric downside condition added to §8.3.

## 5. Falsification Scrutiny

The falsification condition (AUD reserve share within ±0.5 pp of 6.7% through 2040) has several problems:

1. **Baseline uncertainty:** The 6.7% figure uses shadow allocation methods (Arslanalp et al.). Measurement uncertainty is approximately ±0.3 pp, leaving a very narrow window for meaningful falsification.

2. **Two claims conflated:** (a) AUKUS specifically does not produce AUD reserve gains, and (b) AUD reserve share stagnates overall. A stagnation due to fossil fuel NEP deterioration would not falsify the Katzenstein filter per se. The predictions need to be disentangled.

3. **Asymmetric structure:** Only a jump of more than 1 pp falsifies the prediction. Over any 5-year period, this requires a substantial observable shift specifically attributable to Australian nuclear capacity — a very high bar. The condition is structured to be nearly immune to falsification over the relevant horizon.

**A more demanding falsification condition would specify:** (a) AUD reserve share does not rise above 8% by 2040 regardless of AUKUS developments; (b) any AUD gains in the 2030s are explained by commodity diversification or institutional quality, not nuclear capacity; (c) the AUD/INR reserve share trajectory diverges in the predicted direction — INR growing, AUD flat.

---

### Falsification verdict: ✅ PARTIALLY ADDRESSED in draft v4

The "two claims conflated" problem (§5.2) is resolved: §8.3 adds "(regardless of AUKUS progression)" to cleanly separate the AUKUS-specific claim from the general AUD trajectory prediction. The AUD/INR divergence sharpener is added as a "secondary sharpener" in §8.3.

**Measurement uncertainty (§5.1) addressed:** The ±0.3 pp measurement uncertainty in the Arslanalp et al. shadow allocation data is now disclosed in §8.3 AUD claim cell, with the effective signal band for falsification stated as approximately ±0.2 pp net of data noise.

**Asymmetric structure (§5.3) addressed:** A symmetric downside condition is now added to §8.3: AUD share declining below 6.0% in 2035–2040 absent a demonstrated fossil fuel demand collapse would falsify the NEP cliff timeline; AUD share persisting above 6.5% post-2045 despite material fossil fuel export decline would indicate the standalone WGI channel is operative (recorded as a live test tied to §7.4).

## Pushback Summary

The paper holds its ground on two points the reviewer contested:

**1. ASPI's sovereignty framing of AUKUS.** The reviewer argues ASPI frames AUKUS as a sovereignty-enhancement tool and that the paper's embedding narrative is incomplete. The paper's response (implicit in §7.4) is that ASPI's sovereignty analysis concerns *platform and operational sovereignty*, which the paper explicitly concedes. The paper's mechanism operates at the *fuel cycle* layer — and at that layer, AUKUS unambiguously deepens dependence. The two frameworks are not in conflict; they address different dimensions of sovereignty.

**2. AUKUS as potential foundation for future independent positioning.** The reviewer raises the scenario where a weakening US guarantee makes AUKUS a platform for greater autonomy. The paper treats this as a genuine structural risk to the filter prediction — and registers it as falsifiable via the §8.3 condition rather than trying to pre-emptively argue it away. This is the correct posture: if AUD rises above the falsification threshold in a period of US-Australia security friction, the data will tell the story.
