## Prefatory Note

This is a paper with genuine ambitions. The MPI framework is theoretically coherent, the falsification logic is appropriately specified, and the Russia section demonstrates unusual care in distinguishing coercion-driven corridor formation from structurally-grounded energy geography. The Mechanism A / Mechanism B distinction is exactly the right analytical move. I will therefore engage seriously rather than dismissively. The critiques below are substantive and, in my assessment, addressable with revisions — but several are non-trivial.

---

## 1. The WGI Institutional Quality Problem

**The paper uses WGI (Russia = 0.40) as its institutional quality proxy for the GS term, and WGI is doing enormous work here.** Russia's flat-near-zero MPI trajectory is primarily WGI-driven, not energy-driven. The authors acknowledge this directly — "governance quality score (WGI = 0.40) is what depresses its TMPI" — which is intellectually honest but raises a deeper problem they do not fully confront.

**The measurement validity problem.** WGI aggregates perceptions-based surveys administered largely by Western institutions (World Bank, Freedom House, PRS Group). Kurtz and Schrank (2007, *World Politics*) demonstrated that WGI scores correlate heavily with GDP per capita, conflating wealth effects with institutional quality. For Russia specifically, post-2022 WGI scores embed punitive sentiment from Western respondents that is indistinguishable from measurement of actual governance capacity.

**What the MPI actually needs** is a measure of the capacity to convert energy sovereignty into monetary credibility. The relevant dimensions are: (a) contract enforcement in energy transactions, (b) financial regulatory capacity, (c) central bank independence and credibility, (d) bilateral treaty-enforcement capacity. Russia scores poorly on WGI's rule-of-law subscales but scores adequately-to-well on dimensions (a) and (d) in bilateral contexts: Gazprom contracts were honoured for decades; Rosatom's sovereign-to-sovereign construction contracts have an essentially unblemished completion record.

**The deeper problem.** Russia demonstrably *did* convert gas leverage into monetary corridors in 2022 at speed. The ruble-yuan trading volume on the Moscow Exchange rose from near-negligible to approximately 40–45% of all ruble FX transactions by late 2022 (Bank of Russia data). If the WGI-derived GS score suppresses the GS term to near-zero, and yet Russia formed functioning bilateral corridors, one of three things must be true: (i) the GS term is mis-specified; (ii) Mechanism A is doing more work than the model captures; or (iii) the paper's distinction between "functioning bilateral corridor" and "reserve currency credibility" is doing necessary but insufficiently theorised work. I believe (iii) is correct — bilateral corridors are not reserve status — but the paper needs to be far more explicit that WGI is being used as a proxy for *reserve credibility* specifically, not governance capacity in general, and that this distinction is load-bearing.

**Minimum revision required:** A sensitivity analysis using alternative institutional proxies — ICRG financial risk ratings, IMF Article IV compliance scores, or bilateral treaty-enforcement indices — is necessary to establish that the GS ≈ 0 finding is robust rather than a WGI artefact.

## 2. Rosatom as Knowledge Structure: The Paper's Most Significant Gap

Susan Strange's knowledge structure argument (*States and Markets*, 1988) is that control over knowledge — the production and distribution of technically complex capabilities — is a primary pillar of structural power. The paper invokes Strange in §2.2 but then fails to apply the knowledge structure concept to the state that most obviously exemplifies it in the nuclear domain.

**Rosatom's position is without precedent in the civilian nuclear industry.** As of 2025–2026:
- ~70% of global uranium enrichment capacity
- ~40% of global nuclear fuel fabrication
- Active reactor construction contracts in over 30 countries across four continents
- Portfolio includes: Akkuyu (Turkey, first unit operational 2023), El Dabaa (Egypt, 4×1200 MW), Kudankulam Units 3–6 (India), Rooppur (Bangladesh), Paks II (Hungary — inside the EU)

**Russia is building nuclear reactors inside NATO member states.** When Egypt finances El Dabaa through a Russian state loan, repayable over 35 years through electricity revenues, this creates a structured dependency that Strange would immediately recognise as knowledge-structure leverage. Egypt cannot operate or refuel those reactors without Russian cooperation. The fuel cycle is controlled at every stage — mining, conversion, enrichment, fabrication, spent fuel repatriation — by Russian state enterprises.

**Why does Canada (TMPI = 20.8) outscore Russia (9.3) on TMPI?** The paper owes an explicit answer. Canada has substantial uranium reserves and thorium deposits, but it has no comparable nuclear export programme. The TMPI formula, as constructed, is measuring *reserves and basic governance* rather than *nuclear knowledge structure*, and therefore systematically undervalues Russia's forward position in the thorium transition.

**BREST-OD-300 and the fast reactor frontier.** Russia's BREST-OD-300 fast reactor (lead-cooled, closed fuel cycle, operational at Seversk) represents the most advanced closed nuclear fuel cycle programme globally. The BN-800 at Beloyarsk and its successor BN-1200 represent a parallel fast-reactor lineage. Russia's ability to operate commercial-scale fast reactors — essential for thorium utilisation at scale — is a genuine first-mover advantage that no other state currently matches.

**Minimum revision required:** Either (a) revise the TMPI scoring methodology to incorporate nuclear export programme scope and knowledge-structure leverage; or (b) acknowledge explicitly that TMPI measures reserves and basic governance rather than nuclear knowledge structure, and therefore systematically undervalues Russia's forward position. Option (b) requires a new limitation paragraph discussing what a knowledge-structure-adjusted score would imply for Russia's MPI trajectory.

## 3. Ruble-Yuan and Ruble-Rupee Corridors: 2026 Status

The paper's falsification logic is sound, but the empirical update is incomplete.

**Ruble-yuan: structural deepening at the sovereign level, commercial friction at the bank level.** By 2024–2025, yuan-denominated trade invoicing covered approximately 90% of some Russia-China bilateral commodity categories. However, Chinese commercial banks — including the four major state banks — significantly curtailed ruble-denominated correspondent banking relationships through 2023–2024 due to secondary sanctions risk. The PBoC and Russian Central Bank maintained the swap line architecture, but mid-tier Chinese banks pulled back.

**Theoretically important result:** Mechanism A (energy geography) sustained the sovereign infrastructure; Mechanism B (coercion) created headwinds at the commercial level. The paper's binary A/B framing may need to become a *layered model* in which different institutional levels respond to different mechanisms.

**Ruble-rupee: corridor formation has stalled, possibly failed.** This is the more challenging case for the paper. By 2023–2024, India accumulated large ruble balances (reports of $1–2 billion in frozen accounts with Russian state banks) that Russia could not clear because India had no equivalent demand for Russian exports. India subsequently shifted substantially to dollar-denominated pricing for Russian oil imports, including through UAE-based intermediaries. As of early 2026, the formalised ruble-rupee corridor is largely non-functional as a bilateral mechanism.

**This is significant:** It suggests Mechanism A (energy geography) is necessary but not sufficient for corridor formation — bilateral trade symmetry or a recycling mechanism is also required (which the paper's Channel 2 discusses but does not connect explicitly to this case).

**Minimum revision required:** A 2025–2026 empirical update on both corridors, with the ruble-rupee stalling treated as a potential partial falsification of the pure Mechanism A argument. The layered institutional structure of the ruble-yuan corridor should be theorised.

## 4. Realism: The Separability Hypothesis

From a structural realist perspective, Russia's post-2022 behaviour is exactly what we would expect: a great power using energy leverage instrumentally for geopolitical ends. But the paper claims something more ambitious — that energy geography creates *monetary* power, not merely geopolitical leverage. The evidence is awkward.

The ruble's share of global FX reserves remains below 0.5% (IMF COFER). The ruble's share of global trade invoicing is negligible. Russia's SPFS payment system operates in approximately 70 countries but carries a small fraction of global transaction volume. None of these facts suggest that energy leverage is translating into monetary power in any conventional sense.

**The Gaddy-Ickes parallel.** Gaddy and Ickes argued in the 1990s that Russia's industrial economy was partially fictitious — value-destroying enterprises sustained through non-monetary exchange and cross-subsidisation from the energy sector. There is an analogous structure in Russia's current monetary position: the energy sector generates hard currency, but that currency is increasingly trapped in bilateral arrangements that are not fungible in global markets. Russian energy revenues in yuan cannot be freely deployed; they circulate within bilateral frameworks. This is a kind of monetary "virtual economy" — energy leverage generates nominal monetary flows lacking the convertibility and fungibility that define genuine monetary power.

**The separability thesis.** The paper would benefit from directly confronting the hypothesis that energy leverage and monetary power are causally separable, and that Russia is the test case demonstrating this separability. Russia has maximal energy leverage by the paper's own metrics but minimal monetary power outcomes. If the MPI is correct, Russia should be converting energy to monetary power. If Russia is not doing so, either (a) the GS term is correctly suppressing the conversion (the paper's current answer), (b) Mechanism A is being offset by Mechanism B, or (c) the MPI theory is wrong for this case.

The paper's answer (a) is internally consistent but raises the question: is there any plausible governance trajectory for Russia (under current political economy constraints) that would allow MPI to rise? If the answer is genuinely no, Russia becomes a case study in energy-monetary *non-conversion*, and the paper should frame it explicitly as such — with implications for other authoritarian energy states (Iran, Venezuela, Saudi Arabia pre-Vision 2030).

## 5. BRICS and Alternative Monetary Architecture

The paper analyses Russia's monetary position in 2026 without engaging with the BRICS monetary architecture discussions that Russia has been driving since at least 2022. This is a significant omission.

**The BRICS Unit.** At the 2023 Johannesburg and 2024 Kazan BRICS summits, Russia advocated strongly for a BRICS settlement currency based on a basket of member currencies and potentially commodity-anchored. The Kazan summit produced an agreement to develop alternative payment system infrastructure. Russia has a direct interest in this: a commodity-anchored BRICS settlement unit would formally institutionalise the energy-to-monetary-power conversion that the MPI claims to measure.

**A theoretical point the paper needs:** If a BRICS monetary institution emerges with commodity anchoring (even partial), it could provide *external governance scaffolding* that partially substitutes for Russia's domestic WGI deficit. The paper's model assumes GS is a domestic variable. The BRICS case suggests it may be partially exogenous and provided by multilateral institutions. The MPI formula may need modification to accommodate multilateral governance scaffolding as a partial GS substitute.

**Russia's revealed preference.** Russia's aggressive promotion of BRICS monetary alternatives — combined with development of SPFS, the Mir payment system, and bilateral currency swap lines — suggests that Russian policymakers are behaving as if energy-to-monetary conversion is achievable. The paper should engage with whether they are correct or pursuing a strategically rational but structurally constrained agenda.

## 6. Sanctions Duration and the Falsification Timeline

The paper's falsification — test whether ruble-yuan corridors persist after sanctions are lifted — is well-specified. The implicit assumption that sanctions will be lifted on a policy-relevant timescale requires scrutiny.

The Russia sanctions regime is historically unprecedented for a P5 member: multilateral (G7 + EU), includes SWIFT exclusion of major banks, asset freezes at the sovereign level (~$300 billion frozen Russian Central Bank reserves), and secondary sanctions targeting third-country entities. The political economy of lifting such a regime — particularly given frozen assets institutionalised as a "Ukraine reconstruction fund" — suggests a decades-long horizon.

**The path dependency problem.** Russian energy infrastructure has been physically redirected eastward (Power of Siberia 2 negotiations, Arctic LNG rerouting). Once pipeline and port infrastructure is redirected, the transaction costs of re-routing westward are high. This suggests Mechanism A may be self-reinforcing regardless of sanctions trajectory — the natural experiment the paper proposes may never produce clean results because the independent variable cannot be cleanly removed.

**Potential vs. actualisable TMPI.** If the sanctions regime persists for 20–30 years, Russia's TMPI = 9.3 is monetarily inert for any practical forecasting horizon. The paper should acknowledge more explicitly: the inertness is not merely a function of WGI scores but of a geopolitical lock-in that makes the governance improvement pathway structurally blocked. Russia's *potential* TMPI score is real; its *actualisable* TMPI score under any plausible sanctions scenario is near-zero. This distinction deserves explicit theorisation, with Russia as the canonical case.

## 7. Channel 2 Asset Recycling and Military Crowding-Out

The paper's Channel 2 (energy revenues → monetary infrastructure investment → reserve credibility) is under-theorised for the Russia case.

**Military spending crowding out Channel 2.** Russia's National Wealth Fund had approximately $185 billion in assets pre-2022. Post-2022, NWF assets have been substantially drawn down to finance budget deficits from military spending (approximately 6–7% of GDP, roughly 30% of the federal budget by 2024). The pattern is: energy revenues → NWF → military spending, not energy revenues → monetary infrastructure investment. **Channel 2 in Russia's case is being crowded out by military finance.** The paper's MPI model does not include a military spending crowding-out mechanism — an important gap for any energy-state analysis.

**Shadow fleet and sanctions evasion as quasi-Channel 2.** Russian energy revenues are reaching Russian accounts through sophisticated evasion networks — the "shadow fleet" of oil tankers (estimated 600+ vessels as of 2024–2025), UAE and Turkish intermediaries, and cryptocurrency-adjacent settlement mechanisms. Whether shadow-fleet-mediated revenue recycling contributes to monetary infrastructure investment is an open question, but the paper's clean Channel 2 model is not capturing the actual institutional architecture.

## 8. Required Revisions

**Non-negotiable:**
1. WGI sensitivity analysis using ICRG financial risk ratings or equivalent monetary-credibility-appropriate institutional measures. GS ≈ 0 for Russia must be shown to be robust to alternative measures, not a WGI artefact.
2. Rosatom knowledge structure discussion with explicit TMPI implications — either revise TMPI scoring or acknowledge the current formula measures reserves and governance rather than nuclear knowledge structure.
3. Empirical update on ruble-rupee corridor stalling (2023–2025), treating it as a potential partial falsification of Mechanism A's sufficiency conditions.
4. BRICS monetary architecture sub-section. The paper's framework speaks directly to BRICS feasibility and the omission is conspicuous in a 2026 paper on multipolar monetary failure.
5. Military-spending crowding-out of Channel 2: Russia's revealed preference is military finance over monetary infrastructure investment.

**Recommended:**
6. Potential vs. actualisable TMPI distinction, with Russia as the canonical case of a state whose potential score is structurally blocked from actualisation.
7. Layered analysis of ruble-yuan corridor (sovereign vs. commercial bank levels) replacing the current binary characterisation.
8. Direct engagement with the separability hypothesis: Russia may be demonstrating that energy leverage and monetary power are separable variables.

---

*The Russia case is, despite the revisions required, the most illuminating section of the paper. The Mechanism A / B distinction is analytically correct and genuinely novel. Revise and resubmit with confidence.*
