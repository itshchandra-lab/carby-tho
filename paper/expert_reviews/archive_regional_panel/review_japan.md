## Prefatory Assessment

This is an intellectually serious paper with a bold central claim. The Japan case is genuinely the most analytically interesting material in the present register — not because it provides the "cleanest" identification (a claim I dispute below), but because it forces the paper to confront the limits of a purely materialist account of reserve currency status. The Fukushima shock was real. The NEP decline was real. The yen's underperformance against GDP benchmarks is real. But the explanatory architecture the paper constructs is considerably thinner than the empirical density of the Japan case demands.

---

## 1. Is the Fukushima Natural Experiment Really "Clean"?

The paper's claim that Japan provides "the cleanest identification" rests on the assertion that the Fukushima shock was exogenous to "the variables the standard reserve currency literature deploys." This claim is defensible with respect to the *origin* of the shock (earthquake, regulatory response) but it conflates the origin of the shock with the *transmission environment*, which was profoundly non-neutral.

**Confound 1 — Fiscal deterioration.** The disaster precipitated the largest peacetime fiscal deterioration in Japanese postwar history outside COVID. Reconstruction spending ran to approximately ¥32 trillion over the first decade. LNG substitution costs ran to ~$30–40 billion per year in additional imports at peak (2012–2014), simultaneously collapsing Japan's current account surplus from 3.7% of GDP (2010) to 0.5% (2013). Current account collapse is an equally plausible mechanism for reserve currency stagnation, operating independently of NEP, and the paper does not attempt to disentangle them.

**Confound 2 — Abenomics/BOJ QQE.** The paper's Japan identification runs from the 2011 shock to changes in yen reserve share. The entire post-2013 period is dominated by the Bank of Japan's Quantitative and Qualitative Easing programme under Governor Kuroda — the most aggressive central bank balance sheet expansion in G7 history relative to GDP, deliberately engineering yen depreciation as a reflation mechanism. The yen fell ~35% against the dollar between mid-2012 and late 2015. A currency undergoing deliberate structural depreciation is mechanically less attractive as a reserve asset. The paper cannot claim "standard predictors have not deteriorated" while ignoring that the BOJ's explicit policy objective during the identification window was to make the yen a less attractive store of value.

**Confound 3 — MOF anti-reserve currency policy (the most significant omission).** The most important omission in the paper's Japan case is its complete silence on the MOF's long-standing and explicitly articulated policy of *discouraging yen internationalisation*. The MOF expressed systematic concern through the 1980s and 1990s that reserve currency status would compromise its exchange rate management capacity and subject Japan to permanently overvalued exchange rate risks. Takashi Hosomi at the MOF articulated this explicitly in the 1970s. Japan's own "Blueprint for the Internationalisation of the Yen" (1999) was widely read as a reluctant concession to US pressure rather than a genuine strategic reorientation.

William Grimes (2003) — absent from this bibliography — documents in *Unmaking the Japanese Miracle* how the MOF's institutional preferences consistently subordinated yen internationalisation to domestic monetary management priorities. If the yen's reserve share is 5–6% rather than the GDP-predicted 10–15%, the most parsimonious explanation is that Japan's own monetary authorities worked for decades to prevent exactly the reserve currency status the paper treats as unexplained. This is a direct rival theory with substantial empirical support, and the paper must engage it.

**The Plaza Accord legacy.** The yen's reserve underperformance begins in earnest in the early 1990s — well before Fukushima. The managed yen appreciation of 1985–1987, producing a 100% strengthening against the dollar, triggered the Bubble Economy, the Lost Decade, and a structural determination by Japanese policymakers that currency misalignment via reserve status posed an existential economic risk. The paper should test whether the yen's reserve share trajectory *changes* at Fukushima relative to its post-Plaza trend.

## 2. Realism and the Security-Monetary Linkage: The Kishida Doctrine Gap

The paper's claim — "Japan imports 97% of its energy and depends on the US security guarantee in the Pacific; its monetary subordination is inseparable from this energy-security dependence" — is descriptively accurate but analytically static.

The Kishida doctrine (December 2022 National Security Strategy revision) committed Japan to doubling its defence budget to 2% of GDP by 2027, acquiring counterstrike capabilities, and repositioning itself as a forward power projection partner. This represents the most significant transformation of Japan's security posture since the 1947 Constitution. If Japan's monetary subordination is a function of security dependence, and Japan's security posture is becoming substantially more autonomous (in capacity terms), the model should address whether the security-monetary linkage changes. The paper is silent on this.

---

## 3. Forward Prediction: Falsification Conditions Are Missing

The paper predicts "a gradual recovery in yen reserve share from approximately 2025 to 2030, conditional on nuclear restart proceeding as planned." As of March 2026, 14 of 33 operable reactors have been restarted (~10 GW), against a pre-Fukushima total of ~48 GW. The 2035 target of 20% nuclear electricity share requires restarting ~25–30 reactors plus new construction — a timeline METI's own assessments acknowledge is at risk (notably Kashiwazaki-Kariwa remains blocked by Niigata Prefecture despite NRA safety clearance).

The forward prediction is stated in directional terms ("gradual recovery") with a conditional ("as planned"). **This is not a falsification condition.** A rigorous condition would read: "If Japan fails to restore nuclear capacity to 15% or more of electricity generation by 2030, and yen reserve share remains at or below 6.5% in the 2028 IMF COFER data, the model's Japan prediction is confirmed null."

The India BIS 2028 condition is specified precisely. Japan deserves at least equivalent rigour given it is the paper's primary natural experiment case.

---

## 4. Japan's GX Plan Is Invisible to TMPI

Japan's Green Transformation (GX) basic policy (February 2023) involves ¥150 trillion in investment over ten years, explicitly prioritising nuclear (restart + new construction including SMRs), hydrogen, and ammonia co-firing alongside renewables. Japan is the world's most advanced developer of hydrogen co-firing technology for thermal power. Japan is collaborating with NuScale and Westinghouse on SMR development.

The paper's binary frame (present era / thorium era) cannot accommodate Japan's actual position: a state with negative NEP pursuing a multi-decade hybrid energy sovereignty strategy. The TMPI framework — calibrated entirely to thorium endowment — cannot register the ammonia co-firing pathway, the hydrogen import strategy (primarily from Australia via JERA/JOGMEC supply agreements), or the SMR programme. The forward register systematically understates Japan's potential energy sovereignty improvement because it recognises only one pathway (sovereign thorium cycle).

---

## 5. BOJ ZIRP and the Credibility Mechanism

The paper's transmission mechanism runs energy position → monetary credibility. Japan maintained zero or negative interest rates for approximately 25 years — the longest sustained ZIRP/NIRP in modern central banking — during a period of extreme energy dependence. The claim that energy dependence constrains monetary policy would predict Japan should have been unable to sustain loose monetary policy during LNG price spikes of 2012–2015, the oil recovery of 2017–2019, and the energy surge of 2022–2023. Japan ran zero rates through all of these and finally moved off ZIRP in March 2024 for domestic wage-price reasons.

The paper should either (a) theorise why Japan's zero rates are a *manifestation* of energy dependence (loose policy as a dollar-recycling, carry-trade-enabling structure) rather than a falsification of it; or (b) acknowledge the apparent tension with the credibility mechanism and specify more precisely what the mechanism predicts about interest rate *levels*, not just reserve shares.

---

## 6. Katzenstein Filter: Deliberate Choice vs. Structural Dependence

The paper applies Katzenstein's small-states framework to the world's third-largest economy without methodological justification. Japan's monetary underperformance is better explained by deliberate policy choice than by structural constraint. The relevant literature — Grimes (2003) on yen internationalisation failure, Lipscy (2003) on Japan's resistance to reserve currency status, Henning (1994) on Japan's currency policy — consistently finds that Japan's monetary underperformance relative to GDP reflects institutional preference, not incapacity.

Japan's policymakers have repeatedly been presented with opportunities to pursue yen internationalisation — including the post-1997 Miyazawa Initiative for Asian monetary cooperation, which the MOF partly buried due to reserve currency status concerns — and have consistently declined. The causal story is reversed: Japan's policymakers *chose* security dependence on the US precisely to free up policy space for export-led growth, and *chose* not to internationalise the yen precisely because reserve currency status would constrain that strategy.

## 7. Missing Literature

The following omissions are material and inexcusable given the Japan case carries the paper's identification burden:

- **Samuels, Richard J. 2013.** *3.11: Disaster and Change in Japan.* Ithaca: Cornell University Press. — The definitive political analysis of Fukushima's policy consequences, including the nuclear-energy-security nexus. Its absence from a paper making Fukushima its central natural experiment is a significant scholarly gap.
- **Grimes, William W. 2003.** *Unmaking the Japanese Miracle.* Ithaca: Cornell University Press. — Essential on MOF preferences and yen internationalisation failure.
- **Lipscy, Phillip Y. 2003.** "Japan's Asian Monetary Fund Proposal." *Stanford Journal of East Asian Affairs* 3(1). — On Japan's reluctance to leverage monetary power.
- **Henning, C. Randall. 1994.** *Currencies and Politics in the United States, Germany, and Japan.* Washington: Peterson Institute.
- **Samuels, Richard J. 1987.** *The Business of the Japanese State.* Ithaca: Cornell University Press. — On Japanese energy governance; why "exogenous" shocks in Japan's energy system are never purely exogenous.
- **METI. 2024.** *Sixth Strategic Energy Plan: Interim Report.* — The operative policy document for nuclear targets the paper cites but does not reference.
- **METI. 2023.** *GX Basic Policy.* — The decarbonisation investment framework entirely absent from the paper's forward analysis.

## 8. Required Revisions

**Non-negotiable:**
1. Engage current account surplus collapse (2010–2013) and BOJ QQE (2013–2023) as alternative explanations for yen reserve share dynamics. A regression discontinuity accounting for the simultaneous BOJ policy shift would substantially strengthen the identification claim. Absent this, withdraw the "cleanest identification" claim.
2. Engage MOF anti-reserve currency policy as a rival theory. Grimes (2003) and Lipscy (2003) are direct competitors. Either demonstrate that the MOF preference cannot account for the observed pattern or acknowledge that the energy mechanism and institutional preference mechanism are observationally equivalent — which would require conceding that Japan does not provide the identification the paper claims.
3. Add a Japan-specific falsification condition at the precision level of the BIS 2028 India condition.
4. Acknowledge that the GX plan's hydrogen/ammonia/SMR pathways represent hybrid energy sovereignty strategies the TMPI cannot score.

**Recommended:**
5. Address the BOJ/ZIRP apparent tension with the credibility mechanism
6. Engage the Kishida doctrine implications for the security-monetary linkage
7. Discuss the Plaza Accord as institutional context for why yen reserve underperformance predates Fukushima by two decades
