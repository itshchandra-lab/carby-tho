## Preliminary Statement

This review is filed from the perspective of France's monetary periphery — the CFA franc zone (UEMOA and CEMAC), France's DOM-TOM overseas territories, and the Francophone African states whose energy endowments and monetary subordination constitute the most directly relevant test cases for the paper's central argument. I do not represent the French Treasury, the Banque de France, or the ECB. I represent the fourteen states whose central banks are required to deposit their foreign exchange reserves with the French Treasury as a condition of guaranteed convertibility, the overseas territories whose independence movements turn in part on control of strategic mineral reserves, and the citizens of Niger who watched their uranium leave through Arlit for fifty years before a coup attempt to reclaim it ended in financial isolation.

The paper is theoretically ambitious and technically competent. Its MPI framework is coherent and its case selection — Japan, India, China, Russia, the UK — reflects genuine analytical sophistication. But from where I sit, the paper has constructed an elaborate theory of energy-monetary power while ignoring the most explicit, most institutionalised, and most coercive example of that theory operating in colonial-extractivist form. The CFA franc zone is not a footnote to this paper's argument. It is the argument — in its most naked form. The paper's failure to engage with it renders several of its central mechanisms empirically undefended.

---

## 1. The CFA Franc Zone as the Paper's Missing Test Case

The paper's core claim is that net energy position, mediated by governance sensitivity, translates into monetary power. The Katzenstein filter explains why energy-rich states embedded in dense alliance networks channel their monetary strategies through the dominant power's financial architecture rather than developing independent monetary leverage.

The CFA franc zone is the most institutionalised example of this mechanism in existence — more explicit, more coercive, and more durable than anything the paper describes for Japan, the United Kingdom, or even post-2022 Russia.

The architecture is worth stating precisely. Two monetary unions — UEMOA (West Africa, eight states) and CEMAC (Central Africa, six states) — use currencies pegged to the euro at fixed rates (655.957 FCFA per euro) with the French Treasury guaranteeing unlimited convertibility. Until 2005, CFA zone central banks were required to deposit 65% of their foreign exchange reserves in an "Operations Account" (Compte d'opérations) held at the French Treasury. Following the 2019 "ECO reform" announcement — itself largely cosmetic — the deposit requirement was reduced to 50% and then nominally to zero, though the peg and convertibility guarantee remain. Two French representatives retained seats on the board of the BCEAO (Banque Centrale des États de l'Afrique de l'Ouest) until 2020.

Now apply the paper's MPI framework. **Niger**: uranium reserves ranked globally in the top ten (approximately 7th by production as of 2022, with Arlit operating since 1971 under SOMAÏR and COMINAK). **Gabon**: oil producer since the 1970s, OPEC member 1975–2023, with production peaking at approximately 370,000 barrels/day, providing roughly 45% of government revenues. **Guinea**: among the world's largest bauxite producers, with hydropower potential attracting Chinese BRI investment (the Souapiti and Kaleta dams). **Chad**: landlocked oil producer, with the Chad-Cameroon pipeline (World Bank-financed, 2003) representing a textbook case of energy resource extraction failing to produce monetary leverage.

Under the paper's framework, all of these states should display positive NEP values. Their energy resources are not hypothetical. Niger's uranium has been extracted for over fifty years. Gabon's oil revenues built the infrastructure of an independent state. Guinea's bauxite endowment is among the largest on the planet. And yet, monetarily, these states are structurally subordinated to the euro — not through market forces, not through the kind of voluntary alliance-embedding the paper describes for Japan, but through an explicit institutional architecture that removes monetary policy autonomy as a design feature.

The paper's MPI formula cannot explain this. It predicts positive energy-monetary leverage for states with substantial NEP and structured governance sensitivity. CFA zone states have both — and their monetary power is not merely zero but negative: they export monetary sovereignty as a structural condition of membership.

This is a direct empirical falsification test for Mechanism A (energy wealth translating into monetary leverage). The paper does not engage with it at all.

## 2. Niger Uranium and the Thorium Analogy: A Near-Perfect Natural Experiment Ignored

In July 2023, the Presidential Guard of Niger overthrew President Mohamed Bazoum in a coup that immediately provoked ECOWAS sanctions threats, suspension of French military presence, freezing of IMF programme disbursements, and diplomatic confrontation. General Abdourahamane Tchiani's junta identified France's uranium extraction terms — long-term contracts with ORANO (formerly AREVA) at prices widely characterised as below market — as a central grievance. Niger had supplied roughly 15–20% of France's uranium requirements. The coup leaders explicitly invoked the resource sovereignty argument.

The paper's framework predicts — in its Mechanism A formulation — that states with energy resources can translate those resources into monetary leverage by developing domestic processing capacity, establishing pricing power, and using energy exports as collateral for alternative monetary arrangements. The paper also acknowledges — in its Mechanism B formulation — that states attempting to escape the existing dollar/euro system face financial exclusion, sanctions, and coercive reintegration pressure.

Niger in 2023–2024 is the cleanest real-world test of this binary the paper discusses. A uranium-exporting state, with substantive nuclear fuel resources analogous (though not identical) to the paper's thorium reserves, attempted exactly the kind of resource sovereignty the paper treats as a precursor to monetary power. The immediate result: IMF programme suspension, loss of approximately $400 million in budgetary support from Western donors, ECOWAS economic blockade threats, and complete financial isolation from the Bretton Woods institutional architecture.

The paper's thorium narrative predicts India rising monetarily as thorium reserves are mobilised. The paper does not acknowledge that the institutional pathway from having thorium reserves (or uranium reserves, as Niger demonstrates) to exercising monetary leverage runs directly through existing power structures that will deploy financial coercion to prevent it.

Niger is not India. The difference is not primarily governance quality. The difference is alliance architecture, institutional embedding, and strategic weight. India (large domestic market, nuclear capability, strategic autonomy, non-aligned tradition, Quad membership) can survive coercive response; Niger cannot. **This is actually a productive theoretical distinction the paper should make explicitly:**

- **Mechanism A precondition**: sufficient strategic weight to absorb or deter the coercive response to resource sovereignty claims
- **Mechanism B activation**: insufficient strategic weight → bilateral corridor formation under exclusion

Niger's experience establishes the lower bound on what strategic weight is required for Mechanism A to operate. The paper should model this threshold rather than treating India's energy sovereignty as a costless structural outcome.

## 3. New Caledonia's Nickel: Critical Minerals and the Boundaries of the Framework

New Caledonia holds approximately 25% of global nickel reserves, concentrated in the laterite deposits of Grande Terre. The territory's three independence referenda (November 2018: 56.7% No; October 2020: 53.3% No; December 2021: 96.9% No, under boycott by the FLNKS independence movement) have produced an unresolved constitutional situation. The May 2024 unrest — triggered by French government proposals to expand the electorate for territorial elections — resulted in deaths, major economic damage to Nouméa, and suspension of the Grand Leaders' Summit process.

The energy transition — which the paper treats as the structural driver of TMPI's eventual dominance — is a battery-intensive transition. Battery storage at grid scale requires nickel (particularly class 1 nickel for NMC lithium-ion chemistries), cobalt (DRC), lithium (Australia, Chile, Argentina), and manganese. New Caledonia's nickel is not a nuclear fuel input in the way thorium is, but it is a critical mineral input to the energy transition infrastructure that the paper's entire forward scenario depends upon.

An independent New Caledonia — a live political scenario despite the 2021 referendum result, given the contested legitimacy of that vote — would need to establish its own monetary architecture. Currently using the CFP franc (Franc Pacifique, pegged to the euro at 119.33 CFP per euro, convertibility guaranteed by the French Treasury under Operations Account logic identical to the CFA franc), an independent New Caledonian state with the world's largest per-capita nickel endowment would potentially constitute a commodity-backed monetary claim of exactly the kind the paper discusses for thorium.

This illustrates a broader gap: the paper's sharp periodisation — "carbon era" (NEP-driven) versus "thorium era" (TMPI-driven) — misses a transitional critical minerals era that is happening now. New Caledonia, DRC, Zambia, the Philippines — states with battery-transition mineral endowments — occupy a monetary-strategic position the paper's two-era framework cannot accommodate. This limitation should be acknowledged explicitly.

## 4. The ECO and Regional Monetary Architecture in West Africa

The paper identifies "layered" monetary alternatives in the Indo-Pacific (CMIM, $240 billion) and in BRICS (Bridge, the proposed Kazan settlement system). It treats these as evidence of Mechanism B — states building alternative monetary infrastructure outside the dollar system. The paper does not discuss the ECO.

ECOWAS has pursued a West African single currency since the Abuja Treaty framework, with launch dates of 2020 and now 2027. The project has two components: the existing UEMOA CFA franc states (eight states already in a functioning monetary union) and the non-CFA ECOWAS states (Nigeria, Ghana, Sierra Leone, Liberia, Guinea). An ECO incorporating Nigeria's oil economy into a West African monetary union would have NEP characteristics fundamentally different from the current CFA franc zone. Nigeria alone has proven oil reserves of approximately 37 billion barrels. Ghana has offshore oil (Jubilee field since 2010) and substantial gold production. Guinea's hydropower and bauxite have been noted above.

The ECO's repeated failure to launch — despite decades of institutional preparation and a functioning regional community template — is itself analytically significant. It suggests that the governance prerequisites the paper bundles into GS are genuinely binding constraints, not parameters to be populated with WGI scores and assumed to be achievable. The paper's relatively optimistic reading of CMIM and BRICS Bridge as viable monetary alternatives should be tempered by the West African experience: even a region with functioning monetary unions, legal frameworks, and shared colonial monetary heritage cannot achieve broader integration when domestic political economy constraints and strategic rivalries (particularly Nigeria's) dominate.

## 5. Françafrique as a Governance Score Problem: Causality Running Backwards

The paper uses WGI (World Governance Indicators) as the institutional quality proxy in TMPI and implicitly in GS. CFA zone states systematically score poorly on WGI dimensions: Government Effectiveness, Rule of Law, Control of Corruption, Voice and Accountability. The paper treats low WGI scores as evidence of low institutional capacity that then constrains GS and MPI.

This causality is partly inverted for CFA zone states. The CFA franc's convertibility guarantee — preventing balance of payments crises through automatic French Treasury liquidity access — removes a major incentive for domestic fiscal discipline and monetary institution-building. The Operations Account system, by recycling foreign exchange reserves through Paris rather than domestic financial systems, suppresses the development of local capital markets. The fixed peg, by removing exchange rate as a policy instrument, forecloses an entire dimension of macroeconomic management that builds institutional capacity in central banks.

Pigeaud and Sylla (2018, *L'Arme Invisible de la Françafrique*) and Monga and Yifu Lin (2015) in the broader African development context have argued that the CFA franc's design actively prevents the monetary institution-building that WGI scores partially measure. If this literature is even partially correct, the paper's use of WGI as an exogenous input into GS and TMPI is methodologically flawed for states in institutionalised monetary dependency arrangements. The governance score is partly an **outcome** of the monetary architecture, not a prior condition.

The paper should either: (a) explicitly exclude CFA zone states from its main empirical tests on grounds that their monetary architecture is externally determined — but state and defend this exclusion, not simply effect it by omission; or (b) adopt an instrumental variables approach to WGI for states in institutionalised monetary dependency arrangements.

This matters for the paper's TMPI formula more broadly: if institutional quality is endogenous to monetary architecture for a significant class of states — including potentially India in the post-colonial context, Niger post-coup, and certainly all fourteen CFA zone states — then TMPI predictions are systematically biased. States that would have higher institutional quality under different monetary arrangements are predicted to have lower monetary power. The paper treats this as an exogenous constraint rather than a structural condition to be theorised.

## 6. France's Geopolitical Retreat from the Sahel: Russia's Mechanism B in Real Time

The paper discusses Russia's Mechanism B in theoretical terms — a state institutionally excluded from the Western monetary order building bilateral energy-monetary corridors. The paper's evidence is drawn primarily from the ruble-yuan and ruble-rupee energy corridors post-2022. The paper entirely misses what is arguably the most significant real-time test of Mechanism B in operation: Russia's systematic replacement of French security and monetary infrastructure in the Sahel between 2021 and 2024.

The sequence is documented. Mali expelled French forces in January 2022, replacing them with Wagner Group (subsequently rebranded Africa Corps under direct Russian Ministry of Defence command). Burkina Faso expelled French forces in January 2024. Niger expelled French forces following the July 2023 coup. Chad — historically France's most secure Sahel partner — requested French troop withdrawal in late 2024. In each case, Russian security presence has been accompanied by Russian economic penetration: gold extraction in Mali (Wassoul'or, Robex, and artisanal sites under Wagner protection), uranium discussions in Niger, general mineral prospecting.

The currency implications are developing: the Alliance des États du Sahel (Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger) has discussed monetary alternatives to the CFA franc, with some officials referencing a future AES currency and bilateral ruble settlement for Russian goods.

This is Mechanism B in precisely the form the paper describes: a state institutionally excluded from the Western monetary order (Russia) building bilateral energy-mineral corridors in states that have themselves exited or been excluded from Western monetary architecture. The paper predicts Russia's Mechanism B as a theoretical possibility. It is not theoretical. It is happening in Africa, on a timeline that postdates the paper's most recent cited evidence. The paper's Russia analysis should engage with this.

## 7. France's Nuclear Programme and the ORANO Uranium Corridor

France's civilian nuclear programme — at its 2005 peak supplying approximately 78% of domestic electricity — represents the highest sustained civilian nuclear share of any major economy. As of 2022–2023, following EDF's corrosion crisis (nuclear output fell to approximately 279 TWh, the lowest since 1988), France's nuclear share had fallen to roughly 63%, with announced programmes for fourteen new EPR2 reactors.

France's state nuclear programme depends on uranium supply chains running through Niger (SOMAÏR, the surviving Arlit mine post-COMINAK closure in 2021), Kazakhstan (Orano stakes), and Canada. Under the paper's MPI formula, France presents an analytically interesting case that is entirely absent.

France's NEP should be high: it is a large net energy producer relative to consumption due to nuclear capacity, despite having no domestic fossil fuels. France's GS should also be substantial: the ETS is central to European energy governance, and France's domestic nuclear governance (ASN, CEA, IAEA engagement) represents sophisticated institutional capacity. France's TMPI is weaker — no significant thorium reserves — but France's nuclear technology export capacity (EPR reactors in Finland, UK, China, India) means it has nuclear capability share in the paper's sense.

But the more important question from this review's perspective is the ORANO-Niger corridor. For fifty years, France has extracted uranium from Niger at prices set under long-term bilateral contracts, processed it at the Georges-Besse II enrichment facility (Tricastin, operated by Orano), used it to generate nuclear electricity under state utility EDF, and used that energy advantage to maintain monetary credibility within the euro area — while simultaneously guaranteeing the convertibility of Niger's CFA franc at terms that prevented Niger from developing independent monetary institutions or capturing the value-added from its own uranium endowment.

This is the paper's central mechanism — energy resources translating into monetary power — **operating in explicit colonial-extractivist form**. France captures Niger's uranium energy value. Niger's monetary architecture is structurally subordinated to France. The WGI scores that register Niger as institutionally incapable of monetary leverage are partly a product of this arrangement.

The paper's framework, if applied honestly to this case, would describe a predatory Mechanism A: France's MPI is enhanced by its parasitic relationship with Niger's energy endowment, while Niger's MPI is suppressed by the institutional architecture France maintains as a condition of that parasitism. The paper's decision to analyse energy-monetary power as if it operates through neutral institutional processes — governance scores, reserve shares, trade invoicing — without attending to the extractivist arrangements that determine which states capture energy value and which states supply it, represents a significant theoretical blind spot. Power is not just a variable in the MPI formula. Power determines who gets to have a positive MPI in the first place.

## 8. Technical and Formal Comments

**On the GS term.** The paper's GS = 2.79 is derived from EU ETS Phase I data. For CFA zone states, there is no carbon pricing mechanism; energy governance operates through bilateral concession agreements, production-sharing contracts, and investment treaties — instruments whose CV would reflect very different dynamics than ETS phase price volatility. GS = 2.79 is being applied as if it were a global constant. This requires justification or restriction of scope.

**On the Katzenstein filter operationalisation.** The paper applies the filter qualitatively to Japan and the UK. For CFA zone states, the filtering mechanism is quantified: the Operations Account deposit requirement, the fixed peg, the French Treasury board seats. The paper should use the CFA architecture as a model for operationalising the Katzenstein filter quantitatively.

**On panel regression and case selection.** The paper acknowledges its panel finding is US-driven. If the entity-specific model approach is adopted, the paper must contend with the CFA zone states as entity-specific cases running counter to Mechanism A. Selecting case studies (Japan, India, China, Russia) all outside institutionalised monetary dependency while ignoring the fourteen CFA states whose monetary arrangements most directly test the mechanism is selection bias with direct implications for the paper's claims.

## 10. Overall Verdict

**Score: 6.2/10. Recommendation: Major Revisions.**

The theoretical framework warrants publication. The empirical grounding, case selection, and engagement with the most relevant existing instances of the paper's proposed mechanisms require substantial revision.

**Required revisions:**
1. Engage with the CFA franc zone as a direct empirical test case for Mechanism A — the most institutionalised example of the Katzenstein filter in existence
2. Include Niger 2023 as a natural experiment: attempted energy resource sovereignty → financial coercion → Mechanism B activation. Explain why India escapes this trap and Niger does not — this theoretical distinction would substantially strengthen the paper
3. Address the WGI endogeneity problem for states in institutionalised monetary dependency arrangements
4. Engage with Russia's Mechanism B operating in the Sahel in real time — Françafrique's collapse as the enabling condition for Russian energy-monetary corridor formation in West/Central Africa
5. Acknowledge the ORANO-Niger uranium corridor as the colonial-extractivist form of the paper's central mechanism — France's MPI enhanced by Niger's MPI suppression

The paper's silence on the CFA franc zone is not a peripheral omission. The CFA zone is the paper's core mechanism operating without analytical camouflage. Engaging with it would not only fill a gap — it would make the paper more honest about the politics of energy-monetary power.
