# Peer Review — India Specialist
## "The Carbon-Thorium Standard: Energy-Backed Currencies and the Architecture of Monetary Failure in a Multipolar World"

**Reviewer specialisation:** India/South Asia; strategic autonomy doctrine, energy policy, monetary sovereignty, South Asian political economy
**Journal:** Review of International Studies
**Recommendation:** Major Revisions
**Score:** 5.5/10

---

## Summary Assessment

The manuscript makes a genuinely ambitious theoretical contribution — the Monetary Power Index formalises a mechanism that mainstream reserve currency theory has treated as background noise. The three-register architecture is analytically elegant and the Japan natural experiment is among the paper's strongest passages. However, the India case study, which the authors clearly intend as the paper's prospective centrepiece, is built on a series of compounding optimistic assumptions that individually appear defensible but together produce a prediction well beyond what the evidence can sustain.

---

## 1. Realism Lens: Strategic Autonomy and the Non-Alignment Framing

**The framing is frozen in time.** The paper treats strategic autonomy as a stable, operationalised foreign policy output rather than what it actually is: a contested rhetorical norm whose substantive content has shifted dramatically since 2017. India's Quad membership (formalised at leaders' level in 2021), its participation in iCET (the Initiative on Critical and Emerging Technology) with Washington, co-production arrangements with GE for TEJAS Mk2 engines, and the June 2023 Modi state visit outcomes all represent a qualitatively different posture than the non-alignment of Nehruvian or Manmohan Singh eras.

The revisionist literature — Rajagopalan (2020), Hall (2019), Miller (2021) — documents strategic autonomy's transformation from a substantive doctrine into a diplomatic brand that legitimises case-by-case alignment with the West. The paper cites Katzenstein but does not engage this revisionist scholarship.

**The Quad contradiction is unaddressed.** If India is simultaneously (a) building the strategic case for INR internationalisation on the basis of energy independence and (b) deepening defence and technology integration with the United States at a pace not seen since 1947, the paper owes the reader an account of how these vectors coexist.

**The Civil Nuclear Agreement constraint.** Stage 3 thorium deployment cannot be politically insulated from US licensing and technology transfer constraints, since reprocessing technology essential for Stage 2→3 remains on the NSG dual-use control list. The paper's forward-register scenario implicitly requires India to develop AHWR reactors using domestically developed reprocessing, creating a US-India technology relationship structurally incompatible with a sharp departure from the dollar system.

---

## 2. Liberalism Lens: Capital Account Liberalisation and Domestic Political Economy

**The RBI is not just conservative; it is institutionally committed to capital controls.** The paper nods to "RBI conservatism" but treats it as a friction rather than a structural feature. The empirical record is instructive: India's capital account has been managed under FEMA 1999 for over 25 years; every post-1991 liberalisation episode has been either delayed, reversed, or implemented partially. The 2013 taper tantrum (20% rupee depreciation in four months despite capital controls) and the 2022 Russia shock (which produced fresh bilateral payment construction *outside* convertibility frameworks) both strengthened the hand of control advocates.

**Liberalisation in India is supply-constrained, not demand-constrained.** The constraint is a durable coalition: export-oriented manufacturing, agricultural sector (benefits from managed exchange rates), state banking system (captive domestic liability pools), and the RBI's institutional interest in exchange rate management instruments. None of these coalitions becomes weaker as nuclear energy output increases. No mechanism is provided.

**The invoicing norm barrier is more fundamental than acknowledged.** As of 2024, approximately 86% of India's merchandise trade is still invoiced in USD or EUR. For INR to reach 3% of BIS FX turnover, it would need to become a genuine third-currency vehicle for transactions not involving India at all. The BIS 2022 Triennial showed CNY at 7% after 15 years of maximum Chinese effort. If China at 7% after maximum effort, the India 3–5% target requires a much more specific causal account than the paper provides.

---

## 3. Energy-Security Nexus: Nuclear Programme Timeline

**The PFBR delay is evidence about institutional capacity, not an implementation detail.** PFBR was originally scheduled for criticality in 2010. It achieved first criticality in 2024 — a 14-year delay. The AHWR remains in detailed design phase at BARC as of 2025. Stage 3 at commercial scale is a post-2040 scenario under optimistic assumptions.

**The 20% nuclear share by 2035 target requires extraordinary scrutiny.** India's current installed nuclear capacity is approximately 7.5 GW. A 20% nuclear share in 2035 — assuming 5–6% annual electricity generation growth — would require roughly 65–80 GW of nuclear capacity, implying construction of 55–70 GW in ten years. India has never built more than approximately 1 GW of nuclear capacity in a single year. NPCIL's own projections give 22 GW by 2031 under the most optimistic scenario. The 20% figure appears to derive from a DAE planning document predating multiple revision cycles and should not be used as a modelling input without flagging its aspirational status.

**The coal lock-in is underweighted.** India added approximately 24 GW of coal capacity between 2022 and 2024 and has 55 GW under construction as of early 2026. The IEA's 2025 India Energy Outlook projects coal remaining at 55–60% of India's power mix through 2035 under stated policies. The MPI's NEP formula will be dominated by coal dynamics far more than nuclear for the foreseeable future. The paper overfits the theoretical model to India's most attractive feature (thorium reserves) while underweighting fossil fuel dynamics.

---

## 4. Monetary Power: INR FX Turnover Prediction

The BIS 2022 Triennial reported INR at 1.6%. The 2019 figure was 1.7% — *flat or slightly declining* despite India's GDP growing from $2.7tn to $3.7tn. The paper predicts acceleration from a baseline that shows stagnation. The structural obstacles — oil market pricing conventions (USD), the SWIFT-exclusion risk pushing India toward bilateral systems as insurance not internationalisation vehicles, absent liquid INR-denominated sovereign debt markets accessible to non-residents — receive insufficient attention.

The paper does not engage with the Chinn-Ito financial openness literature on sequencing prerequisites. The standard finding (Prasad 2016; Eichengreen, Mehl, Chiţu 2017) is that FX turnover share responds to: (1) financial market depth, (2) capital account openness, (3) anchor currency network effects. India scores poorly on (1), is constrained on (2) by design, and faces overwhelming (3) from the dollar.

---

## 5. Geopolitical Positioning

**The Russia-India energy relationship creates trapped rupees, not monetary architecture.** Since February 2022, India purchases approximately 35–40% of its crude from Russia at a discount. Indian refiners are now adapted to high-sulphur Russian crude, creating switching costs. The accumulated rupees in Vostro accounts are structurally trapped — Russia cannot spend them. These arrangements are designed to manage a sanctions-driven anomaly, not to build a new monetary architecture. The paper's treatment of bilateral non-dollar arrangements as stages toward INR internationalisation is misleading.

**India-China rivalry creates structural drag on the CA improvement pathway.** The border confrontation since 2020 (Galwan, ongoing LAC standoffs) is pushing India toward defence imports from the US and France, denominated in dollars and euros, worsening India's current account at the ministry of defence level. The paper treats India's geopolitical positioning as strategically autonomous; the security spending dynamic is a structural counterforce to the CA improvement pathway.

---

## 6. Gaps and Oversights

**Missing literature:**
- Patel and Bhattacharya (2023) on INR internationalisation constraints
- Mohan and Ray (2019) on RBI institutional conservatism and capital account management
- Saran and Pant (2021) on India's strategic autonomy doctrine under Modi
- DAE AHWR pre-feasibility reports (BARC 2020, 2023) on Stage 3 timelines
- Gopinath (2015) on dominant currency paradigm — invoice currency choice is sticky and driven by market structure, not monetary credibility signals

**Data gap:** KAOPEN = -1.254 may not capture post-2022 regulatory changes (FAR scheme for government securities, relaxed ECB norms). Should be updated.

**Mechanism gap:** The credibility literature (Svensson 1999; Carlin and Soskice 2015) locates credibility in institutional frameworks, not productive capacity. India has had robust GDP growth without visible INR internationalisation improvement. The paper needs a more specific mechanism.

---

## 7. Strengths to Preserve

- The Fukushima natural experiment is genuinely original and the identification strategy is sound
- Relegating pooled OLS to appendix reflects appropriate methodological humility
- The TMPI formula is transparent and independently reconstructable
- The core theoretical claim — energy sovereignty as constitutive variable — is important and underargued in the existing literature

---

## Required Revisions

1. **Acknowledge or defend** the Quad-era transformation of India's strategic autonomy; the non-alignment framing as currently written is an assertion, not an argument
2. **Provide a political economy mechanism** for how energy credibility overcomes the RBI coalition — not just a sequencing diagram
3. **Update nuclear timeline inputs** to current DAE/NPCIL projections; flag the 20% target as aspirational
4. **Integrate coal dynamics** into the NEP trajectory analysis
5. **Explain the stagnation paradox**: INR was flat 2019–2022 despite massive GDP growth; the model must account for why acceleration is now predicted
6. **Engage Gopinath (2015)** on dominant currency paradigm stickiness
