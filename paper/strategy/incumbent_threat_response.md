# Strategic Vulnerability Assessment and Response Plan
## "The Carbon-Thorium Standard" — Incumbent Industry Threat Analysis

**Document type:** Internal strategy memo
**Audience:** Author / research team
**Date:** March 2026
**Classification:** Working document — not for circulation

---

## Executive Summary

This document identifies the specific vectors through which fossil fuel incumbents — majors, petrostate sovereign wealth funds, and allied policy organisations — are likely to attack the Carbon-Thorium Standard framework, and provides a concrete response plan for each vector. The goal is not to make the paper "safe" for industry but to make it technically unassailable before those attacks materialise. A paper that reaches policymakers with its empirical errors intact is a paper that can be discredited with a single well-funded technical rebuttal. A paper that reaches policymakers with those errors corrected forces incumbents to attack the theory, which is where the paper is strong.

**The core strategic principle:** Fix the empirical errors first. Every technical weakness the domain panel identified is simultaneously an academic problem and an industry attack surface. They are the same list.

---

## Part I: The Threat Landscape

### 1.1 Who Will Attack and Why

**Integrated oil majors (Shell, BP, ExxonMobil, TotalEnergies, Chevron)** — threatened by the NEP formula as a policy instrument that quantifies energy import dependence as monetary credibility drag. Their government affairs and policy teams will engage if this paper gains traction in central bank or IMF circles.

**Gulf petrostate sovereign wealth funds (ADIA, PIF, QIA, KIA)** — threatened by the forward scenario that marginalises Gulf hydrocarbon states in the thorium-era monetary order. The paper gives reformers within these institutions a monetary sovereignty argument for accelerating away from the petrodollar architecture. Incumbent factions within Gulf governments will resist this framing.

**Carbon market opponents (industrial lobbies, CBAM-resistant exporters)** — threatened by the GS operator's implication that a well-governed carbon market is a monetary power instrument. They have strong incentives to amplify the paper's ETS technical errors to discredit carbon pricing generally.

**Dollar-system beneficiaries (US financial sector, Treasury-aligned think tanks)** — threatened by the Katzenstein filter applied in a way that names the dollar's security-monetary entanglement as a constraint on ally autonomy. Will push the causality inversion narrative.

**Russian and Chinese state-adjacent research organisations** — paradoxically, may selectively cite Mechanism B and the corridor formation argument to legitimise bilateral energy settlement outside SWIFT. This is not a threat but a co-optation risk: the paper could be weaponised for a geopolitical agenda it does not endorse.

### 1.2 The Attack Playbook

Incumbents do not typically attack academic papers directly. They run a three-stage playbook:

**Stage 1 — Technical discrediting.** Fund or commission a narrow technical rebuttal targeting empirical errors. Goal: get the paper labelled as sloppy before it reaches non-specialist policymakers. Does not require engaging the theory at all. A 1,500-word blog post by a well-credentialed carbon market specialist pointing to CV=0.815 data provenance issues is sufficient to stall adoption in policy circles.

**Stage 2 — Narrative substitution.** Amplify the causality inversion counter-narrative through op-eds, policy briefs, and think tank papers. Goal: establish "monetary credibility enables energy investment" as the dominant causal story, making the paper look like it has the arrow backwards. This is the slow burn — works over 12-24 months.

**Stage 3 — Institutional quarantine.** Ensure the paper does not get cited in IMF Article IV consultations, BIS working papers, or central bank reserve strategy documents. Achieved through direct engagement with senior staff at these institutions, not through public attack. The paper needs enough credibility and technical robustness to survive this stage independently.

## Part III: Response Plan

### Tier 1 — Pre-Publication Fixes (Must complete before submission)

These are the vulnerabilities that, if left unaddressed, allow a single well-funded rebuttal to discredit the entire framework. They are also among the easiest to fix.

---

#### R1.1 — Obtain Primary EUA Price Data

**Threat addressed:** CV=0.815 data provenance attack

**Action:**
Access primary Phase I EUA price data from one of:
- ICE (Intercontinental Exchange) historical data portal — ECX EUA daily settlement prices 2005-2007
- World Bank's State and Trends of Carbon Pricing reports (2007-2008 editions cover Phase I)
- ICAP Allowance Price Explorer (free public tool, covers Phase I)

Recalculate CV=0.815 from primary data. If result differs from 0.815, report the corrected value. If it confirms 0.815, add a data footnote citing the primary source directly.

**Deliverable:** Revised GS calculation with primary data citation or explicit uncertainty bounds. Add to paper as a data appendix.

**Timeline:** 1-2 days

---

#### R1.2 — Separate the Two Phase I Events

**Threat addressed:** "Author doesn't know carbon markets" attack

**Action:**
Add a single paragraph to §5 distinguishing:
- **Event 1 — May 2006 information shock:** Verified 2005 emissions data released 15 May 2006, revealing 44 Mt surplus. EUA spot prices fell from ~€30 to approximately €10-12/tonne within days. Mechanism: asymmetric information resolution.
- **Event 2 — End-2007 Phase I expiry:** Prices fell to near-zero because Phase I allowances carried **no banking rights** into Phase II. A structural design feature, not a governance failure. Mechanism: intertemporal arbitrage eliminated by design.

Add a sentence noting that CV=0.815 is dominated by Event 2, and that the banking restriction contribution to CV has been separated from the governance-attributable residual as follows: [add decomposition calculation].

This two-paragraph fix eliminates the most credible single-point attack on the backward register.

**Deliverable:** Revised §5 with clear event distinction and CV decomposition.

**Timeline:** 1 day

---

#### R1.3 — Fix the GS Temporal Mismatch

**Threat addressed:** GS = 2.79 vs. 5.4 inconsistency

**Action:**
Report GS under two specifications:
- GS_contemporaneous = CV(EUA Phase I, 2005-2007) / CV(Brent crude, 2005-2007) ≈ 0.815 / 0.15 ≈ 5.4
- GS_longrun = CV(EUA Phase I, 2005-2007) / CV(Brent crude, 2005-2024) ≈ 0.815 / 0.292 ≈ 2.79

The contemporaneous window is the methodologically correct comparison. The long-run window is presented as a robustness check showing the result is not sensitive to a low oil volatility baseline. Note: GS_contemporaneous = 5.4 actually *strengthens* the paper's argument (higher governance amplification than reported), so reporting it honestly is an asset, not a concession.

**Deliverable:** Table in §5 showing both GS specifications with explanation.

**Timeline:** Half a day

---

#### R1.4 — Correct the India 20% Nuclear Figure

**Threat addressed:** "Physically impossible" target attack

**Action:**
Replace "20% nuclear by 2035" with the correct figure from India's Department of Atomic Energy planning documents: approximately 22.5 GW installed nuclear capacity by 2031-32 (from current ~7.5 GW). Express this as a share of projected total installed capacity (~1,250 GW by 2031-32) = approximately 1.8% of installed capacity but a higher share of firm despatchable capacity.

Add a footnote: "The paper uses India's official DAE capacity targets, not aspirational long-run share statements. The distinction matters: DAE targets are construction-pipeline-based; share targets are contingent on the broader electricity mix trajectory."

Also correct Stage 2 blanket material: PFBR uses a **depleted uranium** blanket to breed plutonium. Thorium breeding begins in Stage 3. This is a one-sentence factual correction that prevents the "author doesn't understand India's nuclear programme" attack.

**Deliverable:** Corrected §7 India analysis with sourced DAE figures and Stage 2 correction.

**Timeline:** Half a day

---

#### R1.5 — Decouple AUKUS from the Thorium/Civilian Nuclear Argument

**Threat addressed:** "Author conflates HEU naval propulsion with civilian thorium cycle" attack

**Action:**
Add a box or footnote in the Australia/AUKUS section making explicit:
- AUKUS Pillar One = HEU naval propulsion. No thorium. No civilian nuclear. No fuel cycle development.
- Australia's TMPI score reflects its thorium/uranium geological endowment and current near-zero nuclear capability
- AUKUS does not change Australia's TMPI because AUKUS does not create civilian nuclear capability
- If Australia ever develops civilian nuclear power (politically contested, ARPANS Act constraints), that would affect TMPI independently of AUKUS

If the paper currently implies AUKUS boosts Australia's nuclear-monetary position, remove or heavily caveat this. It is technically indefensible and will be exploited.

**Deliverable:** Revised Australia section with clean separation of AUKUS (military) and civilian nuclear (TMPI-relevant).

**Timeline:** Half a day

### Tier 2 — Fortification Before Journal Submission

These fixes address the narrative and causal attacks that incumbents will run in Stage 2 of their playbook.

---

#### R2.1 — Address the Causality Inversion with an IV Strategy

**Threat addressed:** "Monetary credibility enables energy investment, not vice versa"

This is the most intellectually serious attack and cannot be dismissed. The paper must engage it directly.

**Action:**
Add a section (approximately 800 words) in §4 or §6 titled "Addressing Reverse Causality." The argument structure:

1. Acknowledge the reverse causality concern directly. It is a legitimate identification challenge.

2. Propose an instrumental variables strategy: geological energy endowments are exogenous to monetary architecture — a country's thorium or uranium deposits were determined by geological processes predating monetary systems. Use lagged geological endowment as an instrument for current energy position.

3. Present the Granger causality results from the panel as preliminary evidence: if NEP Granger-causes reserve share but reserve share does not Granger-cause NEP in the same lag structure, this is consistent with the paper's causal direction.

4. Cite the Fukushima natural experiment as the cleanest identification: the reactor shutdown was caused by a tsunami, not by yen movements. The subsequent depreciation is therefore caused by the energy position change, not vice versa. This is the paper's strongest causal identification, and the author should lean into it explicitly as a response to the reverse causality concern.

5. Note the limits: the IV strategy is partial, the Granger evidence is suggestive, and the Fukushima identification applies only to the Japan case. The paper should be explicit that it offers *evidence consistent with the causal claim* rather than definitive identification.

**Deliverable:** Added reverse causality section with IV strategy sketch and Fukushima identification foregrounded as causal evidence.

**Timeline:** 2-3 days

---

#### R2.2 — Strengthen the RGGI Comparison with Controls

**Threat addressed:** "RGGI has a price floor, making the comparison invalid"

**Action:**
Add a paragraph acknowledging the three confounds: (a) RGGI price floor mechanism, (b) GFC timing (RGGI Phase I = post-2008 recession), (c) covered sector composition. Present a "best-case RGGI comparison" that strips out the price floor's mechanical contribution to lower CV — even under conservative assumptions, the EU Phase I vs. RGGI CV ratio remains substantially above 1. The conclusion (EU Phase I shows more political construction than RGGI) survives; the mechanism claim (it's all governance, not design) requires qualification.

**Deliverable:** Revised RGGI comparison paragraph with explicit controls discussion.

**Timeline:** 1 day

---

#### R2.3 — Add the Proliferation Caveat Explicitly

**Threat addressed:** "This paper incentivises U-233 production" — a regulatory/ethical attack that is also a political attack surface for nuclear opponents who would otherwise be natural allies

**Action:**
Add a paragraph in §9 (forward register / India section) addressing U-233 directly:

"A thorium fuel cycle produces U-233 as its fissile product. U-233 is classified by the IAEA as a direct-use material — it is weapons-usable fissile material. The paper's argument that thorium fuel cycle sovereignty creates monetary leverage therefore implies that the relevant capacity is also a latent dual-use capacity. Three clarifications are warranted. First, the monetary leverage argument does not require weapons production — it requires *demonstrated fuel cycle capacity*, which is a different and narrower claim. Second, India's three-stage programme operates under IAEA safeguards arrangements (INFCIRC/754) that apply to its civilian facilities. Third, the paper is not a normative argument for thorium proliferation; it is a positive analysis of how energy governance capacity translates into monetary credibility in the existing state system. The proliferation implications of that translation are a constraint on the pathway, not an endorsement of weapons development."

This paragraph neutralises the attack without conceding the analytical claim.

**Deliverable:** Added U-233 paragraph in §9.

**Timeline:** Half a day

---

#### R2.4 — Add the Malacca Dilemma to China's NEP

**Threat addressed:** Weakens the China TMPI = 4.2 prediction which will otherwise be attacked as ignoring basic strategic facts

**Action:**
Add a Strategic Energy Vulnerability Index (SEVI) as a supplementary component to NEP for states with significant chokepoint exposure. For China:

SEVI_China = f(Malacca dependency ratio, route diversification index, pipeline alternative share)

Note that China's NEP ≈ 0 (coal self-sufficiency offsetting oil imports) *understates* China's strategic vulnerability because the import component is disproportionately chokepoint-exposed. A simple adjustment: weight import shares by strategic vulnerability (inverse of route diversification). This does not require recalculating the entire MPI; it adds a qualitative caveat plus a bounded sensitivity analysis showing how TMPI_China changes under different Malacca vulnerability assumptions.

**Deliverable:** Added SEVI footnote or box for China analysis, plus sensitivity table.

**Timeline:** 1-2 days

### Tier 3 — Strategic Positioning (Pre-Publication Outreach)

These are not paper revisions but actions that shape how the paper is received in policy circles before incumbents can run their Stage 3 (institutional quarantine) playbook.

---

#### R3.1 — Target Early Adoption by Non-Western Central Banks

**Rationale:** If the paper's first citations are in IMF working papers or Western central bank publications, incumbents will engage directly at the institutional level. If the first citations are in RBI (Reserve Bank of India), SARB, or BIS emerging markets working papers, the incumbents' institutional access is more limited.

**Action:**
- Submit a shortened policy brief version (3,000 words) to the BIS Working Papers series or an IMF Working Paper simultaneously with or before journal submission
- Present at the RBI's annual monetary policy research conference
- Submit to the ASEAN+3 Macroeconomic Research Office (AMRO) working paper series, which has direct CMIM institutional linkage
- Engage the New Development Bank's research division — NDB has direct interest in the governance construction argument

---

#### R3.2 — Pre-emptive Technical Transparency

**Rationale:** The data provenance attack (CV=0.815 from secondary sources) is most dangerous when it feels like something the author tried to hide. Pre-empt it by being maximally transparent.

**Action:**
Create a public data repository (GitHub or Harvard Dataverse) containing:
- All EUA price data used, with source citations
- Replication code for CV calculations, GS computation, ZA test
- Sensitivity analyses for alternative GS temporal windows
- Raw TMPI calculations for all case study states

A paper with a public replication repository is orders of magnitude harder to discredit with a data provenance attack. "The data is wrong" requires actually running the replication code and showing different results — a much higher bar than "we can't verify this."

---

#### R3.3 — Engage One Major Carbon Market Specialist as Co-Author or Acknowledgement

**Rationale:** The carbon science reviewer's 4/10 score is the most credible single attack surface. If a named, credentialed carbon market specialist has reviewed and endorsed the revised GS methodology, the "doesn't know carbon markets" attack loses its bite.

**Action:**
Identify a carbon market specialist willing to review §5 in detail and either co-author the ETS section or provide a named acknowledgement. Target: someone with Ellerman and Buchner's institutional credibility (MIT Energy Initiative, Resources for the Future, ICIS, BloombergNEF). Even a documented exchange in the acknowledgements ("We are grateful to [name] for detailed comments on the EU ETS empirical methodology") signals that the carbon science has been independently checked.

## Part IV: The Counter-Narrative

The paper needs an affirmative counter-narrative ready for when incumbents run the causality inversion argument. The counter-narrative is:

> "The Carbon-Thorium Standard does not argue that energy wealth automatically creates monetary power. It argues that energy governance capacity — demonstrated through the institutional management of energy systems — is a *precondition* for monetary credibility in an era when energy security has become the primary determinant of national economic stability. The gold standard was not backed by gold production alone; it was backed by the institutional credibility of central banks that managed gold reserves. The Carbon-Thorium Standard argues that states which develop the institutional capacity to govern their energy transitions — sovereign fuel cycles, transparent carbon pricing, resilient grid infrastructure — are building the monetary credibility infrastructure of the next era, whether or not they currently hold reserve currency status."

This counter-narrative:
- Shifts from "energy quantity → money" to "energy governance → money" (harder to attack causally)
- Connects to existing institutional monetary theory (Goodhart, Ingham) rather than requiring a new causal claim
- Makes the Fukushima natural experiment its centrepiece: Japan's *governance* of its nuclear programme (shutdown and restart) caused yen movements, not the physical presence or absence of uranium
